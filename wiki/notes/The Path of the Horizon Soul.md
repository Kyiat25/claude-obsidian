# The Path of the Horizon-Soul

*"We were not shaped by gods. We were shaped by the climb."*

Known to outsiders as the Climb Doctrine, and to Goliaths simply as the Path. Not a doctrine of worship but a creed of endless becoming, in which existence is treated as a mountain and every breath a test of resolve. Goliaths believe the soul is a horizon — always ahead, never reached — and that divinity is achieved by climbing, not granted from above.

## Origin myth — The Climb That Broke the Sky

Before names, there was only stone and wind. **Valkaan, the Wind-Shouldered**, was not born but simply stood up from the stone and began walking toward an unseen summit — the first of the Horizon-Souled. He was followed by **Orenya** (who laughed at every fall), **Koraak** (the Silent Watcher, whose gaze judged kings and cowards alike), and **Uldra** (who stayed behind to teach memory, becoming the first cairn). When their climb reached where even the wind could not breathe, the sky opened and stars poured out — these became the Horizon-Soul, and from its echoes the Goliaths were born: not children of divine will, but "grandchildren of the climb."

## The Five Stones of Ascent (core tenets)

1. The Soul Is a Horizon — it moves with you; you cannot reach it, only walk toward it.
2. The Body Is a Cairn — marked by acts of endurance, truth, and strength.
3. The Clan Is the Rope — you do not climb alone; honor is shared, shame is carried.
4. The Wind Is the Test — every challenge is sacred; without struggle, you are hollow.
5. The Summit Is Not the End — even in death, the climb continues; only cowards stop.

## The Horizon-Souled (revered, not worshipped)

Valkaan (First Strider — striving, courage, endurance), Koraak (Sentinel of the Upper Sky — judgment, witness, legacy), Orenya (She-Who-Fell-and-Climbed — resilience, joy in pain), Uldra (the First Cairn — memory, oath, community), and the Horizon-Soul itself (the self becoming divine). These figures are invoked in deed, not prayed to.

## Spiritual roles

**Stone-Speaker** (oral archivist, memorizes the fallen's names and deeds), **Wind-Walker** (interpreter of natural signs, never prophesies — only listens), **Climb-Bound** (wandering Goliaths on oath-bound exile), **Echo-Bearer** (inherits an unfinished task from the dead). Rare/forbidden roles mark spiritual failure states: **Cairn-Binder** (binds their own name to a fallen soul's shame), **Pathbreaker** (the gravest crime — one who misleads others from the Path, stripped of name and hunted), **Hollow-Clad** (trauma-induced loss of memory/purpose, pitied not shunned), **Summit-Denier** (philosophical heretic who claims the climb is a lie — punished only by being universally ignored, "the closest thing to spiritual execution").

## Why Goliaths do not worship

The gods are treated as fellow climbers who never reached the summit either — respected individually (Aelys for stillness, Gaeldros for judgment though his written order is distrusted, Ignara for hearth-protection, Thirian quietly honored by some Wind-Walkers, Vatharun's bones said to form the first peaks) but never knelt to. Worship is framed as abdication of one's own climb: "to kneel is to surrender your stride." No god made the first climb — Valkaan climbed before the Pattern was even named.

## Death and the afterlife

Death is a milestone, not a destination. Those who die striving become part of the **Circle of the Summit**, their names carved in cairns and voices becoming guiding wind. Those who died having abandoned their Path descend into the **Echoing Valleys** — not torment, but silence and slow forgetting, redeemable only through an Echo-Bearer or Cairn-Binder. Funerary rites are entirely communal: the Cairn-Words (each mourner carves one honest word, flattering or not), the Last Echo (a Stone-Speaker recites the deceased's name, trial, and last oath), and the Watch of Still Wind (a silent night vigil when "the echoes begin").

## Relationship to other faiths

Respects [[The Faith of the Bound Pattern]]'s complexity but rejects its impulse to name and order all things — "we leave threads for the weavers; we carve stone." Treats Eldritch forces with uneasy vigilance rather than fear, refusing to name them; those who take Eldritch shortcuts are branded Pathbreakers.
