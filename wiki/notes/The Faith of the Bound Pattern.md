# The Faith of the Bound Pattern (Creed of the Fivefold Loom)

*"Threads fray. Threads return. The Pattern holds."*

The dominant, city-spanning religion of Dór-o Estel, practiced across the Loom-Cities. Teaches that all five elemental pantheons (Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Spirit) are equal, interwoven threads of one Weave, none dominant — "the gods are threads; we are the needle." Its symbol, the Spiral of Unity, depicts five interwoven threads circling a hollow, unfillable core representing the unknowable Primal Glyphs and the ever-present risk of Eldritch corruption ("the Fray").

## Creation account

Before the gods, the Fivefold Loom produced five primal forces — the Hammer (Fire), the Wheel (Water), the Loom (Spirit), the Breath (Air), and the Stone (Earth) — from which pantheons of gods later arose through belief, conflict, and myth. Their discord, not their unity, is what created balance. The Faith teaches that elemental glyphs are divine script made visible; any deeper glyphic layer beneath the Pattern (compare [[The Weave Unbroken]]) is treated as heresy or madness.

## The Five Sanctums

Each city's temple structure divides into sanctums for Fire (ambition, forging destiny), Water (cycles, healing, truth), Earth (law, memory, death), Air (change, freedom, fate-defiance), and Spirit (magic, fate, dreams, soul) — no single thread is meant to be pulled alone, "or the Pattern may tear."

## Threadbearers

The Faith's central mechanic: one **Threadbearer** per elemental Thread, spiritually bound to their city's [[World Anchors and Thread System|World Anchor]], chosen not by mortals or gods but by the Weave itself in an event called a **Beaming** (visible elemental manifestation, prophetic dreams, leyline surges). A Cycle lasts unpredictably — days to up to 137 years — ending with a Threadbearer's death or the Weave's judgment. An Anchor can reattune to the same Thread consecutively, but three times in a row has happened only once: the [[Fall of Ashkarra]]. If a World Anchor is endangered, all five Threadbearers unite in a sacred rite called **the Last Call**; failing to answer brands a Threadbearer a **Frayed Hand**, a title of deep shame. Threadbearer death triggers a local unraveling of the Weave and begins the search for a successor — a dangerous period when Drow are known to hunt surviving Threadbearers.

## Heresy and the Eldritch

Worship of "Frayed Threads" or "Null Threads" — the Eldritch gods of Unbeing — is outlawed but not uncommon, associated with signs like children forgotten mid-conversation or temples with no doors that everyone nonetheless remembers praying in. Temples keep hidden black mirrors to watch for the Eldritch's return.

## History (condensed)

Loomless Time (no Faith, only unnamed spiral symbols in cave art) → First Age (divine wars, no unified faith, only scattered heresies) → Second Age (Threadseekers emerge from burned scrolls near proto-Veltharyn, doctrine forms in secret) → Third Age (formalizes into five Threads and consecrated World Anchors; the [[Fall of Ashkarra]] — three consecutive Spirit-aligned Cycles — nearly destroys the Faith via the First Hollow Breach and a shattered World Anchor, until [[Aeravae]] is founded and a new Anchor forged, marking "the Rebinding") → Fourth Age (present: the dominant religion, defining law and calendar in the Loom-Cities, though cracks remain and Eldritch Moons rise more often).

Aeravae's founding required cooperation with [[The Woven Reverence]] (elves) and Dwarven Rooted Silence — producing a lasting internal doctrinal split: **Traditionalists** (World Anchors are the "proper" system; World Trees are primitive), **Reformists** (Trees prove the Weave predates divine authority; Faith must humble itself), and the current **Syncretist Compromise** (both systems are sacred — Trees "earth-blessed," Anchors "spirit-refined").

## Racial/cultural reception (summary)

Humans are the Faith's central architects and most devoted adherents. Elves treat it as a poetic echo of older truths, politely superior. Dwarves respect the metaphor without worshipping. Gnomes treat it as a fascinating hypothesis rather than dogma. Half-Elves find in it ideal symbolic belonging ("we walk the seams"). Drow reject it totally in favor of [[The Ashen Web]]. Dragonborn tolerate it as a metaphor for their own rigid caste order. Halflings enjoy its poetry without reverence. Orcs reject it outright as another attempt to impose fate on a people who define themselves by freedom from it.
