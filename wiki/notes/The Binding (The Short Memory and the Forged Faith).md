# The Binding — The Short Memory and the Forged Faith

*"We do not remember what the elves remember. We were not there when the world was young. But the gods spoke to us, and we listened."*

A `status: excluded`-tagged DM-only source deepening [[The Lie of Godhood]] specifically for Humans and Dragonborn — treat as evocative, thematically-consistent color, not fully confirmed canon. Its thesis: humans and dragonborn genuinely, sincerely believe the gods created them, and that sincerity is exactly what made them useful instruments of a much older deception.

## The core asymmetry

Elves encountered the Weave first, and gods claimed authority over it only afterward. Humans and Dragonborn encountered gods *first*, and never knew anything else — to them, the idea that gods didn't create them is as incomprehensible as the sun not rising.

## The Dragonborn Compact

Emerging during the Loomless Time before developing any culture of their own, the Dragonborn were found by Soltharion before they found themselves — he offered dominion in exchange for absolute loyalty, and they accepted through genuine belief, becoming the first race to truly worship a god and the template for what every pantheon wanted from its worshippers. Their resulting empire persecuted elves not from malice but from sincere belief they were "saving" a people trapped in delusion — burning sacred groves and texts, forcing conversions, all while believing themselves liberators. The empire's fall (~1500–1200 years ago) is remembered by modern Dragonborn as an unresolved crisis of faith: conservative loyalists double down on devotion, while a persecuted reformist minority quietly asks whether Soltharion could be worthy of worship without having been their creator.

## Humanity's short memory

Arriving last (~8,000 years ago) into a world already fully claimed by organized pantheons, human 80–100 year lifespans meant no cultural continuity beyond four generations and no ability to verify ancient divine claims — making them, per this source, "perfect believers" and "ideal enforcers." Each elemental pantheon cultivated a different human warrior/trade/agricultural/scholarly/mystic culture around its own promises, and within 2,000 years humanity was fully integrated into divine worship with no memory of anything else.

## The Faith of the Bound Pattern as humanity's synthesis

Unlike elves, humans organized divine worship into systematic written theology — [[The Faith of the Bound Pattern]] — precisely because short memory required doctrine, competing divine claims needed hierarchy, and social order required clear rules. This source frames the Faith's genius (comprehensible, practical, universal, enforceable) as identical to its poison (systematic, self-reinforcing, totalizing, taught to children before they can question). Elves rejected the Faith because they remembered a world before gods organized and could sense the Pattern wasn't divine will (compare [[The Woven Reverence]]) — a threat serious enough that the Faith, backed by Fire-pantheon and Dragonborn military power, launched centuries of forced conversion, grove-burning, and text-destruction, framed internally as liberation rather than conquest.

## Present day (Year 487)

Modern Dragonborn remain predominantly faithful, holding military and clerical leadership roles within Faith of the Bound Pattern societies, while a growing minority privately questions why divine favor didn't prevent their empire's fall. The source frames both Humans and Dragonborn as simultaneously victims (genuinely deceived, never given the chance to know otherwise) and perpetrators (the deception's actual instruments against elves and other pre-divine peoples) — a tragedy rather than a villainy, per its own framing.
