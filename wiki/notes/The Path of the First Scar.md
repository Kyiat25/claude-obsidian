# The Path of the First Scar

*"They gave us laws. We broke them. They offered peace. We spat it back. They called us beasts. So we made the name sacred."* — Tarn Griefscar, First Warchanter of the Scarbound

Orcish faith holds that **all gods are enemies first** — anything earned through worship must first be taken through struggle. Orcs claim no pantheon of their own; they claim to have won the right to name the gods in blood. See [[Orc (Lineage)]] and [[Half-Orc (Lineage)]] for the mechanical lineage pages this faith underlies.

## The Scarbound Names (gods by Orcish reckoning)

Every major god is known to Orcs not by its true name but by the scar it left resisting them: **Grak-Thul, the First Scar** (= Soltharion, who tried to burn Orcs into obedience — his fire is now the pain that teaches), **Ugra-Mara, the Ash-Mother** (= Ishara, who tried to erase Orc settlements — revered as the one who destroys but also tests), **Korrun, the Iron-Tongue** (= Vaedoris/Echoed Mother, whose memory-preservation was ignored until Orcs forced their legacy into the Weave by will), and **Varkhul, the Wound That Laughs** (= Zephir, whose mockery Orcs turned into laughter as a weapon).

## Sacred Scar-Law

"Every god has tried to kill us. So we made gods of the wounds they left behind." Prayer is a duel, ritual is defiance, myth is the record of what did not kill them. A god who cannot be fought is not worthy of worship.

## Honor (zakkur) as literal currency

Honor is counted by deeds, scars, titles, and victories, stored in names and lineage, and traded only once — when a child inherits a parent's legacy. Five honor rules: earn it alone but it belongs to the bloodline; wasting it on petty pride steals from descendants; facing an easy test when a harder one was available is dishonor; lying about honor is worse than death (erasure); defeating a greater foe mints new honor for the whole clan. Clan genealogy is recorded in **Scar Scrolls**.

## The Seven Tests of Worth

The Test of Flesh (endurance without retreat), Wit (outsmart an unbeaten riddle/foe), Kin (defend family alone), Flame (face Grak-Thul's trial), Ash (rebuild from ruin), Voice (recite an ancestor's name before the gods without faltering), and the Scar-Earned (survive a body-marking challenge and prove it makes you greater).

## Names, exile, and return

Names are earned "by Blood" (birthright, confirmed at the Test of Voice), "by Blade" (a Challenge Name earned through witnessed trial), or "by Trial of Fire and Bone" (adoption into a clan). Dishonor triggers **Name-Burning** — total erasure from the Ledger-Stone, leaving the offender as "the Unnamed." Return from exile requires one of three grueling paths: the Path of the Red Ledger (public reckoning and judgment by combat), the Path of the Bonekeeper (a solo sacrificial deed plus ancestral omen), or the Path of the Black Scar (a voluntary, witnessed death in service, restoring the name posthumously).

## Death: the Afterchallenge

Death is not an end but a transition into an eternal realm of trials, where the worthy are tested again by ancestors and gods. Funerals span three days: the Scarring of Memory (self-cutting in shared remembrance), the Gear of Passage (burial or cremation with weapon/token), and the Burning Oath or Burial Stone (fire for those beginning their Afterchallenge immediately, burial for those who must first heal). Honored dead may become **Ancestral Watchers**, or rarely reach the **Scar-Stone Dream**, where they duel eternally and forge new tests for their descendants — sustained only by being remembered and spoken of, never prayed to.

## The Scar-Wardens

Judges, executioners, memory-keepers, and death-guides belonging to no single clan, answering only to the Ledger's truth. Three branches: the Ash-Branded (executioners and duel overseers), the Ledger-Scribes (record-keepers and Name-Carvers), and the Bone-Walkers (semi-druidic spiritual guides to the Afterchallenge). A secret inner council, the **Circle of Brands**, resolves disputes too great for any single tribe and is feared even by powerful clan elders.

## Relationship to the Faith of the Bound Pattern

Orcs neither follow nor scorn [[The Faith of the Bound Pattern]] — they respect its concern for balance but would rather "burn the pattern and reweave it in blood." They do not obey Threadbearers, but honor their deaths if earned. Orcs view Humans as kneeling too easily, Elves as listening too long without acting, Dwarves as understanding pain through labor, and Halflings as bafflingly wise-or-mad for laughing at death.
