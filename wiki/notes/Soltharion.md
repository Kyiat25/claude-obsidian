# Soltharion, The Sun Tyrant

*"Fire must not burn uncontrolled. Fire must be law. I am fire's law."*

**Domains**: light, order, war, authority. **Symbol**: a golden sun crowned in flame. **Worshiped by**: kings, lawkeepers, warlords who seek absolute rule. **Enemies**: [[Xethar]], [[Zerah]], Pyrris, and quietly even [[Ignis]] himself.

Believes Fire must rule as the supreme force of all things, waging a slow-burning war against [[Ignis]] (whom he considers weak for allowing wildfire and heresy to spread) to claim Fire's throne for himself. His **Doctrine of the Unyielding Flame** — "Fire Cannot Burn Wild" — holds that all fire must serve a purpose (the Law of the Forge), belongs only to those strong enough to wield it (the Law of the Throne), and must burn away all resistance (the Law of Conquest). Enforced by the Flame-Keepers: the Ember Judges (who brand heretics), the Sunforged Paladins (who burn cities resisting Fire's Law), and record-keepers who track every weapon forged in his name. The Dragonborn were once his chosen warriors, forged as living heralds of his empire, but sought to claim Fire's power as his equals rather than his servants and were cast down for it — their divine fire diminished, their bodies hardened into scaled flesh. He still hates their arrogance and waits for the day Ignis falters to take the throne.

**Myth — The Ember Throne Challenged**: Soltharion once seized the Ember Throne through tyranny and ritual, declaring Fire "a crown, not a crucible." He challenged [[Xethar]] (who burned a continent in protest), out-ritualed [[Zerah]], snuffed Ignara's hearth, and shattered [[Vaeldros]]'s chains — even Pyrris briefly bowed. But the throne rejected unity; wildfires surged, and the Weave cracked near what is now [[Ashkarra]]'s ruins, drawing [[The Hollow Thread|the Hollow Vault]]'s attention. Fearing the Pattern's unraveling, the fire gods united for the first time ever and cast Soltharion into the heart of the sun, where he burns but does not rule. He swore vengeance; the throne remembers.
