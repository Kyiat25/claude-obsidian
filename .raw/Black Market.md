# Whispers in the Shadows

The black market is a clandestine network of illicit trade, operating outside the bounds of law and legitimate commerce. It thrives in the shadows, catering to those who seek forbidden goods, discreet services, or a means to circumvent the authorities. Accessing the black market requires connections, discretion, and a willingness to take risks.

## **Finding the Black Market**

- **Contacts and Informants:** The most reliable way to access the black market is through existing contacts or informants. Characters can cultivate these connections through their background, class features (e.g., the Rogue's Criminal Contact feature), or by making inquiries in shady establishments like taverns, gambling dens, or fighting pits.
- **Investigation:** Characters can attempt to uncover black market operations through investigation. This requires a successful Intelligence ([[Streetwise]]) check (DC 15 + the settlement's size modifier: +0 for Hamlet/Thorp, +2 for Village, +4 for Town, +6 for City, +8 for Metropolis). Failure means they cannot search again for 1d4 weeks + their wanted level.
- **Reputation:** A character with a significant criminal reputation ([[Wanted Score]] 3 or higher) might be approached by black market agents seeking to offer their services. However, this also increases the risk of attracting unwanted attention from law enforcement or rivals.

## **Services and Goods**

The black market offers a wide range of illicit goods and services, often at inflated prices:

- **Illegal Weapons and Armor:** Poisoned daggers, enchanted blades, smuggled firearms, and illicit armour.
- **Contraband:** Stolen goods, rare artifacts, forbidden texts, and other items deemed illegal by the authorities.
- **Information Brokerage:** Black market informants can provide valuable information for a price, including rumours, secrets, and the whereabouts of wanted individuals.
- **Forgery and Counterfeiting:** Skilled forgers can create fake documents, identification papers, or even counterfeit currency.
- **Magical Services:** Some black market mages offer illicit magical services, such as curses, removals of curses, or other spells normally forbidden by ethical or legal codes.
- **Assassination and Other Criminal Services:** Characters can hire assassins, thugs, or other criminals to carry out their dirty work.
## **Risks**

- **[[Law Enforcement]]:** Black market transactions are illegal and carry the risk of attracting the attention of law enforcement. The risk increases with the character's Wanted Score and the notoriety of the black market operation.
- **Rivals and Betrayal:** The black market is a dangerous place, filled with rivals and double-crossers. Characters must be wary of being cheated, robbed, or betrayed by those they deal with.
- **Inferior Goods:** Not everything on the black market is of high quality. Characters might be sold counterfeit goods, faulty weapons, or inaccurate information.
- **Hidden Costs:** Black market deals often come with hidden costs, such as favours owed, unexpected fees, or dangerous obligations.

# **Black Market Prices**

Prices on the black market are typically inflated by 50-100% or more compared to legal markets. The exact price depends on the rarity of the item or service, the character's [[Wanted Score]] (higher scores often lead to higher prices), and their negotiation skills ([[Streetwise]]).
### Examples

A rogue seeks to purchase a poisoned dagger from a black market contact in a city. The contact offers the dagger for 50 gold pieces, double the price of a normal dagger. The rogue attempts to haggle, using their Charisma (Persuasion) skill to reduce the price. 

The DM might set a DC 15 Persuasion check to reduce the price by 10 gp, a DC 20 to reduce it by 20 gp, and so on. However, pushing too hard might offend the contact, leading to a higher price or even a refusal to sell. The rogue must also be mindful of the risk of being caught by the city guard while conducting the transaction.

## **Black Market Reputation**

Separate from a character’s Wanted Score, **Black Market Reputation** represents their standing within the shadow economy. It reflects trustworthiness, ruthlessness, reliability, or notoriety _within criminal circles_. Reputation can be positive or negative, and affects prices, access, and loyalty among black market contacts.

### Earning Reputation

- Completing deals without betrayal or conflict.
- Honouring bargains or paying extra in desperate situations.
- Performing services for influential underworld figures.
- Showing consistent discretion and professionalism.
### Losing reputation

- Failing to pay, scamming contacts, or botching jobs.
- Drawing unwanted law enforcement attention.
- Betraying or exposing black market agents.
### Effects:

|Reputation Level|Effects|
|---|---|
|-2 or lower|Banned from most networks, marked for death or arrest.|
|-1|Viewed as a liability. Prices rise 50–100%. May be set up or ambushed.|
|0 (Neutral)|Treated as an unknown. Standard inflated prices apply.|
|+1|Known name. Slight discounts (10–15%), better service.|
|+2|Trusted dealer. Access to rare items, discreet services, loyalty bonus.|
|+3 or higher|Respected agent. Priority treatment, major favors, or network protection.|

> _Note: Black Market Reputation is **regional**, unless a character gains mythic status or serves a global syndicate. Separate reputations may exist in rival cities or factions._

# **Black Markets and the Oppressed**

While often associated with crime and corruption, not all black market dealings are malevolent. In regions ruled by tyrants or burdened by unjust laws, black markets may serve as vital lifelines for the desperate and oppressed. 

Secret networks might traffic in stolen food redirected to starving villages, rare medicines banned by aristocratic edicts, or magical healing outlawed by paranoid regimes. 

Relic retrieval crews risk death to return ancestral artifacts to their rightful homes. 

Underground circles sometimes form around these “grey markets,” guided by codes of honor, resistance, or liberation. While still dangerous and illegal, these markets blur the line between outlaw and hero—offering characters a chance to make moral choices in morally ambiguous spaces.
