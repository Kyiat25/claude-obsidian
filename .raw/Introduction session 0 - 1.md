# Session 1–2 Combat: **The Fog on the Water**

## Encounter Name (GM-facing)

**“The River Does Not Care Who You Are”**

Players won’t remember the name — they’ll remember the _feeling_.

---

## Narrative Setup (10–15 minutes before combat)

- Late sunset, the river widens
    
- Fog rolls in unnaturally fast
    
- Sound dulls (oars, voices, water)
    
- Lantern light diffuses, visibility drops to ~20 ft
    
- The barge slows to avoid hidden shallows
    

Key NPC beats _before_ initiative:

- Captain orders reduced speed (irritated, nervous)
    
- Tax collector insists they keep moving (authority vs reality)
    
- One NPC goes quiet — they _know_ this stretch of river
    
- Prophet murmurs: _“This is where people disappear.”_
    

No checks yet. Let the tension breathe.

---

## The Ambush (What Actually Attacks)

This is **not** a random monster fight.

### Attackers: _River-Reavers_

Choose **one** origin depending on tone you want:

- **Smugglers-for-hire** (political violence)
    
- **Corvessan irregulars** (border escalation)
    
- **Thread-touched scavengers** (early weirdness)
    
- **Crannog raiders** (local survival economy)
    

They are:

- Lightly armed
    
- Desperate
    
- Organised enough to be dangerous
    
- Trying to **board**, not slaughter
    

### Composition (Balanced for Level 1)

For **4 PCs**:

- 4 Reavers (melee + grapples)
    
- 1 Reaver Spotter (bow or sling, stays in fog)
    

For **3 PCs**:

- Remove the spotter
    

For **5–6 PCs**:

- Add 1 extra Reaver _or_ a trained hound on a rope
    

⚠️ No boss.  
⚠️ No spellcasters.  
This fight is about _positioning, reactions, and panic_.

---

## Terrain (This Is the Secret Sauce)

The barge is the encounter.

### Zones

- **Deck** (slippery, half cover from crates)
    
- **Cargo Hold Hatch** (can be blocked or defended)
    
- **River Edge** (fall = cold water, STR save to climb)
    
- **Fog** (light obscurement, heavy beyond 20 ft)
    

### Environmental Rules (Simple, L1-safe)

- First time a creature is shoved/grappled → CON save or Prone
    
- Ranged attacks beyond 20 ft have disadvantage
    
- Falling into water costs next turn unless helped
    

This lets:

- Fighters feel tanky
    
- Rogues disengage meaningfully
    
- Casters control space
    
- Everyone matter _without lethality spikes_
    

---

## How the Fight Starts (Very Important)

Do **not** start with initiative.

Start with:

- A _thud_ against the hull
    
- A grappling hook biting wood
    
- A scream from the stern
    
- Then: **“Roll initiative.”**
    

Players feel ambushed, not staged.

---

## What This Combat Is Actually Teaching

### At a Systems Level

- Movement matters
    
- Positioning matters
    
- Helping actions matter
    
- The world is dangerous but _not unfair_
    

### At a Narrative Level

- Violence here is **economic**
    
- No one is laughing
    
- No one wants glory
    
- This is survival, not heroics
    

This aligns perfectly with the tone of:

- Corvessa preparing for war
    
- Kel Varyan enforcing control
    
- A world under strain
    

---

## NPC Behaviour During the Fight (Crucial)

NPCs do **not** fight well.

- Captain tries to keep control, not kill
    
- Tax collector hides (remembers this later)
    
- Merchant screams about the cargo
    
- Prophet _helps someone up_ instead of fighting
    

Players notice who acts — and who doesn’t.

That matters later.

---

## Ending the Fight (Do NOT Overextend)

The reavers:

- Retreat once 2 fall
    
- Cut ropes and vanish into fog
    
- Take **one crate or person if they can**
    

Victory feels:

- Incomplete
    
- Messy
    
- Real
    

---

## Aftermath (Where the Arcs Start Whispering)

Post-combat consequences:

- One NPC is injured (healing choice)
    
- One crate is gone (illegal or sensitive)
    
- The tax collector _now knows_ the captain is lying
    
- The captain _now owes the party_
    
- Rumours spread upriver
    

And then:

> **Imperial recall orders arrive the next morning.**

The timing feels ominous — and correct.

---

✔ No lore dump required  
✔ Violence has consequences  
✔ Sets tone without revealing stakes

Most importantly:

> This fight teaches them  
> **“You are small — but your choices are not.”**

# Escalation Phase: **When the River Answers**

## Design Principle (GM Rule)

> **The predators do not start the fight.  
> They are drawn by it.**

This preserves realism and tension.

---

## When to Trigger the Predators

Use **one** of these triggers (GM choice, invisible to players):

- Two reavers drop to 0 HP
    
- A body hits the water
    
- Blood spreads across the surface
    
- A loud spell or explosive effect is used
    
- The reavers disengage and flee
    

The moment the _fight changes_, the river responds.

---

## The Predators (What They Are)

Do **not** present them as a “new enemy wave”.

They are:

- Ambush hunters
    
- Opportunists
    
- Territorial
    
- Half-seen
    

### Recommended Creature Type

**River Lurkers** (use one flavour):

- Lamprey-things with too many teeth
    
- Crocodilian shapes with pale, lidless eyes
    
- Long-bodied eels with grasping maws
    
- Shadowed silhouettes that _pull_, not strike
    

They are not magical.  
They are not evil.  
They are **hungry**.

---

## How Many?

For **any party size** at level 1:

- **2–3 predators total**
    
- Only **1 is fully visible at a time**
    
- Others are hinted by ripples, shapes, screams
    

This keeps threat high but damage controlled.

---

## Target Priority (Critical)

The predators **do not** focus the PCs first.

Their priorities:

1. **Fleeing reavers**
    
2. **Anyone already in the water**
    
3. **Dangling ropes, legs, bodies**
    
4. **Only then** the barge itself
    

This does two things:

- Extends combat naturally
    
- Makes players feel relief _and then dread_
    

> “Oh thank gods they’re attacking the raiders—  
> …wait, it’s coming this way.”

---

## Mechanical Role (Level-1 Safe)

### Predator Behaviour

- High grapple / pull chance
    
- Low raw damage
    
- Disengages after a successful grab
    
- Drags prey underwater or under the hull
    

### What They Teach

- Reactions matter
    
- Helping actions matter
    
- Environment matters
    
- Killing isn’t always the solution
    

### What They Avoid

- No multiattack
    
- No save-or-die
    
- No burst damage
    
- No spell-like abilities
    

---

> He doesn’t scream — not at first.

The predators don’t “attack the barge”.

They:

- Slam into the hull
    
- Drag at ropes
    
- Pull someone off balance
    
- Snap at lantern light reflecting on water
    

Simple effects:

- One section of deck becomes difficult terrain
    
- A rope snaps (someone nearly falls)
    
- A crate shifts dangerously
    

Now the party must:

- Fight
    
- Stabilise
    
- Rescue
    
- Choose priorities
    

That’s **excellent early-campaign pressure**.

Do **not** let this turn into a boss fight.

## RIVER LURKER — STAT BLOCK (LEVEL 1 SAFE)

**Medium Beast (Aquatic), Unaligned**

**AC:** 12  
**HP:** 18 (4d8)  
**Speed:** 20 ft, swim 40 ft

**STR** 14 (+2)  
**DEX** 12 (+1)  
**CON** 13 (+1)  
**INT** 2 (-4)  
**WIS** 10 (+0)  
**CHA** 5 (-3)

---

### TRAITS

**Amphibious Hunter**  
The lurker does not suffer disadvantage attacking creatures in water or at the waterline.

**Blood-Sense**  
If a creature within 20 ft is injured or bleeding, the lurker has advantage on its first grapple attempt against that creature this turn.

---

### ACTIONS

**Bite**  
_Melee Weapon Attack:_ +4 to hit, reach 5 ft  
_Hit:_ 4 (1d6+2) piercing damage  
⚠️ Damage is intentionally low

---

**Drag Under (Recharge 5–6)**  
If the lurker hits a creature **already grappled**, the target must succeed on a **DC 12 STR or DEX save** or be pulled 10 ft toward water or under the surface and knocked **Prone**.

> This is positioning pressure, not a kill move.

---

### BONUS ACTIONS

**Submerge**  
If in water, the lurker can submerge, gaining half cover until it attacks again.

---

### REACTIONS

**Slip the Line**  
When hit by a melee attack, the lurker can move 5 ft without provoking opportunity attacks.

---

### MORALE RULE (IMPORTANT)

If reduced to **8 HP or fewer**, the lurker:

- Immediately disengages
    
- Grabs the nearest unconscious or fleeing creature
    
- Retreats underwater
    

They **do not fight to the death**.

---

## FEARS NOTES (OPTIONAL)

- Treat **Drag Under** as a **Forced Movement + Off-Balance** condition
    
- Lurkers never gain Momentum
    
- Players gain Momentum for rescues, not kills

# 🏪 THE TOWN ECONOMY (SESSION 0 CANON)

**Location context:** a poor farming–trade hub in **Corvessa**, politically irrelevant, economically necessary.

This is a place that:

- feeds others
    
- supplies others
    
- moves goods onward
    
- keeps _nothing_ worth stealing long-term
    

---

## CORE RULE (PIN THIS)

> **If something is powerful, impressive, magical, or rare — it does not stay here.**

It is:

- sold onward
    
- taxed away
    
- requisitioned
    
- hidden
    
- or never brought here in the first place
    

---

## WHAT SHOPS EXIST (AND WHY)

### 🧺 General Traders

They sell:

- rope
    
- sacks
    
- oil
    
- lamp wicks
    
- travel rations
    
- cheap tools
    
- replacement parts
    

Everything is:

- serviceable
    
- worn
    
- practical
    
- unremarkable
    

Nothing is:

- masterwork
    
- pristine
    
- aspirational
    

A fighter does not envy these weapons.  
A wizard does not envy these supplies.

They’re just _enough_.

---

### ⚔️ Weapons & Armour (Very Limited)

If present at all:

- farm spears
    
- wood-hafted axes
    
- hunting bows
    
- patched leather armour
    
- helmets with dents
    

No:

- plate
    
- fine steel
    
- exotic materials
    
- military-grade gear
    

If a PC asks _why_:

> “That sort of thing doesn’t stay here long.”

Which is the truth.

---

### 🧪 Alchemists / Healers

One apothecary at most.

They have:

- poultices
    
- bandages
    
- painkillers
    
- herbal teas
    
- basic antitoxins (weak, unreliable)
    

They do **not** have:

- strong healing potions
    
- combat drugs
    
- experimental concoctions
    

Their value is **care**, not power.

---

### 📜 Scribes & Faith Services

Through the Faith of the Pattern:

- birth and marriage records
    
- basic education
    
- contract witnessing
    
- simple blessings
    

No:

- relics
    
- holy weapons
    
- sanctioned miracles
    
- inquisitorial authority
    

This reinforces:

> Faith here is maintenance, not dominance.

---

### 🍞 Taverns & Inns

Food is:

- filling
    
- bland
    
- seasonal
    

Drink is:

- weak beer
    
- cheap spirits
    
- locally made
    

Comfort exists — but it’s not indulgent.

---

## WHAT DOES **NOT** EXIST HERE

This is as important as what does.

❌ No magic shops  
❌ No enchanters  
❌ No rare tomes  
❌ No elite trainers  
❌ No exotic mounts  
❌ No black market worth exploiting  
❌ No hidden “starter artifacts”

Anyone offering those things would:

- attract attention
    
- be arrested
    
- vanish
    
- or leave quickly
    

---

## PLAYER FEEL (THIS IS THE GOAL)

Players should think:

- “This is fine.”
    
- “This is normal.”
    
- “This place isn’t for us long-term.”
    

Not:

- “We need to strip this place bare.”
    
- “Why don’t they have X?”
    
- “This feels artificially limited.”
    

Everything makes sense **in-world**.

---

## WHY THIS IS GOOD DESIGN

### For the World

- Keeps Corvessa coherent
    
- Reinforces trade flow
    
- Makes the empire feel _real_, not distant
    
- Explains why the river matters
    

### For the Campaign

- Prevents early power creep
    
- Makes later cities feel _huge_
    
- Makes upgrades meaningful
    
- Keeps focus on **people, not gear**
    

---

## GM LINE TO USE (AND REUSE)

If a player asks:

> “Do they have anything better?”

Answer:

> “If they did, it wouldn’t still be here.”

That single sentence does a lot of work.

---

## FINAL CANON STATEMENT

> **This town does not produce heroes.  
> It produces food, labour, and survivors.**

Heroes pass through.
