# REEDFORD — WORLD CONNECTIONS SUPPLEMENT ## 

*The Thornward Cost, The Wardbreakers' Debt, The Threadbearer's Face, and the Red Fog* > *"The north asks for grain and sons. The war asks for sons and grief. The river asks for nothing and takes everything anyway."* > — Overheard at the Mudlark, attributed to a stevedore, Year 483 > *"We don't need another crusade. We need three hundred more men who can tell the difference between a Thread anomaly and a marsh gas flare."* > — Renn Ashford, field log, Year 486 --- *This supplement adds four layers of world-connection to Reedford, each addressing a gap in how the starting location ties into the broader campaign setting. All content is GM-only except where noted. It is designed to integrate directly with the Reedford Definitive Guide, the Divine Presence Supplement, and the Leyline Supplement.* *The four sections are:* 1. *The Thornward Cost — what the Fifth Failure did to the Lower Ivora Flats* 2. *The Wardbreakers Expanded — a border veteran running the guild, and a standing northern call to arms* 3. *Mira Sunken-Lance — a human face on the Threadbearer crisis* 4. *The Crimson Veil's Reach — vampire pirate influence on river insurance and estuary trade* --- --- # SECTION I: THE THORNWARD COST ## *What the Fifth Failure Did to Reedford* --- ## The Geography of Supply Reedford sits in the Lower Ivora Flats — the breadbasket region that fed Corvessa's Sixth Crusade. When nine thousand lancers and a thousand clerics marched northeast toward Talanthar's edge in Year 482 of the Fourth Age, the food in their saddlebags, the grain in their supply carts, and the river-barge tonnage that moved it all passed through towns like Reedford. The knights came from the Upper Ivora Hills, the glory from the Middle Crossing Lands, and the logistics from the Lower Flats. The glory did not return. The logistics did. But the logistics came back empty, and the men who drove the carts and crewed the supply barges didn't always come back at all — because when the Sixth Crusade collapsed, Talanthar's halfling joy-tribes didn't stop at the knights. Supply columns were prey too, and lighter to catch. **What this means for Reedford, fifteen years later:** The town doesn't talk about this. Not because it's secret, but because there is nothing to say that hasn't been said, nothing to feel that hasn't been worn smooth by repetition. Twelve names on Tessra's plaque at the Scale and Anchor are stevedores, barge pilots, and supply handlers, not knights. The plaque says *"who gave their service."* It does not say *"who were not supposed to be in a war zone."* --- ## The Economic Scar The Sixth Crusade created a specific economic wound in Reedford that is still visible in Year 487. **The Commissary Debt.** House Valdrek's grandfather — Lord Edran Valdrek, dead eleven years — supplied the crusade on royal credit. He expected reimbursement from the campaign's spoils. There were no spoils. The Crown paid back approximately forty percent of the commissary debt over eight years before the payments quietly stopped. Lord Cassin Valdrek inherited an estate that was, on paper, solvent. In practice, he inherited a family that had been quietly losing ground to the Lower Flats merchant class for a decade, and whose warehouse monopoly is partly a compensation for what the crusade took. He does not discuss this. Anyone with access to the county ledger records in Cindraleth — Ferren Yolt, notably, has examined them — can work it out. **The Levy Gap.** The Lower Ivora Flats contributed levies to the Sixth Crusade proportional to their population. For a town of Reedford's size, that was approximately forty men — footsoldiers and servants, not lancers. Eleven returned. Of those eleven, four are still in Reedford. The Stevedores' Association's informal injury fund was originally established in Year 483, one year after the disaster, when the seven open injury claims listed in the GM tracker's current state were actually nineteen — a combination of crusade survivors and work-related injuries that the fund was never properly capitalised to cover. It has been underfunded ever since. Daven Thatch knows this history. He does not speak of it publicly because the injury fund's continued underfunding is leverage: Valdrek can't fix it without admitting how badly the commissary debt broke the family's finances, and Daven can't fully expose it without destroying the fund's credibility as a negotiating tool. **The Missing Pilot Season.** In Year 482, the Upper Ivora became a military corridor. River traffic for commercial purposes was restricted between Reedford and Cindraleth for approximately four months during active campaign season. This is when Tem Arvish retired — not because his knee finally failed him, but because the Pilots' Register lost seven licensed pilots to the campaign (three dead, four conscripted into military river-work and never formally returned to the civilian register), and the chaos of that season broke something in him that had held for thirty years. He doesn't talk about it. The register shows a gap in licensing records for Year 482 that nobody has ever formally explained. --- ## Living Texture: How the Thornward Cost Appears in Play The following are available to observant players through NPC interaction and environmental detail. None require exposition. **The Plaque's geography.** Tessra's plaque at the Scale and Anchor lists twelve names. A player who examines it closely and asks about the names will find they're not all from the same families — two are common Corvessan names, four are Ivora Flats working names, and one is halfling. The halfling name is Pip Redbottom, a barge-cook who followed the supply column north because the army pay was better than river work. He was twenty-two. This is available through a DC 12 History check or a direct conversation with Tessra on a day when she's willing to talk, which is not most days. **Orven Staak's yard.** The repair yard has, in a dry storage corner, three flat-bottomed skiffs that have never been returned to their owners — owners who died in or around Year 482. Orven holds them legally as uncollected repairs with outstanding bills. He has not sold them. He has not scrapped them. They are maintained, barely, and when asked about them, he says: *"Outstanding accounts."* This is technically true. **The Mudlark's back room.** The Mudlark tavern has a back-room arrangement that pre-dates the Sixth Crusade but expanded during and after it: a mutual-aid tradition among Lower Flats boatmen where levy families were informally supported by river-worker contributions. It is not an organisation. It has no name. It is a clay pot behind the bar that certain people put coin into on certain days, and that Hevra Plum distributes from at her discretion. Hevra did not establish this. She inherited it from the previous landlord, who established it during Year 482 when the levy families started coming in asking if anyone had seen their sons. The pot currently holds about six silver. It has been as high as forty. It has been as low as two. **The "crusade count."** Among older Reedford residents, there is an informal reckoning — used in private conversation, never publicly — of what each family gave to the Sixth Crusade and what they got back. A merchant who lost two cousins and received forty percent reimbursement on commissary credit speaks differently about the Crown's decisions than one who had no family in the campaign and profited from the supply contracts. This divide is not visible until you know to look for it. Players who spend time with Daven Thatch, Hevra Plum, and Orven Staak across separate conversations will notice that three people who seem broadly similar in their economic class have very different responses to the phrase *"what does Corvessa owe you."* --- ## The Seventh Crusade Whispers Year 487. The current year. Fifteen years since Thornward, and the new generation of knights — those who grew up hearing the martyrology rather than the mud and the screaming — are beginning to ride patrol and attend Faith assemblies and talk, quietly, about honour. Reedford hears this as noise from upriver. It arrives in the form of: **A recent increase in military requisition inquiries.** Valdrek's counting house received two communications from Cindraleth in the last three months asking about grain storage capacity and barge charter availability for "strategic contingencies." Valdrek has not responded to either. He is waiting to see which direction the wind blows before committing his infrastructure to another campaign. **Brother-Warden Sethos is aware.** He received a pastoral circular from Cindraleth in Month 7 of Year 487 — before the Threadbearer assassinations — asking for congregational census numbers "for planning purposes." Sethos filed an accurate return and has not thought about it since. Players who find this circular in his study will recognise what a census of able-bodied parishioners "for planning purposes" actually means. **Aldren Valdrek wants it.** The lord's son has never been to war. He has been to three Faith assemblies in Cindraleth in the past year where the Seventh Crusade was discussed, not as rumour, but as prophecy — as the crusade that will succeed because it will be undertaken with true faith, not political calculation. He came back from each with something brighter in his eyes that his father does not like and has not found words to address. **What players can do with this:** The seventh crusade represents a slow-moving crisis that will hit Reedford in the form of requisitions, levy notices, and barge commandeering if it proceeds. The Thread crisis may delay or accelerate it. A party that understands Reedford's position as a logistics node can intervene at the supply-chain level — or simply be aware that the town's economic equilibrium is sitting on a foundation that powerful people upriver are about to test. --- --- # SECTION II: THE WARDBREAKERS EXPANDED ## *Vardis Ashmarch, the Northern Charter, and the Standing Call* --- ## The Wardbreakers of Reedford — Revised Context The Wardbreakers charter in Reedford is officially a Corvessan-registered monster-hunting guild holding a Wardbreaker Certificate issued by the Cindraleth Faith Guard. In practice, it is one of the few institutions in town that has successfully recruited outside Corvessan cultural norms, because monster work does not care about purity of doctrine. It cares about whether you're still alive when the contract closes. The guild was established in Reedford fourteen years ago, one year after the Thornward disaster, by a woman who had no interest in the Faith's interpretation of why the crusade failed and considerable interest in the fact that the region's northern approach was now functionally without the experienced knights who had previously kept certain things from wandering down toward the river settlements. That woman is **Renn Ashford**. She is still the operational commander. She did not found it alone. --- ## Vardis Ashmarch — The Northern Name **Vardis Ashmarch**, age 61, orc, is the second name on the Wardbreakers' founding charter and the reason the charter was granted without the usual three-year probationary period. He presented the Cindraleth Faith Guard with a letter of professional reference from Khan Grazak Firehand of the Ashcrown Khanate — a letter that the reviewing inquisitor spent forty minutes reading and then approved without further question, because refusing a professional reference from the man who had held the Corpse Road for twenty-two years would have required explaining to his superiors why. Vardis spent thirty-one years in the Ashcrown Khanate's northern watch before a vampire ambush at the Grey Sentinel outpost in Year 479 took three fingers from his right hand and replaced his relationship with cold weather with something he describes as *"informative disagreement."* He came south because the hand still works well enough for the work, and because the Khanate's service culture — honourable, demanding, and absolutely not designed for a one-handed orc in his late forties who'd started having opinions about tactics that younger commanders didn't want to hear — was a better thing to leave than to watch calcify around him. He found Renn Ashford through Joss, who found him through a pilot from the upper river who'd transported him south, and the three of them spent an evening at the Mudlark during which Renn explained what she was trying to build and Vardis explained what he knew about the things she'd be hunting, and by morning they had a partnership that has functioned without significant friction for fourteen years because they have almost entirely different opinions about everything except the work. **What Vardis brings to Reedford:** *Knowledge of northern creatures.* Vardis knows more about undead taxonomy, vampire hunting, and the specific behavioural patterns of creatures that bleed south from the Greymarch than anyone else within three days' travel. He maintains correspondence with former Khanate colleagues — three letters a year, arriving irregularly — and his field notes on creature behaviour, written in a combination of Common and Orcish shorthand, are the most practically useful documents in the Wardbreakers' archive. Ashford has been trying to get him to write a formal version for seven years. He says he'll get to it. He will not. *The Khanate connection.* The Ashcrown Khanate maintains informal field contacts throughout the river towns of northern Corvessa — not agents, not a spy network, but former soldiers who came south and know people, and people who know them, and a chain of communication that moves slower than courier but considerably faster than rumour. Vardis is one node in this network. He does not advertise this. It means that when something unusual happens on the northern approach — unusual undead behaviour, unusual migration patterns, unusual Greymarch activity — he hears about it within two to four weeks, filtered through three or four intermediaries. He has heard nothing alarming in the past three months. He is more worried by the silence than he would be by a report. *Veteran's authority.* Vardis's presence is the reason the Faith Guard at Cindraleth doesn't look too carefully at some of the Wardbreakers' operational techniques. An orc with a Khanate reference and thirty years of certified kills carries a specific kind of professional credibility that overrides a great deal of Corvessan doctrinal suspicion. Cors knows this and resents it with the particular resentment of a man who cannot act on what he knows. **Vardis as a character.** He spends most of his time in the Wardbreakers' charter house, reviewing Ashford's anomaly notebook with the methodical attention of someone who has spent thirty years learning to distinguish "something's wrong" from "something's coming." He has not yet told Ashford what he suspects about the anomalies' northern character — not because he wants to withhold it, but because he is not certain enough to say it and doesn't want to start a panic on speculation. Players who earn his trust will find that his suspicion is this: the creatures appearing near the confluence point don't behave like creatures from the Hollowdeep or the forest margin. They behave like creatures that have been displaced — specifically, creatures that should be further north, that have been pushed south by something that has disrupted the Greymarch's usual territorial patterns. He has seen this before. The last time he saw it, a vampire lord was establishing a new domain forty miles past the Corpse Road and the Khanate spent three years cleaning it up. --- ## The Standing Northern Call The Wardbreakers' charter in Reedford carries an unusual addendum — unusual for the Lower Ivora Flats, at least. It is a **Standing Northern Call**: a permanent open contract, maintained at nominal retainer, for any work relating to undead incursions, northern creature displacement, or Greymarch-adjacent threats. This contract exists because Vardis insisted on it as a condition of the founding. It has been activated four times in fourteen years. The current retainer, paid quarterly from a fund jointly maintained by House Valdrek and a Lyrran merchant insurance consortium that covers river goods, is small — twelve silver per quarter. It is enough to cover the cost of maintaining the contract's legal standing. Actual activation requires independent evidence that the threat is northern in origin and escalation approval from the charter holder. The Standing Northern Call is not advertised. It is not secret. It exists in the Wardbreakers' charter, which is a public document that anyone in Reedford could theoretically read. Nobody has read it except Cors, who read it looking for impropriety and found it legally impeccable, and Ferren Yolt, who read it because he reads everything and noted it in a margin annotation on one of his observation maps. **What the Standing Northern Call means for players:** It is the institutional mechanism by which the Wardbreakers can take on work that exceeds the usual monster-hunting remit. A party that discovers something of northern origin — a displaced creature, evidence of unusual Greymarch activity, a connection between the confluence point anomalies and something that Vardis identifies as northern in character — can activate or work within the context of the Standing Northern Call. It gives the Wardbreakers legal standing to operate beyond Corvessa's immediate policing concerns and provides a framework for escalating to the Ashcrown Khanate if the situation warrants. Vardis's condition for activation is not dramatic. It is: *"Show me something I haven't seen before that I can't explain from southern causes, and we'll talk."* --- ## Updated NPC Entry: Vardis Ashmarch | Detail | Value | |--------|-------| | **Role** | Wardbreakers co-founder and northern specialist | | **Race** | Orc, male | | **Age** | 61 | | **Starting Attitude** | Professionally neutral — assesses everyone for competence, not character | | **Key Information** | Khanate reference; creature displacement suspicion; Standing Northern Call; three-finger right hand | | **Faith Position** | Ashcrown fire-worship tradition, practiced privately; declines the Pattern-greeting in the same direct way as Orven Staak, and for reasons he would explain if asked | | **What He Wants** | Enough information to know whether his suspicion about displacement is correct, and if it is, to do something about it before it becomes a problem that requires the Khanate to clean up | | **What He'll Trade** | Northern creature knowledge, Khanate network access, institutional weight of the Standing Northern Call | | **What He Won't Do** | Speculate publicly. What they are not present as is a person. The crisis has no name in Reedford. It has no face. It arrived as a change in the quality of something, not as a letter, not as a loss, not as someone who knew someone who was there. This is the gap. The following NPC addresses it. --- ## Mira Sunken-Lance — The Factor's Assistant **Mira Sunken-Lance**, age 34, human, is Factor Harun Belessi's assistant — a position that has existed for eight months and that Belessi describes, when asked, as *"a temporary arrangement pending reassignment."* She arrived in Reedford with credentials from the Kel Varyan trade apparatus that are technically in order and practically inexplicable for someone her age with her apparent background. She handles correspondence, maintains the manifest records, and does roughly two-thirds of the actual administrative work of the Imperial factor's office. Her name is not Sunken-Lance in the Kel Varyan registration. That is what she calls herself, and nobody in Reedford has examined the original credential documents closely enough to notice the discrepancy. **What she actually is:** She was a third-tier administrative functionary in the Kel Varyan trade apparatus — specifically, she worked in the Prophecy Coordination Office, the department responsible for integrating prophetic guidance from the Tide-Readers into commercial licensing decisions. This is not a powerful position. It is, or was, a position in which you saw a great deal of how the system actually worked, because the Prophecy Coordination Office was where prophetic guidance was translated into bureaucratic language, and the people doing the translation saw both sides. The Prophecy Coordination Office was dissolved in the immediate aftermath of the Threadbearer assassinations when the Tide-Readers' visions became so contradictory as to be operationally unusable. Forty-three staff were reassigned. Mira was not reassigned. Her department head — a man named Vellin Crest, a Tide-Reader whose ability to translate prophetic vision into actionable commercial guidance had made him indispensable for twelve years — walked into the Prophecy Pools on Day 3 after the assassinations and did not come out. She does not know whether he drowned, or walked deeper, or was taken by something that lives in what the Pools have become. She left Kel Varyan on Day 11 with reassignment papers she wrote herself, using credentials that were not forged so much as promoted — she had the access to generate them and the technical knowledge to make them pass scrutiny. She came as far south as Reedford because Reedford is where the river route ends and the Imperial lowlands begin, and she hasn't yet decided whether to continue. **What Mira knows:** - The Kel Varyan prophetic apparatus is not failing. It has failed. Past tense. There is no longer a functioning Tide-Reader coordination system. The visions are there — the Tide-Readers still dream — but they are contradictory, recursive, and in at least three cases showing events that have not happened yet and cannot be integrated with events that have already occurred. - The Prophecy Pools in Kel Varyan's administrative district were closed to public access on Day 5. She knows why. She was present when the decision was made. The official reason is "maintenance." The actual reason is that two administrators who visited the Pools on Days 4 and 5 came back reporting that the Pool showed them their own deaths — different deaths, different circumstances, both involving water. - The Threadbearer assassinations were not random. She does not know who orchestrated them or why. She knows that the Prophecy Coordination Office received seventeen prophetic notices in the six months before the assassinations that, in retrospect, described them — in fragments, in metaphor, in the allusive way that Water Thread prophecy works — and that those notices were classified as "pending integration" because they couldn't be reconciled with other active prophecies. The system failed to act not because the warning wasn't there, but because the warning was too coherent with seventeen other warnings that each contradicted one another in detail. **What Mira doesn't know:** - Whether Vellin Crest is alive. - Whether the credentials she generated will hold scrutiny indefinitely (they won't; she has perhaps three to six more months before a routine audit catches the discrepancy). - Whether leaving was the right decision. She thinks about this most nights. - What she should do with what she knows. **What Belessi knows:** He suspects the credentials are irregular. He has not pursued it because Mira does genuinely excellent administrative work, because the trade apparatus is in enough chaos that one irregular functionary is not his most pressing concern, and because he has a vague, unexamined sense that she is frightened of something and that asking would require him to respond to whatever she said. **Mira in play:** She is not a quest-giver. She is not positioned to be found by asking around — she keeps a low profile and Belessi doesn't discuss her. She becomes available through the manifests, through the factor's office, or through the grey trade network if a party member is pursuing the Joss connection (Mira has noticed several cargo irregularities in the manifest records and has quietly not reported them, which is itself irregular for a credential holder of her stated grade, and which Joss has noticed and filed away as potential leverage or potential alliance, depending on how things develop). She will share what she knows, in pieces, if she trusts the recipients. Trust, for Mira, means two things: demonstrated competence and demonstrated ability to keep information from moving to Cors. She will know, within a day of meeting a party, whether they have the second quality. It takes longer to determine the first. **The name.** Sunken-Lance is a direct translation of her family name in a minor Kel Varyan dialect — *vel'mira soth-kaela*, the collapsed lance — a working-class name from the delta district that she has carried into her cover identity as a small act of defiance. She is the only person in Reedford who knows this, and she would find it quietly meaningful if anyone ever worked it out. --- --- # SECTION IV: THE CRIMSON VEIL'S REACH ## *Vampire Influence on River Trade and the Estuary* --- ## Distance and Its Limits Reedford is not a coastal town. The Crimson Veil's vampire ships raid the northern sea lanes and, in bad years, push as far south as the Velcran estuary — approximately 280 miles downstream. Direct Crimson Veil activity at Reedford itself has never been documented. This does not mean the Crimson Veil is absent from Reedford's economic reality. It means it arrives at Reedford translated through the mechanisms of trade, insurance, and information. --- ## The Insurance Adjustment The Lyrran merchant insurance consortium that part-funds the Wardbreakers' Standing Northern Call retainer — the same consortium Belessi's manifests show as underwriting approximately forty percent of the high-value goods moving through Reedford's warehouses — raised its estuary-adjacent premiums in Year 486 by fifteen percent. The official reason given was "increased navigational hazard assessment." The actual reason, available to anyone willing to read the consortium's quarterly assessments (Belessi has them; they are not classified), is "Crimson Veil activity within documented range of Velcran approaches." What happened in Year 486: two Lyrran commercial vessels were taken within ninety nautical miles of the Velcran estuary. Not sunk — taken. The crews were found alive on the coast three days later, pale, dehydrated, and unable to account for approximately forty-eight hours. The ships were not found. The consortium's assessment team determined this was consistent with Crimson Veil operational methodology and adjusted premiums accordingly. **The effect on Reedford:** Cargo that might otherwise have moved by sea along the Lyrran coast now moves by river because the river is insurable at the old rates. This has increased commercial volume through Reedford's warehouses by approximately twelve percent over the past year and is a significant contributor to Valdrek's current relative financial stability. He understands exactly why his income is up. He does not discuss it because discussing it would require acknowledging that his prosperity is downstream of vampire piracy, which is not the kind of acknowledgment that lands well in a Faith-observant community. The premium adjustment also means that any party member investigating Reedford's commerce through Belessi's manifests or Valdrek's accounts will find a clear economic signal of Crimson Veil proximity, even without knowing to look for it. --- ## Syndra Velth's Other Skill **Syndra Velth**, the Imperial currency exchange at the Counting House, switches greetings mid-conversation based on signals already established in the Definitive Guide. She is Imperial-extraction and professionally fluent in multiple social registers. What the Definitive Guide does not establish is why she is so comfortable with that fluency, and where it comes from. Syndra spent eight years working in Velcran before transferring to Reedford. In Velcran, her position in the Counting House required her to maintain working relationships with Lyrran consortium representatives — including the security assessors who evaluated coastal risk. She knows, through that network, considerably more about Crimson Veil operational patterns than any inland town functionary should. She does not advertise this knowledge. It is available to players who ask her about the Velcran connection, or who ask her specifically about the insurance adjustment, or who approach her after demonstrating they are working on something that involves the estuary. Her information is professional, precise, and offered without theatrical secrecy: she treats Crimson Veil activity as a market factor to be priced, not a horror to be feared, because eight years in Velcran produces a certain pragmatism about what arrives in the fog. **What Syndra knows:** - The two vessels taken in Year 486 were not random targets. Both were carrying a specific type of cargo: raw Threadgold shipments from Thar Vornhal, routed through Kel Varyan and south along the coast. This is not common knowledge. The consortium assessment she reviewed noted it but did not publish it, because publicising that Crimson Veil intelligence services can identify Threadgold shipments would destabilise the insurance market for Thar Vornhal trade entirely. - The ninety-mile penetration of Velcran approaches is historically unusual. The Crimson Veil typically raids the Mirror Chain approaches, not this far north along the Elarion coast. The Year 486 activity represents a southern extension of their operational range that has not previously been documented. - There is a standing arrangement — informal, not written — between the Lyrran consortium and a Velcran harbourmaster's office contact by which unusual estuary activity is reported to the consortium within forty-eight hours. She is part of this information chain. If something unusual appears in the estuary, she hears about it within three days. **The current signal:** Syndra has not received an unusual report in seven weeks. Given the Thread crisis and its effect on prophetic navigation — which Crimson Veil ships use, because their captains are old enough to have developed relationships with the prophetic systems over centuries — she is not certain whether the silence means the threat has retreated or whether the Crimson Veil is operating in ways that the monitoring system isn't currently capable of detecting. --- ## The Red Fog Saying Reedford has a single folk expression for Crimson Veil activity that most residents use without understanding its source. When something arrives unexpectedly from the downstream direction — cargo that wasn't announced, a traveler who arrived without the usual pilot escort, news that moved faster than the river normally allows — someone in Reedford will say: *"came in on the red fog."* This phrase originated in Velcran approximately forty years ago and migrated upriver through the pilot community. It refers to the fogbanks the Crimson Veil uses for cover — described by survivors of encounters as having a faint reddish cast at the edges, though whether this is real or retrospective is debated. In Reedford, the phrase has lost its specific reference and now just means *arrived strangely and quickly* — but pilots of Joss's generation, and older hands like Tem Arvish, know exactly where it comes from and use it with deliberate precision when they mean it in its original sense. Tem Arvish, in particular, has used it twice in the past month in conversation about the Upper Quay departure patterns — specifically about a vessel that arrived from the downstream direction, completed its business in four hours, and departed before dawn, with a manifest that checked out but a crew that Tem didn't recognise from his thirty years of pilot registration records. He has not filed a formal notice because he has no formal position anymore. He mentioned it to Joss. Joss has not yet decided what to do with it. --- ## Updated NPC Additions for Section IV **Syndra Velth — expanded:** | Detail | Value | |--------|-------| | **Role** | Imperial currency exchange, Counting House | | **Key Information (added)** | Velcran background; Lyrran consortium contact; Crimson Veil operational knowledge; Year 486 Threadgold targeting assessment | | **Faith Position** | Unchanged — professionally fluent, no personal observance | | **Trigger for disclosure** | Questions about the estuary, the insurance adjustment, or anything involving the downstream direction from someone who seems to be working on something real | | **What she won't do** | Speculate. He gives Joss a partial description of the vessel's hull markings — unusual black lacquerwork that neither of them recognises as a Lyrran house configuration. **Day 15:** If players have not investigated, Syndra receives a brief consortium communication through the estuary network: *"Unscheduled vessel with modified hull configuration in Velcran approaches, departed east. Crew composition unclear. No manifest filed."* She compares the hull configuration description with the one in her Year 486 assessment notes. It matches the vessels taken in the Year 486 incident — not the ships that were stolen, but the ships that were doing the taking. She will share this with players who have established sufficient trust. She will not go to Cors with it, because the legal position of her Velcran-era information network is not one she wants scrutinised. --- ## Integration Note: All Four Sections The four additions in this supplement are designed to operate independently — each can surface at a different point in the campaign without requiring the others to be present first. However, they converge in a specific pattern that rewards a party that has engaged deeply with Reedford: *The Thornward Cost* explains why Reedford's economic structures are fragile in the ways they are, and provides the historical depth that makes the seventh crusade whispers genuinely threatening rather than abstract. *The Wardbreakers Expanded* provides the institutional and personal framework for escalating northern threats, and connects the confluence point anomalies to the broader Vashkora situation through Vardis's displacement suspicion. *Mira Sunken-Lance* gives the Threadbearer crisis a personal and specific face, and provides information about the crisis that the players cannot get from ambient texture alone — specifically, that the prophetic system's failure was not sudden but cumulative, and that the warning was there and went unheeded. *The Crimson Veil's Reach* connects Reedford to the wider maritime world through the economic signal of the insurance adjustment, provides Syndra with history and purpose beyond her current role, and plants the downstream vessel as a living world event that can develop into something larger if the party pulls the thread. The convergence: a party that has activated all four threads will be in a position to recognise, by around session 8-10, that Reedford is not simply a transit town experiencing isolated local problems. It is a location that is receiving signals from every active crisis in the world — the Thornward wound, the northern threat, the Threadbearer collapse, and the maritime disruption — because it sits at a point in the geography where all of those signals flow through on their way somewhere else. The town does not know this. The party, eventually, will. --- *GM-only document.
