# The core idea

When I say **narrative identity**, I mean this:

> A class should not only tell you how the character fights.  
> It should also tell you what kinds of stories naturally happen around that character.

That is the difference between:

- **mechanical identity**: what buttons you press
- **thematic identity**: the flavour text and vibe
- **narrative identity**: the kind of tension, consequences, relationships, and story beats the class generates

A lot of systems have the first. Good systems have the second. Fewer systems have the third in a strong, structural way.

FEARS already has strong **mechanical identity** and strong **theme**. What I’m proposing is a framework that lets you decide whether you want to strengthen **narrative identity** too.

---

# The three-layer model

Use this as the base framework for every class.

## Layer 1: Mechanical Identity

This is the easiest layer to see.

Ask:

- What does this class do in combat?
- What is its loop?
- What does success look like on a turn?

Examples:

- Rogue: position, exploit, burst
- Warden: hold, punish, deny passage
- Witch: hex, spread, destabilise
- Apothecary: prepare, deploy, react

This is already one of FEARS’ strongest areas.

---

## Layer 2: Thematic Identity

This is the fantasy and imagery.

Ask:

- What is the fantasy promise?
- What emotional tone does the class evoke?
- What kind of character does it imply?

Examples:

- Sangromancer: power bought with blood
- Investigator: occult scholar-hunter
- Bender: disciplined elemental martial artist
- Tattooist: living arcane artist

FEARS is also strong here.

---

## Layer 3: Narrative Identity

This is the most important layer for the framework.

Ask:

- What recurring story pressure does this class create?
- What kind of complications follow them?
- What relationships, obligations, fears, temptations, or consequences are built into being this class?
- How does the world react to them differently from another class?

Examples:

- Witch: spirits notice you, bargains linger, curses spread beyond your intent
- Reaper: death follows you, the dying affect you, people fear what you harvest
- Warden: places become “yours,” retreat has moral weight, boundaries matter
- Investigator: knowledge has cost, obsession grows, truth isolates

This is the layer I mean when I compare FEARS to Blades.

---

# The simplest definition

You can boil the whole thing down to one sentence:

> A class has narrative identity when the system helps create class-specific story problems, not just class-specific combat choices.

That’s the real test.

---

# The framework itself

For each class, build narrative identity using six parts.

## 1. Narrative Promise

What story does this class invite?

This is not “what powers do they have?”  
It is:

- what kind of arc are they naturally good at?
- what sort of fiction do they pull into the campaign?

Examples:

- Paladin: conviction tested by compromise
- Witch: power through relationships with unseen forces
- Reaper: intimacy with death changes the self
- Inventor: progress creates unintended consequences

This is the class’s story engine.

### Test question

If I removed combat entirely, would this class still imply a distinct kind of story?

If yes, the narrative promise is strong.

---

## 2. Narrative Pressure

What ongoing tension comes with being this class?

This is the most important part.

A class should have a pressure that keeps showing up in play. Not necessarily as punishment, but as tension.

Examples:

- Sorcerer: loss of control, expectation, inherited power
- Warlock: patron demand, dependence, transaction
- Witch: spirit debt, taboo knowledge, unintended curse spillover
- Sangromancer: corruption, stigma, bodily cost
- Investigator: obsession, pattern-seeking, inability to let mysteries rest
- Warden: duty to place, inability to walk away from threat

Narrative pressure is what stops flavour from being passive.

### Test question

What trouble follows this character even when they are not choosing trouble?

That answer is usually the narrative pressure.

---

## 3. Narrative Currency

What does the class gain, spend, risk, or accumulate in story terms?

You already have some excellent possible currencies in FEARS:

- Focus
- Sanity
- Corruption
- Thread strain
- social reputation
- spirit debt
- omen marks
- anchor resonance
- eldritch attention

This is where system and fiction meet.

Examples:

- Witch gains Spirit Debt when binding or compelling spirits
- Sangromancer gains Corruption when pushing beyond safe thresholds
- Investigator gains Obsession or Insight strain from forbidden truths
- Reaper gains Death Resonance from repeated harvesting
- Warden gains Territory Bond from protecting a place
- Bender gains Elemental Imbalance from over-leaning into one element

A narrative currency does three things:

- records what the story says is happening
- creates future consequences or opportunities
- distinguishes one class’s non-combat arc from another’s

### Test question

What invisible cost or trace does this class leave behind?

That trace is often your currency.

---

## 4. World Reaction

How do people, factions, places, spirits, institutions, or the Weave react to this class?

This is where the class stops being private flavour and starts becoming setting reality.

Examples:

- Sangromancer: feared, hunted, distrusted, studied
- Investigator: sought out for impossible cases but isolated socially
- Witch: tolerated in rural places, condemned in formal institutions, pursued by spirits
- Inventor: desired by states, feared by guilds, blamed for accidents
- Warden: recognised by local communities, resented by encroaching powers
- Reaper: welcomed in war, feared in peace

This does not need to be constant punishment. It can also be access.

Examples of positive reactions:

- Druids may be recognised by primal sites
- Investigators may gain audience where others would not
- Wardens may calm a wild place
- Apothecaries may be essential in plague zones

The point is:

> the world should know what this class is

### Test question

If two strangers meet a Witch and a Fighter, does the setting have a reason to treat them differently before initiative starts?

If no, then world reaction is weak.

---

## 5. Story Triggers

What kinds of moments should repeatedly happen for this class in play?

These are recurring beats. They help GMs and players know what to bring forward.

Examples:

- Witch: bargains, hauntings, contamination, whispered offers
- Investigator: clues, forbidden archives, half-truths, false patterns
- Reaper: funerary rites, battlefield aftermath, ghosts of the recently dead
- Warden: sacred ground, broken boundaries, migrations, intrusions
- Apothecary: disease, scarcity, triage, desperate improvisation
- Tattooist: marks of belonging, stolen designs, symbolic body magic
- Shaman: offended spirits, totem desecration, ancestral demands

These are not mandatory scenes. They are recurring story prompts.

### Test question

What 5 scene types should appear more often because this class is in the party?

That answer defines story triggers.

---

## 6. Narrative Reward

What kind of story behaviour should feel rewarded for this class?

This is where you decide whether the system merely suggests narrative identity or actively reinforces it.

Reward does not have to mean XP only. It could be:

- advantage in certain interactions
- access to lore
- narrative permissions
- reduced costs
- class-specific downtime gains
- stronger recoveries when acting in accordance with identity
- special bonds or anchor interactions

Examples:

- Warden rewarded for defending a chosen place or person
- Investigator rewarded for solving mysteries and pursuing truth despite risk
- Witch rewarded for fulfilling or renegotiating spirit obligations
- Reaper rewarded for giving the dead proper passage, not just harvesting them
- Inventor rewarded for solving problems through creation, not just force

This is one of the best ways to turn “theme” into actual play.

### Test question

What behaviour should make the player feel, “yes, I am playing my class properly,” even outside combat?

That is the narrative reward target.

# How to use this as a design template

For each class, fill in this structure:

## Class Narrative Identity Template

**1. Narrative Promise**  
What kind of story does this class naturally create?

**2. Narrative Pressure**  
What tension or problem comes with being this class?

**3. Narrative Currency**  
What invisible story-cost, trace, or resource does this class accumulate or manage?

**4. World Reaction**  
How do people, places, factions, and supernatural forces react to this class?

**5. Story Triggers**  
What recurring scene types should this class pull into the campaign?

**6. Narrative Reward**  
What class-appropriate story behaviour should the game encourage?

That’s the framework in its cleanest usable form.

---

# A worked example: Witch

Here is what it looks like in full.

## 1. Narrative Promise

A mediator and manipulator of spirits whose power always comes with relationship, omen, and unintended spread.

## 2. Narrative Pressure

Spirits are not tools. They remember coercion, they make demands, and curses often travel farther than intended.

## 3. Narrative Currency

**Spirit Debt**  
Built when binding, compelling, borrowing from, or betraying spirits.

## 4. World Reaction

Villagers may seek her in desperation but fear her privately. Formal religions distrust her. Spirits notice her presence more readily than others.

## 5. Story Triggers

Hauntings, inherited curses, owed favours, offended household spirits, bargains, eldritch bleed-through through old bindings.

## 6. Narrative Reward

Reward the Witch for:

- solving problems through spirit negotiation
- accepting or cleverly twisting obligations
- spreading a curse at a cost she then has to live with

Now the class has:

- combat identity
- theme
- story engine

That is the difference.

---

# Another worked example: Warden

## 1. Narrative Promise

A living boundary whose identity is tied to protection, place, and refusal.

## 2. Narrative Pressure

A Warden cannot easily abandon what they have claimed to defend. Failure is personal and territorial.

## 3. Narrative Currency

**Territory Bond** or **Guard Oath**  
Built when they defend a site, people, or path repeatedly.

## 4. World Reaction

Communities near the defended place trust or depend on them. Predators, invaders, and rival powers learn their name. The land itself may answer to them.

## 5. Story Triggers

Border violations, sacred places under threat, natural imbalance, requests for guardianship, conflict between duty and mobility.

## 6. Narrative Reward

Reward the Warden for:

- holding a line others would abandon
- taking burdens of defense personally
- defining and protecting meaningful ground

Now the Warden is not just “tank with elemental powers.”  
They are a story type.

# The four levels of implementation

You do not need to go all the way. This framework can be used lightly or heavily.

## Level 1: Flavour Guidance Only

You add these ideas as prose to class intros, sidebars, GM advice, and campaign prompts.

Best if you want:

- low complexity
- strong world flavour
- no new systems

This is the lightest version.

---

## Level 2: Soft Narrative Hooks

You add suggested triggers and rewards, but they are mostly adjudicated by the GM.

Examples:

- “Witches often attract spirit obligations”
- “Wardens may form deep ties to defended places”
- “Investigators risk obsession when pursuing forbidden truths”

Best if you want:

- identity in play
- little rules overhead
- flexibility

---

## Level 3: Light Mechanics

You add one class-specific narrative currency or pressure mechanic to each class.

Examples:

- Spirit Debt
- Obsession
- Death Resonance
- Territory Bond
- Invention Heat
- Blood Taint

These do not need full subsystems. Even a 0–3 track is enough.

Best if you want:

- strong identity
- visible story consequence
- still manageable complexity

---

## Level 4: Full Narrative Integration

Classes directly interact with:

- Sanity
- Focus
- Corruption
- faction standing
- downtime
- exploration
- recovery
- class-specific obligations

This would make FEARS much more systemically narrative.

Best if you want:

- very high cohesion
- strong campaign-facing class identity
- a heavier, more distinctive game

This is powerful, but you should only go here if you want the game to lean that way consistently.

---

# The decision framework for you

To decide whether you want this, ask these questions.

## Question 1

Do you want classes to matter mainly in combat, or also in campaign texture?

If combat only, stop at Level 1 or 2.  
If campaign texture too, go to Level 3.

## Question 2

Do you want the world to care what class someone is?

If yes, you need World Reaction and Story Triggers.

## Question 3

Do you want class fantasy to have a cost beyond action economy and HP?

If yes, you need Narrative Pressure and Currency.

## Question 4

Do you want a Witch, Warlock, and Sorcerer to feel different outside combat even when all are charismatic spellcasters?

If yes, this framework helps a lot.

## Question 5

Do you want players to be guided into class-shaped arcs without railroading them?

If yes, use soft or light mechanics rather than hard mandatory story rules.

---

# The main caution

This framework is powerful, but it can go wrong in two ways.

## Risk 1: Overdetermination

If you force the story too hard, players may feel trapped.

Example:

- “Every Witch must be hunted”
- “Every Reaper must be feared”
- “Every Warden must stay in one place”

That becomes restrictive instead of evocative.

The fix:  
Make these pressures common and meaningful, not mandatory every time.

---

## Risk 2: Mechanical bloat

If every class gets a full extra subsystem, the game may become overloaded.

The fix:  
Give each class one clear pressure or currency, not five.

---

# My recommended version for FEARS

For FEARS specifically, I would recommend this structure:

Every class gets:

- 1 Narrative Promise
- 1 Narrative Pressure
- 1 World Reaction note
- 3–5 Story Triggers

Only some classes get a real currency track:

- Witch
- Sangromancer
- Reaper
- Investigator
- Warden
- Shaman
- Inventor
- maybe Warlock

Why these?  
Because they already imply consequence-heavy identities and FEARS already has setting tools that suit them.

Other classes can remain lighter:

- Fighter
- Rogue
- Barbarian
- Ranger
- Monk
- Paladin
- Bard
- Cleric
- Wizard
- Sorcerer

They still get narrative identity, just with less extra tracking.

# Final distilled framework

Here is the shortest practical version.

For each class, define:

**How it fights**  
Mechanical identity

**What fantasy it promises**  
Thematic identity

**What story tension follows it**  
Narrative pressure

**How the world recognises it**  
World reaction

**What recurring scenes it invites**  
Story triggers

**What behaviour the game should encourage**  
Narrative reward

If all six are present, the class is no longer just a rules package.  
It becomes a campaign-shaping identity.
