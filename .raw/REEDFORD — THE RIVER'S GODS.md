# REEDFORD — THE RIVER'S GODS ## *Divine Presence, Folk Practice, and the Spirits Beneath the Faith* > *"The Pattern holds the Weave. But the river has its own memory."* > — Saying among Ivora pilots, origin unattributed > *"Pray to the Codex in the sanctum. Pray to the water before you step onto the boat."* > — Overheard at the Mudlark, attributed to nobody in particular --- *This supplement details how the Water Pantheon's spirits, and traces of other divine traditions, manifest in Reedford's daily life — not as formal worship competing with the Faith, but as the sediment beneath it. These are the habits nobody quite remembers learning, the shrines nobody quite remembers building, the sayings that survived the Faith's formalisation because nobody thought to remove them. For the GM, they are texture, plot hooks, and a reminder that Reedford sits on a river older than the Codex.* --- ## PART I: THE LOGIC OF LAYERED BELIEF Reedford's Faith observance is genuine but pragmatic — the town prays because the calendar says to, and because Inquisitor Cors is watching. What sits beneath the Faith's formal structure is not heresy. It is something harder to prosecute: **habit**. River towns accumulate practice the way rivers accumulate silt. Over enough generations, the silt becomes the riverbed. The pilots who knock twice on the hull before departure don't think of themselves as performing a religious act. They think of themselves as not being stupid. The factor who pours the first inch of water from the morning pitcher onto the ground before drinking isn't propitiating a spirit. She's doing what her mother did. **The Faith's position on this:** Technically, any invocation of named spirits outside the sanctioned pantheon requires a Fraywatcher certificate. Practically, the Faith in Reedford has understood for six generations that a town of eight thousand people on a working river is going to have river habits, and that enforcing theological purity over every knocked hull and poured libation would produce more resentment than righteousness. Sethos has made a pastoral calculation: what the Codex doesn't see, the Pattern doesn't need to correct. Cors has made a different calculation: documentation, for later. **The result:** Reedford's folk religious layer is not hidden. It's just unspoken. People don't announce what they do before a run. They simply do it. --- ## PART II: THE SHRINES ### The River-Marker Stone **Location:** In the Ivora itself, approximately forty metres upstream of the main quay. Visible at low water as a dark, mossy shoulder of stone; submerged to mid-height at peak flow. Pilots and experienced quay-workers know exactly where it is. **What it is:** A stone marker of unknown age — older than Reedford's current name, older than the Valdrek charter, possibly older than the Faith's formal presence in Corvessa. Carved into its upstream face are three symbols: Marethis's winding-river glyph (a river splitting in two directions), Ithrien's unbreaking wave, and a third symbol worn past legibility that those with Water Pantheon knowledge would recognise as an archaic form of Zerathis's clasped hands. **What people do:** Pilots departing the Upper Quay on good-weather days frequently orient their vessel to pass within a few metres of the stone — not so close as to ground, but close enough to brush past it. They don't explain this to passengers. On the Mudlark's wall there is a chalk tally: a running count of boats that have passed the stone safely this season. Nobody takes responsibility for maintaining the tally. Somebody always does. **Cors's position:** He has recorded its existence. He has not yet decided whether it constitutes a prohibited shrine or merely a geological feature with cultural associations. The legal distinction matters: the former requires a formal proceeding; the latter requires nothing. He is waiting to see if anyone performs an explicit act of worship at it before committing to a position. Three years of waiting. **What Sethos knows:** That the stone is probably pre-Faith and probably Marethis-adjacent, and that the pilots' habit of passing close to it has kept more boats off the gravel bars than his Weaveday blessing. He has not looked at it directly in eleven years. He considers this a spiritual discipline. **GM note:** Players with Religion (DC 12) who examine the stone can identify Marethis's glyph. DC 16 identifies Ithrien's. DC 20 identifies the third as Zerathis — and recognises that the three together represent a complete river-compact: *guidance, safe passage, and oaths held*. --- ### The Bridge Niches (South Quay Bridge) **What they are:** Two carved stone niches on either side of the south-end bridge across the Quay Road, each holding a worn axe-head — the Faith's standard symbol. The axe-heads are approximately 150 years old, grimy, and touched unconsciously by almost everyone who crosses. **What the document already establishes:** People touch the upstream axe-head without slowing. Muscle memory from childhood. **What the supplement adds:** The upstream niche is Ithrien. The downstream niche is Tzhor. Nobody has used those names in a generation. But the original placement was deliberate: the upstream niche faces the direction the Ivora comes from, toward the source and the journey ahead; the downstream niche faces where the river goes, toward the sea, and toward what the river keeps. Touching upstream before you depart is asking for safe passage. Touching downstream when you return is acknowledging what you came back from. The Faith's redecoration of the niches with axe-heads approximately 150 years ago was a successful theological reframe — officially, they now represent the Pattern's protection of travellers. The habit the axe-heads inherited, however, is older. Touching the upstream axe before a journey, downstream upon return: this is still what people do, with complete unconsciousness, in an act that means exactly what it always meant. **Variant behaviour worth noting:** - Stevedores going to the Lower Quay before dawn touch only the upstream niche. They will return the same day. - Pilots departing on multi-day upper-river runs touch upstream going out, and stand for a moment at the downstream niche when they return — not touching it, just pausing. Joss does this. He could not tell you why. - Tessra, who lost her uncle at Thornward, touches neither. She crosses the bridge on the extreme outer edge, furthest from both niches. --- ### The Quay Drain Offering **Location:** The primary drainage channel where the main quay surface slopes toward the Ivora. A narrow stone runnel, always damp, slightly green with algae. **What people do:** The first bucket of wash-water poured on the quay each Weaveday morning is poured toward the drain rather than away from it — a small redirection of effort, always upstream-facing. Factors do this. Dock supervisors do this. The stevedores' morning leader does this before calling the shifts. It is so habitual that new workers copy it within a week without being told. **What it means, theologically:** This is Ithraval — the River's Bargain. The belief, unspoken and unformulated, that water controlled for civilisation's benefit should be returned to the river with acknowledgment. The quay extracts value from the Ivora; the morning pour is the town's unconscious accounting. **What the Faith says about it:** Nothing. No one has asked. If pressed, any Codex-literate person would say it's practical drainage management. Cors once watched a factor perform it and wrote in his notes: *"poured water south. Standard quay maintenance."* He was not wrong. He was also not right. --- ### Maret's Shrine Room **Location:** The back room of Maret's community gathering space in the Reed Quarter. Officially a storage room. Actually the most active site of deliberate Water Pantheon and Spirit Thread observance in Reedford. **What's there:** A shallow stone basin kept always full of river water, changed daily. Three river stones resting in it, each with a small carved mark: one Marethis, one Ithrien, one — this is the one Cors would find most troubling — Eryndal, the One Who Listens, who receives the final words of the dying. **Why Eryndal:** Maret's community programs include end-of-life support for Reed Quarter families who can't afford a full sanctum ceremony. In Halfling tradition, the dying tell their best story before passing. Maret has adapted this so the story is spoken over the basin — told to the water, on the belief that Eryndal will carry it. This is unambiguously outside the Codex. It is also the only comfort several families in the Reed Quarter have received in their worst hours. **How she explains it if asked:** "Water carries things. I just make sure the water knows to listen." Sethos has heard this explanation. He said nothing and walked away. **What the basin looks like:** River water, always clear because she changes it every morning. The three stones are smooth, unremarkable, worn. Any halfling with knowledge of Joy Truths would recognise the stone arrangement as a variant of the Three-Thread Naming (ancestor, god, child) adapted for grief work. Maret would not use those words. She would say: *"One for where you came from. One for the journey. One for what you leave behind."* --- ### The Counting House Silver **Location:** Syndra Velth's desk at the Counting House. Not a shrine. Possibly a shrine. **What it is:** A small silver disc, no markings, kept under the corner of Syndra's primary ledger book. She takes it out, turns it over once, and replaces it before beginning each day's work. She has done this for nine years, since the day a deal she'd witnessed in Kel Varyan collapsed and ruined three parties who hadn't kept their word on the water. She carries the disc as a reminder that wealth that moves fairly keeps moving. Wealth that doesn't will eventually stop. **What spirit this invokes:** Syralis, the Silver Current. Not consciously. Syndra would describe herself as having "a professional discipline around fair dealing." The disc is "just an old habit from the south." If someone described what she was doing as invoking Syralis, she would look at them with the expression of someone deciding whether to be offended. --- ### The Scale and Anchor's Plaque (Addition) **What the document already establishes:** Twelve names on a beam above the east fireplace. Thornward dead. Tessra's uncle. **What the supplement adds:** Below the plaque, almost invisible unless you're sitting at the specific corner table, is a small carved mark worked into the beam's lower edge — not an axe, not a Faith glyph. A knotted rope, half-submerged. Vaedoris, the Oath of the Tide. The Oaths Remembered. **Who put it there and when:** Tessra's father, the year the plaque went up. He carved it himself, the night after the dedication, with a belt-knife. Tessra knows it's there. She has never explained it to anyone. It means: *the names on that plaque are oaths the river holds. They went and did not come back. The water remembers.* Nobody has noticed it is not a Faith symbol. The beam is high and dark and the Scale and Anchor is always busy. --- ## PART III: FOLK SAYINGS AND THEIR ORIGINS The following sayings are in active circulation in Reedford. Most speakers have no idea where they come from. Theologically literate characters (Religion DC 14+) may recognise the divine origins; river-workers (Insight or History DC 10) will know them as standard Ivora idiom. --- **"Follow the current; it knows the way."** *Spoken when giving directions, making a decision, or counselling patience.* **Origin:** Tylos, the River-Seeker. Pre-Faith river-worker wisdom that the water's path is more reliable than a traveller's assumptions. Sethos uses this phrase in pastoral counselling. He would say it expresses the Water Thread's doctrine of flow and inevitability. He is not wrong. He is also, technically, echoing Tylos. --- **"What's sworn on the water holds."** *Spoken when sealing a deal, confirming a promise, or calling out a broken one.* **Origin:** Zerathis, the Silver Oath. This phrase is the reason Joss always makes his arrangements at the water's edge — the parties understand, instinctively, that this context is different from a tavern handshake. Cassin Valdrek uses this phrase. He thinks of it as a commercial custom. Cors has noted it in passing as "regional contract idiom, possibly pre-Codex." He has not investigated further. **Variant:** *"Swear it on the Ivora."* Used when someone is pressing for a more binding commitment. River-workers treat this as the highest available level of personal assurance. --- **"The river doesn't forget a body."** *Spoken about consequences, inevitability, and the impossibility of truly hiding something done near water.* **Origin:** Tzhor, the Drowned King, and Vaedoris, the Oath of the Tide, in combination. Those lost to the river are kept by it. Those who do something near the water have done it in front of a witness that never leaves. This phrase is used most often as a warning — by parents to children, by old pilots to young ones — but its meaning has expanded to cover general consequence: *the Ivora saw what you did.* Daven Thatch used this phrase when a warehouse operator tried to deny a weight discrepancy that had been measured at the quay. He was not making a theological statement. The warehouse operator immediately produced corrected figures. --- **"Let it find its level."** *Spoken when urging patience, recommending against intervention, or describing a situation resolving itself.* **Origin:** This is Maret's variant of the Pattern-greeting response ("the water finds its level"), which is itself derived from Marethis doctrine. In folk usage, it has fully divorced from any theological context. Sethos says it. Cassin Valdrek says it. Daven Thatch says it. Orven Staak has been heard to grunt it. Cors does not say it. He considers entropy a failure mode, not a virtue. --- **"Good water, clean run."** *Spoken as a farewell to departing pilots, or as an expression of goodwill before any risky undertaking.* **Origin:** Ithrien, the Tidemother's Blessing. River-pilot community. The full version is *"good water and a clean run home,"* but it has shortened over generations. Tem Arvish says it to every pilot he registers. Joss says it back without thinking. It means: *may the river carry you and return you.* It is, in effect, a prayer. Nobody calls it that. --- **"Don't dam the Ivora."** *Spoken against hoarding, monopolisation, or any action that stops the natural flow of goods, information, or goodwill.* **Origin:** Syralis, the Silver Current, and Ithraval, the River's Bargain — the twin beliefs that wealth must keep moving and that the river will punish those who try to control it entirely. This phrase is used most often by stevedores and pilots against the factors, and occasionally by factors against the lord. Daven Thatch deployed it during the crane-three work action. It landed well. Valdrek knows this phrase is aimed at him approximately thirty percent of the time it's used in his presence. He gives no indication of noticing. --- **"The Ivora's got your measure."** *Spoken of someone who has been on the river long enough that their character is known — or who has done something the river has "seen."* **Origin:** Vaedoris and Tzhor together, filtered through generations of river-worker culture. The river is a witness. The river knows the worth and the failing of everyone who works it long enough. Used admiringly ("Joss? The Ivora's got his measure — solid pilot") and as a threat ("the Ivora's got your measure, and we all know it"). --- **"One foot wet, one foot dry — pick a side."** *Spoken to someone who is being indecisive, duplicitous, or trying to maintain incompatible positions.* **Origin:** River-bank idiom. No specific divine origin, but resonates with Ithraval's domain — the god of the bargain between the wild river and controlled civilisation. Used by: Renn Ashford (frequently), Cassin Valdrek (occasionally, with precision), Hevra Plum (constantly, at gamblers who hedge). --- **"What the current carries, the current keeps."** *Spoken of things lost, irreversible decisions, and the futility of trying to reclaim what's been let go.* **Origin:** Tzhor. River-workers who lose equipment, cargo, or companions to the Ivora do not attempt to search below a certain depth. The river has what the river has. Grief is acknowledged; retrieval is not attempted. This phrase has expanded to cover any irrecoverable situation. Sethos used it at a funeral, once. The family found it more comforting than the Codex's prescribed words. He has not told Cors this. --- **"Sarvion sent it."** *Spoken of unexpected good fortune, a windfall, or a contract that materialised without obvious cause.* **Origin:** Sarvion, the Unlooked-For Rain — the blessing that arrives when you've stopped expecting it. This is the only saying on this list where the spirit's name is still used directly, and only in this contracted, half-jocular form. Most speakers couldn't tell you who Sarvion is. They know the phrase means "luck came from nowhere." Factors use it. Tessra uses it. Aldous Caer uses it about particularly good wine arriving. Cors has heard this phrase six times and made a note: *"Sarvion — pre-Codex water spirit, domain unknown. Name in folk use, non-invocative. Monitor."* He has not yet moved it from the monitor category to the investigate category. --- ## PART IV: TRADITIONS AND HABITUAL PRACTICES ### River-Pilot Traditions **The Double Knock** Before casting off, pilots knock twice on the hull at the waterline — once aft, once at the bow. Not a knock for passengers or cargo. A knock done when alone, or turned away, or done quietly. The explanation, when one is offered, is structural: *"checking the hull's sound."* The hull is never actually checked this way. *What it is:* Ithrien. The asking of permission from the water. The acknowledgment that the river could refuse you. **The Push-Off Pause** Joss, and the three other pilots in town who've run the upper river for fifteen or more years, all pause for approximately three seconds after pushing off from the quay — standing in the boat, not yet moving to the tiller, facing upstream. Nobody taught this. They all arrived at it independently. *What it is:* The listening posture of Tylos. The water tells you, in those three seconds, what kind of run it will be. Experienced pilots know this is true without being able to explain how. **Stillwater Departures** Joss consistently departs on ghost-road runs on Stillwater, the seventh day of the week — the Faith's calendar calls it a day of reflection. River-workers have always called it the day the Ivora runs most honestly: the commercial traffic is low, the water hasn't been churned by barge traffic, and the current shows its actual character rather than its working-day character. *What it is:* Marethis recognition — the river is most itself when the town stops using it. Departing on Stillwater is departing in accord with the river rather than in exploitation of it. **The Cargo Toast** When a particularly valuable or difficult cargo is secured and loaded, the supervising factor will, at some point during loading, walk the length of the barge alone and pour a small amount of whatever they're drinking — water, ale, wine — overboard. Always toward the upstream side. Always silently. *What it is:* Ithraval. The acknowledgment that the river is a party to the transaction. Belessi does this, and he's been in Reedford eight months. He picked it up without noticing. --- ### Commercial Traditions **The Weaveday Morning Pour** The first wash-bucket of Weaveday morning goes to the drain, upstream-facing. (Described above.) What the document should add: this is also the moment when factors traditionally open their ledgers for the first time. No entry is made before the pour. An old factor who trained in Reedford would be genuinely, viscerally uncomfortable making a ledger entry before the pour — in the same way a Corvessan aristocrat would be uncomfortable signing a contract facing a non-Pattern direction. **Rootday Contract Signing** Already established: contracts signed on Rootday carry additional legal weight. What the supplement adds: specifically, contracts signed at the Upper Quay — on or near the water — on Rootday are considered doubly binding by the merchant community. The Faith's Earth Thread gives the day its weight. Zerathis gives the location its weight. Nobody uses Zerathis's name. They just know that a Rootday quay contract means something that a Rootday tavern contract does not. **The Honest Weigh** Before the primary market scale is used each Floodtide morning, Tem Arvish's predecessor (before his bad knee) would pour a small cup of river water over the scale plate, then dry it, then begin weighing. Tem Arvish's successor at the Pilots' Register does this before recording any licensing transaction. Orven Staak does this with his yard-scale before accepting a salvage measurement. *What it is:* A ritual of accuracy and honesty that invokes no god by name but sits squarely in Ithraval's domain — the river as the standard of true measure, the thing that cannot lie about its own level. **Grey Trade Location Protocol** All significant grey trade arrangements — Layer 2 and above — are conducted at the water's edge, not under a roof. This is understood as practical (no witnesses, easier exit routes), but the deeper logic is older: *what's sworn on the water holds.* Zerathis is the silent guarantor of agreements between people who cannot rely on courts. Among those who work the grey economy, this is the most reliable enforcement mechanism available. --- ### Domestic and Community Traditions **The Morning Pitcher** In Reed Quarter households, the morning pitcher of water is not drunk from first. The first inch is poured onto the threshold — outside the door, not inside. This is Ithraval and Marethis together: the house taking its share of controlled water should acknowledge the source. Children are taught to do this by the time they can hold a cup. It has no name. It is simply what you do. **River Stones for the Dead** When a Reedford resident dies, family members go to the Ivora within three days and select a smooth stone from the riverbed. The stone is placed with the body for the sanctum ceremony, then — regardless of where the body is buried or committed — returned to the river afterward. The Faith's ceremony makes no provision for this. Sethos has never told a family not to do it. *What it is:* Tzhor. The stone carries the dead person's name to the river, where the Drowned King holds what the water is given. Even those who die on land are, by this practice, partially committed to the Ivora's keeping. Families who have lost people to the river already, who didn't get to perform the stone-return, find this tradition painful in a specific way that's hard to articulate. **Eryndal's Cup** When someone is dying and conscious — and only when they are conscious and able to speak — a family member in the Reed Quarter tradition will bring a cup of river water and hold it near the dying person while they speak. Not for drinking. For listening. The water carries the last words. This is Maret's practice formalised into a community tradition; she has taught it, quietly, to perhaps thirty families over eight years. *What Cors knows:* Nothing. This happens in private rooms in the Reed Quarter. *What Sethos knows:* He has been called to sanctum ceremonies where he noticed the cup still present, not yet emptied. He has said nothing. He has started bringing his own cup of water to such ceremonies, which he pours at the end as part of a "closing blessing" he has added to the standard Codex rite without documentation. **The Gravel Bar Warning** A specific community tradition among the pilots' families: when someone does something dangerous or foolish — not in a criminal way, but in the way of overconfidence — they're told *"you've not charted the bars."* The full version is *"you've not charted the bars and the Ivora doesn't grade on intention."* This is Marethis-inflected common sense: the river does not care that you didn't know the gravel was there. It grounds you anyway. --- ## PART V: NPC PRACTICE — HOW EACH CHARACTER LIVES THIS The following is GM reference for how specific NPCs express these traditions in play. These details are available to players through observation, conversation, or both — they are not secrets, but they require attention to notice. --- **Sethos Annyr** — The Floodtide river-blessing he performs at dawn uses the standard Codex wording for approximately two-thirds of its length. The final third uses older language — a cadence that residents who've attended for thirty years know by heart, and newcomers notice sounds different. The older language is Ithrien-phrasing: *"carry those who offer themselves honestly; return those who have respected the way."* Sethos would say he's preserving pastoral tradition. He is also preserving Ithrien. He keeps a cup of river water on his writing desk that he replaces each Weaveday. He has written extensively about why this is a Water Thread meditation practice. The writing is sincere and theologically coherent. It is also a cup of river water that he changes every week, which is what Maret does, and for not entirely different reasons. --- **Joss** — His great-grandmother's silver earring is river-silver — a specific term among old Ivora families for silver objects dredged or found in the river rather than bought. River-silver is kept, never sold. It accumulates luck the way a river accumulates silt. The earring is not a religious object. It is a piece of his great-grandmother's relationship with the Ivora that he carries. The effect is theologically indistinguishable from a Ithrien token. He pours the first measure of any drink overboard when on the water. Not when in a tavern. Only when afloat. He has done this since his first solo run at seventeen and cannot explain it except to say: *"the river paid for it."* He is the person most likely to use the phrase *"what's sworn on the water holds"* in a context where he means it completely literally. He has ended business relationships over violations of this principle. The party, if they enter a grey-trade arrangement with him, will be asked to confirm it at the quay rail rather than across a table. He will not explain why. --- **Maret** — Her variant of the Pattern-greeting ("and the water finds its level") is not an accident or a quirk. It is a deliberate theological statement she has been making every day for eleven years, in the softest possible way, to anyone who uses the standard greeting with her. She is saying: *the water is the standard, not the Pattern. The water is the real thing.* Cors has noted it twice. He has not yet decided whether to act on it because she is the Reed Quarter's primary community organiser and moving against her would require more political capital than the statement warrants. She touches the river-marker stone from a punt every Stillwater morning when the water is low enough. She does not perform any visible act at the stone — she simply rests her hand on it for a moment before pushing on. She has done this long enough that a few Reed Quarter families know she does it, and have started doing it themselves on the days they have reason to be out on the water early. --- **Orven Staak** — He doesn't perform any of Reedford's inherited water-folk practice. He has his own. Each time a vessel comes out of his yard after a major repair, he walks its deck alone before returning it to the owner. He stops at the bow, faces upstream, and stands in silence for approximately thirty seconds. His sons know not to interrupt this. His sons do not know what it is. It is a dwarf mason's blessing adapted for water: the completed work being presented to its element, the craftsman confirming the work will hold. Ithraval and Marethis would both recognise it. Orven would not use either name. He would say he's checking the trim. The trim is always fine. He already checked the trim. --- **Renn Ashford** — Crosses the bridge and touches the upstream axe-head. Every time, without slowing, without looking, without appearing to notice she's done it. She is aware she does it. She mentioned this, once, to her field log: *"noted that I continue to perform the upstream-niche contact before field departures. Concluded it costs nothing and the data on field outcomes is inconclusive."* She is the most empirical person in Reedford. She has collected three years of anomaly data. She cannot prove the touch does anything. She also cannot prove it doesn't, and the asymmetry of risk is unfavorable. She does not touch the downstream niche. She considers returning from an operation its own evidence. --- **Daven Thatch** — Uses "don't dam the Ivora" in its most literal sense and its most metaphorical sense with equal fluency. He is the single character in Reedford most likely to use folk-religious language consciously, because the Stevedores' Association's informal culture has preserved the river idiom more completely than the merchant or noble stratum. His people work the quay. The quay is the river. You know the river or you don't last. He pours water before weighing. He uses the Rootday quay location for the Association's most significant agreements. He says *"good water and a clean run"* to stevedores starting a difficult shift. He does not think of any of this as religious. He thinks of it as proper. The distinction, in Daven's worldview, is not significant. --- **Tessra** — Does not touch either bridge niche. Does not pour water. Does not say "good water." Has removed all pre-Faith practice from her personal life with the quiet completeness of someone who decided, at some point, that if the river kept her uncle then she would not feed it anything of herself. The exception: the plaque. She maintains it. She replaces the candle beneath it. She does not look at it. But she knows where her father carved the Vaedoris knot, and on the Day of Broken Hooves each year she touches it — just once, when the Scale and Anchor is empty before opening — and says nothing and opens the shutters and starts the fire and does not speak of it to anyone. --- ## PART VI: THREAD ANOMALIES AND FOLK INTERPRETATION The Thread anomalies Ashford has been documenting — three events in sixty days, the grey-water disturbances in the reed-banks — are being interpreted by the river-working community through the lens of the folk practice described above. This is the GM's understanding; it is not information the players have access to unless they engage closely with the community. The stevedores, pilots, and Reed Quarter families have no theoretical framework for what's happening. What they have is a feeling: *the Ivora is wrong.* And wrong rivers, in the tradition of every water-working community that has ever invoked Mirethis or Yltheris, mean something has been withheld, broken, or permitted to fester. **Phrases in circulation, quietly:** - *"The current's running angry."* — The Ivora's character has changed since the anomalies began. Pilots feel it in the tiller. - *"Something upstream of us."* — A statement of uncertain geography. Nobody knows what is upstream of the anomaly. But the cause is upstream, not in the river itself. - *"The water's not finding its level."* — Maret's phrase, but now used by people who've never spoken to her. It has escaped its source and become the community's way of saying: *something has disrupted the order that was there before.* These phrases will mean something different to a player character with Water Pantheon knowledge. They are not metaphors. They are accurate descriptions of a river that is carrying Mirethis-flavoured corruption upstream from the Thread instability, filtered through a folk tradition that has the right vocabulary and the wrong confidence about what it's describing. --- *GM-only document.
