The Vaelthir Crown: An Imperial Study on the World Tree and the Greenwood

Filed by Legate–Scholar Marcion Vhailen, Collegium Archivum, Seventh Office of Strategic Ecclesia
Year 487 of the Fourth Age – Under Imperial Seal of the Kel Varyan Senate


---

I. Preface and Classification Notice

Clearance Level: Sigma–Primus
Distribution: Restricted to members of the Ecclesiarchal Collegium, the Office of Strategic Theology, and the Imperial Senate Sub-Committee on Frontier Anomalies.
Summary: This document collates five months of observation, cartographic survey, and ethnographic record concerning the forest realm known as Talanthar, its central phenomenon — the World Tree Vaelthir — and the socioreligious system that sustains both.

> Previous expeditions to the Greenwood Frontier (Ref: Cases 472-481 F.A.) resulted in twenty-three fatalities, seven disappearances, and one confirmed conversion. This report seeks to establish factual baselines free from mythic distortion, though the author concedes such separation may ultimately prove impossible.




---

II. Mandate and Purpose

By writ of the Imperial Senate, I, Legate–Scholar Marcion Vhailen, was charged to:

1. Map the eastern marches beyond Rivenscar and determine viable ingress routes into the Greenwood.


2. Investigate the material nature of the entity known locally as Vaelthir, reported to exhibit both botanical and arcane sentience.


3. Assess the sociopolitical organization of Talanthar, its population distribution, and military capability.


4. Identify causes of repeated Corvessan military failure and evaluate any potential threat to Imperial interests.



Initial assumptions classified Talanthar as a confederation of druidic cults and halfling nomads occupying ungoverned woodland.
All such assumptions were found inaccurate within the first week of observation.


---

III. Geographical Survey

1. Macro-Region

The Greenwood Sovereignty occupies the north-eastern corridor of Elarion, bounded west by the Kingdom of Corvessa, east by the Skyspine Mountains, and south by the Ivora tributaries that vanish beneath the hills.
It covers approximately thirty-five thousand square miles of contiguous woodland, ravine, and marsh — a territory smaller than Corvessa yet infinitely harder to traverse.

The forest canopy averages two hundred feet in height; in the oldest sectors it exceeds three hundred.
Light filters in muted shafts, giving the entire region a perpetual dusk.
The air is heavy with spores, dew, and a low electrical hum that sensitive augurs describe as a living resonance.

2. Hydrography

Two rivers dominate the landscape:

The Vaelrun, descending from the Skyspine glaciers, narrow and violently swift.

The Thalaris, broader, meandering through limestone before plunging underground.


Their confluence carves the Vaelthir Gorge, a mile-wide cleft whose vertical walls rise near five hundred feet.
Here the rivers entwine, half visible, half subterranean, feeding cascades that echo like distant thunder.
It is within this gorge that the City of Talanthar has taken root — quite literally.

3. Subterranean Topography

Below the visible forest lies an underworld of caverns known collectively as the Underbloom.
These chambers range from narrow crawlways to vaults vast enough to contain entire villages.
Bioluminescent flora — glowmoss, lich-orchids, and silver-cap fungi — illuminate the depths in soft hues of blue and green.
Rivers split and merge through these hollows, occasionally surfacing in shafts of daylight like veins of liquid glass.

Seismic readings (Ref: Glyph-Probe #3) suggest the caverns form a continuous network extending beneath the Thornward Flats and possibly into Corvessan territory.
Whether this is natural erosion or the effect of Vaelthir’s roots remains unconfirmed.


---

IV. The City of Talanthar (Vael’Tharyn)

1. General Disposition

The so-called city is less an urban construct than a vertical ecosystem.
Terraces, bridges, and platforms spiral along the gorge walls; residences grow from living wood coaxed into shape by druidic art.
Below, the river tunnels are lined with carved crannogs — half-stone, half-root — joined by bridges that sway yet never fall.
Above, aerial walkways thread through the upper canopy, linking cliff-perched towers to colossal trees whose trunks serve as halls, archives, and temples.

At night, the entire gorge glows faintly — not by flame, but by the slow respiration of luminous vines.
The effect is breathtaking, and profoundly disquieting: the sense that the city itself is awake.

2. Key Districts

The High Crown: Upper terraces surrounding the base of the World Tree Vaelthir.  Home to the Archdruidic conclave, sacred pools, and living monuments of bark and crystal.  Access restricted; even most citizens enter only for ritual.

Silver Hollow: Mid-level caverns housing forges and laboratories.  Here Magi-smiths alloy imported metals with sapstone, producing weapons that hum with low vitality.

Bridge-Courts: Markets suspended between cliffs.  Traders exchange herbs, pelts, and carved bone for smelted goods brought by dwarven or Imperial intermediaries.

Skyperch Aeries: Upper rim towers and landing platforms for griffon and elk-wing riders.  Defensive patrols launch here, capable of reaching the forest edge within an hour.

Shadow Pools: Lower caverns of dark water and fungal canopy where witch-covens commune with reflected spirits.  The atmosphere is one of serene menace — beauty too still to be benign.


3. Population and Structure

Reliable counts are impossible.  Estimates range from eighty to one hundred thousand permanent residents within the gorge, with perhaps four times that number dispersed through outlying hamlets and groves.
Society divides into Circles rather than classes — Druids, Rangers, Magi, Witches, and Shamanic lineages — each with representation in the High Council of the Greenwood.
No monarch presides; authority rotates with the seasons, granted by ritual consensus rather than decree.

4. Material Economy

The city’s economy functions on symbiosis rather than extraction.
Timber is never felled without replacement; food comes from terraced root-gardens and river fisheries; metal is scarce and revered.
Trade caravans from dwarven holds bring iron, tin, and salt in exchange for medicinal resin, enchanted fabrics, and dream-sap (a controlled narcotic distilled from glowberries).
Coinage exists but is secondary to barter and gift-debt, the latter enforced by magical compacts.


---

V. The Outlying Hamlets and Groves

The Greenwood beyond the gorge teems with small, semi-nomadic settlements — clusters of huts around standing stones, suspended dwellings in the boughs, or subterranean grotto-villages beside hidden streams.
Paths between them shift like living veins: roads migrate, overgrowth conceals yesterday’s trail, yet the locals navigate unerringly.

A few observations of note:

Halfling Joy-Clans: Tribal bands of raptor-riders and elk-hunters.  Their laughter precedes ambush or feast with equal likelihood.  Feared and adored by the forest’s other denizens.

Druidic Groves: Each major Circle maintains a sacred clearing, marked by rune-stones that bloom when the moon is high.  These groves act as both temple and council chamber.

The Wander Inns: Rare wayhouses that appear to move.  My guide swore that a tavern called The Resting Root had stood in four different locations along the same trail within a month.  The proprietor, unchanged, claimed it was the road that moved, not he.

**Ash-Marked Groves**: Descendants of Islands of Eternal Green refugees maintain 
~18 settlements concentrated along borders, particularly Thornward frontier. 
Distinguished by astronomical observatories and memorial traditions. Notable 
settlements include Aeloren's Grove (southern interior cultural center), Ashwatch 
(Thornward primary defensive position), and Skywatch Archive (eastern intelligence 
post). Fully integrated into Talanthari governance but maintain distinct 
sub-cultural identity honoring refugee heritage.


These hamlets form the connective tissue of Talanthar’s society — simultaneously isolated and unified through the forest’s unseen communication.
Messengers claim the trees themselves carry whispers: news, warnings, and songs.
Augur-crystals confirm minute shifts in aetheric resonance across miles whenever ritual drums sound within any grove.


---

VI. Environmental Observations

Seasonal change in the Greenwood differs from the rest of Elarion.
Winters remain mild but bright, with the canopy reflecting snowlight like silver glass; summers plunge into emerald twilight as leaves thicken to near opacity.
Rainfall is constant yet rarely violent — it filters through layers of foliage in a whispering drizzle the locals call the forest’s breath.
Lightning never strikes the same tree twice here; instead it is diverted along invisible wards, a phenomenon that defies arcane explanation.

Flora and fauna exhibit pronounced sympathetic magic.
Predators strike only when the forest hungers; disease seldom spreads beyond a single host before fading into spores.
Scholars of the Collegium may interpret this as extreme ecological balance; I, having walked those paths, suspect willful intent.

Part II – The Vaelthir World Tree, the Old Forest Spirit, and the Blood Covenant


---

I. First Observation and Initial Hypotheses

The World Tree Vaelthir dominates both the skyline and the metaphysics of Talanthar.
From a distance it appears to pierce the very cloud-line, yet its visible trunk accounts for barely a fraction of its true form; augur-soundings detect living matter descending at least a mile into the substrata, entwined with the rivers and cave systems described in Part I.

Vaelthir is not, by any measurable category, a mere organism.
Arcane resonance tests show pulses identical to Weave activity recorded in Imperial sanctums — but inverted, as if the Weave here flows from the material rather than into it.
The phenomenon might be described as self-generating divinity: life creating magic, not magic sustaining life.

Physical Characteristics

Height: Approx. 2 400 ft from root base to upper canopy.

Crown Span: Nearly three miles diameter.

Bark: Silvery-green, layered with translucent resin; inner striations glow faintly with the lunar cycle.

Leaves: Broad, semi-luminous, turning argent under starlight; fall only once every nine years.

Roots: Form the foundations of the High Crown terraces; many double as bridges or aqueducts.


Lightning strikes disperse harmlessly through its surface, conducted down into the riverbed where bursts of phosphorescence follow seconds later — suggesting an internal conduit system.


---

II. Local Doctrine and Mythic Origin

Elvish oral records claim Vaelthir was “the first breath drawn after the gods ceased singing.”
According to the Canticle of Roots, the world was once bound by three great Trees — one for air, one for stone, and one for water.
Vaelthir, the third, anchored the flow between them.
When the others fell during the “Severing of the Weave,” Vaelthir alone endured, sustaining what balance remained.

Priests of the Faith of the Bound Pattern denounce this tale as heretical inversion: where the Pattern teaches order from the divine downward, the Greenwood teaches creation from the earth upward.
The political implications are severe; to accept Vaelthir’s myth is to accept that divinity can arise without sanction — an existential threat to the Imperial ecclesia.


---

III. Observable Phenomena

During the equinox vigil (Month of Reaping, F.A. 486), I witnessed the so-called Rising Veil — a pulse of light ascending the trunk and rippling through the canopy.
All ambient magic within a mile went silent for the duration; even my glyph-quills ceased their hum.
Local druids describe this as the Tree “breathing the Weave back into rhythm.”

Fauna and flora respond in kind: leaves orient toward the trunk, rivers momentarily reverse eddies, and bioluminescent spores ignite across the forest floor like constellations.
If this is symbiotic illusion, it is executed with terrifying precision; if genuine, Vaelthir regulates an ecosystem the size of a small nation by will alone.


---

IV. The Old Forest Spirit

While Vaelthir is the visible heart, the Greenwood’s sentience manifests through a being referred to only as The Watcher in Roots or The Nameless Mediator.
The oldest records suggest it once arbitrated between mortals and gods during the age of chaos.
Now it rarely acts, existing as myth and presence — a neutral guardian neither wholly benevolent nor malign.

During my third month, deep within the Shadow Pools, I experienced what guides termed a listening.
The air thickened; every drop of water hung motionless.
In that stillness came a sound — not voice, but the pressure of words forming behind the skull.
The message, though fragmentary, conveyed intent: “Remember balance, lest blood drown the roots.”
No magic known to the Empire can fabricate such a phenomenon across witnesses.

Whether this entity and Vaelthir are distinct or aspects of one intelligence remains unresolved.
Druids insist there is no separation: “The Tree dreams, and the Forest remembers.”


---

V. The Blood Covenant

1. Ritual Practice

Every child born within Talanthar is introduced to the forest through blood.
A shallow cut is made upon each ear, a single drop offered to the soil alongside the parents’ own.
This act, called the First Knowing, binds the newborn to the Greenwood’s memory.
From that day onward, the forest recognises them; those who refuse the rite are said to vanish within weeks, “forgotten by the roots.”

At adulthood, a second offering — The Second Knowing — reaffirms the bond.
Blood and sap mingle in carved basins at the base of living stones, then are absorbed by the earth.
These ceremonies are communal, joyous, and utterly alien to Imperial theology, which forbids blood-magic as sacrilege.

2. Magical Function

Empirical analysis (sample Ref #12-Vael) shows trace aetheric signatures within the soil containing residual life-essence, confirming that blood is metabolised by microbial flora linked to Vaelthir’s root network.
In effect, each offering becomes data written into a living archive — a biological memory system of staggering complexity.

When a Talani dies, their body is not buried but planted: roots weave through the flesh within hours, leaving no corpse.
The locals claim the person’s memories return to Vaelthir.
I dismissed this until encountering a druid who recited, unprompted, the personal verses of a soldier I had known fifteen years earlier on the Corvessan frontier.
That soldier’s unit perished in the Thornward Flats Disaster.

3. Imperial Theological Reaction

The Collegium brands the practice The Heresy of the Living Pattern.
If the Weave can absorb mortal memory, then immortality lies not in divine grace but in symbiotic surrender — an idea both intoxicating and catastrophic to the Faith.
Petitions have circulated for an Inquisitorial Expedition, though wiser senators recall the fate of Corvessa’s crusades.


---

VI. Vaelthir and the Old Forest as System

From ecological data, it is evident that Vaelthir’s roots distribute nutrients, water, and aetheric energy across the Greenwood, functioning as both circulatory and magical network.
Deforestation anywhere within fifty miles results in immediate regrowth; fires extinguish themselves as if suffocated.
This implies a centralised control system — a planetary nervous structure operating on both biological and arcane principles.

To the inhabitants, this is simply “the forest’s will.”
They do not build against it but within it.
Their architecture bends rather than cuts; their agriculture nurtures rather than replaces.
Even mining — limited to riverstone and bioluminescent crystal — proceeds under ritual sanction.

Corvessan chroniclers call this stagnation: a people frozen in reverence.
Yet observing them, I find it more accurate to term it homeostasis: a civilisation that has achieved balance so perfect it appears inert to those obsessed with growth.


---

VII. Psychological and Cultural Impact

The Blood Covenant shapes every social interaction.
Contracts are sealed by mixing drops of blood with sap on parchment-leaf scrolls; marriages involve entwined cuts upon joined palms; justice rituals force oath-breakers to bleed into stone — if the forest rejects the offering, the wound never closes.

There are no prisons in Talanthar.
Exile is the only punishment, which locals equate to death, for the forest erases the exiled from memory.
I questioned several outsiders living near the borders; none could recall the names of those exiled even when shown sketches.
Either mass suggestion or metaphysical excision is at work.

This omnipresent communion breeds serenity among citizens and dread among foreigners.
The Talani smile often, but rarely laugh.
Their songs echo through the trees in polyphonic whispers, voices overlapping like leaves in wind — beautiful, mournful, and unnervingly unified.


---

VIII. Imperial Scientific Recommendations

1. Arcane Monitoring: Establish stationary scry-beacons along the Rivenscar line to record Weave fluctuations correlated with Vaelthir’s pulses.


2. Biological Sampling: Secure controlled specimens of root tissue and sap; current samples lose vitality within hours outside forest boundaries.


3. Theological Containment: Prohibit civilian pilgrims; suppress dissemination of Greenwood hymns within Imperial borders (they induce trance-states).


4. Diplomatic Approach: Engage through dwarven intermediaries; direct Imperial envoys risk absorption — literal or ideological.

Part III – The Corvessan Conflict, Cultural Contrasts, and Trade Dynamics


---

I. Overview

The relationship between Corvessa and Talanthar has been defined by contradiction: proximity without comprehension, hostility tempered by reluctant fascination.
Corvessa, proud heir to river-knighthood and the Faith of the Bound Pattern, regards the Greenwood as an affront to civilisation itself — a heretical wilderness that refuses the proper order of creation.
To the Talani, Corvessa embodies the disease of conquest: iron carving through living wood, law written without listening.

For nearly a millennium, these opposing worldviews have collided in ritualised cycles of invasion and humiliation, the scars of which still shape the frontier today.


---

II. Early Encounters: The Age of Petty Crowns (Pre-Imperial, ~900–700 F.A.)

Before either polity consolidated, Corvessa’s river-chiefs pushed eastward seeking timber and hunting grounds.
These early expeditions vanished almost without trace.
Only a single recovered journal survives — the Chronicle of Ser Tares the Younger — which records:

> “The woods do not yield their paths; by noon the road is new, by dusk our names unmade.”



When the first fortified crossings appeared, the Greenwood responded not with armies but with silence: bridges collapsed overnight, crops failed, and horses refused to approach the treeline.
Corvessa interpreted this as sabotage, not sorcery, and doubled its effort — thereby feeding the legend of the Cursed March that endures to this day.


---

III. The Crusades of the Pattern (Third Age, 312–360 F.A.)

With the codification of the Faith of the Bound Pattern, the Corvessan Crown declared the Greenwood a spiritual infection requiring purgation.
Three major crusades were launched within five decades:

1. The First Ride of Purity (312–314 F.A.) – Ten thousand horse and levy crossed the Thornward Flats under banners of white flame. None returned save two heralds whose skin bore living vine scars that never healed.


2. The Second Ride of Binding (333–334 F.A.) – A smaller, more disciplined force commanded by Saint-General Virel Marrow.  It reached the Vaelrun ford before being encircled in mists that lasted six days.  When the fog cleared, the army’s standards hung from the trees upside-down, the men gone.  Corvessa canonised Marrow as martyr; Talanthar remembers him as The Weeping Steel.


3. The Third Ride of Order (359–360 F.A.) – Jointly sanctioned by the Kel Varyan Ecclesia before Corvessa became a client kingdom.  This expedition was annihilated at what the elves call The Battle of Singing Thorns: vines reportedly erupted mid-charge, impaling cavalry.  Surviving accounts describe music — the forest humming in time with the slaughter.



These disasters forged Corvessa’s self-image as both victim and crusader.  The Pattern demanded perseverance; honour demanded revenge.


---

IV. The Thornward Flats Disaster (Fourth Age, 482 F.A.)

The culmination of centuries of zeal came in the so-called Holy Campaign of the River Lances, an attempt to permanently subdue Talanthar with Imperial blessing.
Nearly half of Corvessa’s nobility rode that season — nine thousand lancers, a thousand clerics, and innumerable squires and servants.
They expected triumph; they found annihilation.

Rain turned the Flats to mire, arrows fell like sleet, and the forest itself advanced — roots splitting armour, mud swallowing horses whole.
Talanthar’s response was surgical: rangers harried supply lines, witches inverted light, druids drowned the flanks with summoned floods.
When retreat came, halfling joy-tribes hunted the survivors in what they call The Great Chase, each slain knight a trophy and an offering of laughter to the forest.

Within five days the campaign ceased to exist.
Corvessa’s pride broke with its lances; its king aged ten years in mourning.
The Empire took note.


---

V. Imperial Observation: The Border Skirmish of 486 F.A.

Four years later, the Kel Varyan Empire conducted a limited incursion under guise of mapping.
Two cohorts of the 14th Legion entered the eastern marches with orders of reconnaissance and demonstration of strength.
They faced no open resistance — only ambush, misdirection, and weather turning hostile beyond natural measure.
Entire units wandered into ravines unseen on Imperial maps.
After forty-eight hours, command collapsed.
When extraction came, fewer than half returned.

Crucially, analysis showed the legionaries were not slain en masse; they were separated and neutralised piecemeal by small, expert teams.
Corvessa’s generals had called the forest sorcery.
Imperial tacticians now called it superior adaptation.


---

VI. Comparative Culture and Philosophy

1. The Corvessan Ideal: Order Through Pattern

Corvessa defines virtue as structure.
The river is measured, the field furrowed, the bridge sanctified.
To live rightly is to impose form upon the chaos of nature.
Their Faith preaches that the Weave is a loom, each life a thread in divine design; deviation is heresy.
Art glorifies geometry: mosaics of symmetry, hymns in perfect metre, vineyards in ranked rows.
Their knights see themselves as living extensions of law.

2. The Talani Ideal: Harmony Through Yielding

Talanthar inverts that creed.
The Greenwood teaches that survival lies in bending, not breaking.
Where Corvessa builds walls, Talanthar shapes trees; where the Faith demands confession, the druids demand listening.
Their language even lacks a word for possession in the legal sense — land is spoken of as “walked by” or “sung within.”
The people value patience, memory, and the slow cycle of return.
Change is not progress but renewal.

3. Resulting Miscomprehension

To Corvessa, such fluidity is barbarism — a rejection of destiny.
To Talanthar, Corvessa’s order is death without decay.
Each sees in the other its antithesis, and therefore proof of its own righteousness.
Thus even peace between them is merely a season between storms.


---

VII. Trade and Mutual Necessity

Despite centuries of hatred, the two realms remain entangled by necessity.

1. Smuggled Commerce

Iron, salt, and parchment flow eastward in secret caravans escorted by dwarven intermediaries from the western holds.
In return come rare resins, moonwood, and healing tinctures of forest origin that fetch fortunes in Corvessa’s apothecaries.
Officially both crowns deny such trade; unofficially both rely on it.

2. Ritualised Exchange

Every midsummer a neutral market known as the Reed Fair assembles on the border at the River Velis.
Talanthar sends witches and traders draped in living moss; Corvessa sends merchant-priests under white banners.
Goods are bartered in silence, each side leaving items upon woven mats and retreating ten paces.
At dusk the mats are removed; no tally is kept, no words spoken.
The arrangement has held for centuries.

Imperial observers note that no violence has ever marred the Reed Fair — an inexplicable oasis of civility amid perpetual hostility.

3. Influence and Contagion

Corvessan artisans now imitate Talani organic motifs — vine-filigree on armour, flowing lines in architecture — claiming aesthetic appeal while denying spiritual influence.
Likewise, certain Talani smiths experiment with imported alloys, forming stronger bindings for their living wood constructs.
Each culture infects the other while pretending purity.


---

VIII. The Psychological Frontier

Along the Thornward Flats now stands a belt of ruined forts and abandoned chapels.
Here Corvessan pilgrims still come, kneeling before broken lances set as crosses, whispering apologies to ghosts.
Across the river, Talani children play among the ivy-covered stones, unaware or uncaring of their former sanctity.
No clearer metaphor exists for the gulf between them: one mourns its defeat, the other forgets there was ever a war.

The forest does not hate.
It simply absorbs.


---

IX. Imperial Assessment

1. Strategic Analysis

Corvessa’s failures stem not from lack of courage but from doctrinal rigidity.
Their armies cannot adapt to an environment that shifts as a sentient organism.
Imperial legions, though disciplined, would fare little better in direct engagement.
Only decentralised tactics — small units operating with autonomy and magical camouflage — show promise, as evidenced by the limited survivals of the 14th Legion recon.

2. Political Utility

Talanthar’s continued independence serves the Empire’s interest by keeping Corvessa humble and dependent.
So long as the Greenwood endures, Corvessa’s martial pride remains checked; its requests for Imperial engineers and diviners ensure loyalty.
Thus, paradoxically, the unconquerable forest is a tool of Imperial balance.

3. Cultural Opportunity

There is scholarly value in the Greenwood’s symbiosis.
Its blood-bound communion offers insights into self-sustaining magic systems.
Were its principles adapted under controlled conditions, the Empire could achieve environmental harmony without heresy — a sanitised symbiosis.
However, any direct study risks moral and metaphysical contamination.


---

X. Conclusions of the Legate

After six months of comparative observation, I submit the following propositions:

1. Talanthar is not primitive.
It is an alternate civilisation that has achieved ecological equilibrium at the cost of expansion.


2. Corvessa’s crusades failed not against nature, but against patience.
The forest waits; armies do not.


3. Vaelthir represents a living axis of Weave flow distinct from divine or mortal governance.
Suppression is impossible; coexistence may be the wiser heresy.


4. The Empire must study, not conquer.
Where Corvessa sought dominion, we should seek understanding — quietly, with humility.



I am aware these recommendations will displease both the Faith and the General Staff.
Yet to ignore them is to repeat Corvessa’s folly on an imperial scale.

Part IV – The Grand Crusade and the Living Hostility

An Imperial Theological and Cultural Analysis of Corvessa’s Obsession with the Vaelthir Tree and Its Consequences for the Greenwood Sovereignty


---

I. Introduction: The Shadow of an Unending War

Every age of Corvessan history has worn the same wound.
Their knights no longer bear the pure white flame upon their shields, and their priests no longer anoint crusaders for the “Cleansing of the Greenwood,” yet in every sermon, every child’s rhyme, every courtly oath of devotion — the shadow of the Grand Crusade endures.

The Faith of the Bound Pattern officially repealed the Rite of Purgation four centuries ago.
Even so, the nobility, the military orders, and the common folk alike still speak of the “unfinished war” against Talanthar as though it were a holy obligation deferred by circumstance, not revoked by doctrine.

To Corvessa, the World Tree Vaelthir is no longer just heresy — it is proof of their failure to perfect creation.
They are a kingdom haunted by the one stain upon their divine geometry: the living spiral of the forest that refuses to submit.


---

II. The Grand Crusade: Myth, Memory, and Compulsion

1. The Origin of the Creed of Purity

According to the older codices of the Faith, all creation is a tapestry of divine law — every river, every life-thread part of a pattern too vast for mortal eyes.
Corvessa’s early theologians taught that imperfection is rebellion and rebellion is disease.
The Greenwood, shifting and self-repairing, was the visible embodiment of that imperfection.
To conquer it was to cure the world.

When Vaelthir was first encountered, priests declared it a blasphemy of recursion: a tree feeding upon itself through the blood of its worshippers, stealing divine authority to shape life.
Even after the Faith’s central hierarchy softened its interpretation, Corvessa’s clergy refused to yield.
They codified the Creed of the Fell Root, binding every noble house to vow that the Greenwood would one day burn.

2. The “Eternal Muster”

Even now, each spring, every noble barony in Corvessa performs the Ceremony of the Unbroken Muster:
lances are raised, names of fallen crusaders recited, and mock charges executed across ploughed fields.
It is pageantry and theatre — yet also liturgy.
The rite ensures the memory of the lost sons and the fantasy of redemption never fade.

Children grow up hearing:

> “When the Pattern is pure, the Forest shall fall.”



The act of keeping the crusade alive has become the glue of Corvessan identity.
Without the Greenwood as the enemy, they would have no measure of righteousness, no mirror for their discipline.


---

III. Theological Divergence: Pattern vs. Cycle

1. Corvessan Doctrine

The Faith of the Pattern teaches that the gods are weavers, and mortals the threads.
Perfection comes through stillness, obedience, and completion — the fixed design.
Death is the final stitch; chaos the unraveling.

Thus, the Greenwood — growing, shifting, constantly rewriting itself — is spiritual anarchy.
It denies the finality of death and mocks the perfection of divine architecture.

2. Talani Doctrine

The druids and witches of Talanthar see life as an endless song.
Every leaf falls to feed the root; every death a verse that gives the next life its rhythm.
To stop the cycle is the true heresy.
To fix the Weave into a static design is to kill the song.

To them, the World Tree Vaelthir is not a god but a memory of creation itself — a living recording of all growth and loss since the dawn.
They say, “The Tree remembers,” and that when a drop of blood or tears falls upon its roots, the forest knows your name forever.

3. The Irreconcilable Divide

What Corvessa calls “salvation,” Talanthar calls “murder.”
What Talanthar calls “balance,” Corvessa calls “blasphemy.”
No treaty can bridge such faiths because each is not merely a belief — it is a definition of reality.
Thus the war cannot truly end; the enemy is not the other kingdom, but the existence of the other worldview.


---

IV. The Forest’s Memory and the Talani Hostility

Talanthar remembers.

The Greenwood never forgets the ashes and prayers cast into its soil.
In the deepest groves, the spirits of those slain during the Crusades still whisper in the bark.
The halfling tribes sing of the “Iron Feast,” when captured knights were roasted in their own armour as offerings to the Old Wood.
The moon elves tell tales of “Silver Blood,” the streams that ran red after the Lances fell.

To this day, any Corvessan entering the Thornward border uninvited finds paths twisting, food spoiling, and animals fleeing.
Even those who mean no harm are treated with wary contempt.
No Talanthari forget that it was Corvessa who sought to cut the heart out of the world.

1. State Policy of Controlled Isolation

Despite hostility, Talanthar rarely pursues revenge beyond its borders.
Its High Council maintains a doctrine called “Rooted Stillness” — aggression is only sanctioned when the forest itself is threatened.
However, this restraint should not be mistaken for weakness.
When provoked, the Greenwood answers as one organism: rangers, beasts, spirits, and the land itself move in harmony.

2. Corvessan Intrusions

Still, Corvessa’s frontier nobles continue to send skirmishers, relic-hunters, and zealots into the woods, seeking to “redeem” ancestral failures or to find “proof” of Vaelthir’s corruption.
Each expedition vanishes, or returns half-mad, whispering of roots that drink blood and faces in the fog.
The Faith does not sanction these missions — but neither does it forbid them.
They are tolerated, even quietly celebrated.
The Crusade that never ends has simply become a cultural instinct.

V. Geography of Division

1. The Thornward Flats

Once a fertile plain of trade and migration, now it is a cursed frontier.
Blackened trees, vine-choked ruins, and shallow marshes filled with broken helms and rusting banners.
From the Corvessan side, the horizon appears misted — yet scouts report the fog never crosses the border into Corvessan lands.
It waits, like a living sentinel.

Talanthar’s side is eerily alive: green, bright, yet oppressive.
Flowers bloom from skulls; roots form bridges over forgotten roads.
The land itself has become a monument to balance restored through violence.

2. Fortifications and Watch Points

Corvessa maintains six active forts and dozens of ruined chapels along the border.
The Faith Guard performs nightly vigils, ringing bells to “keep the forest awake.”
In Talanthar, griffin towers perch upon cliffs overlooking these same valleys, their riders watching in silence.
Neither side fires the first shot — both remember what happened when they did.

3. Trade Corridors and Taverns of Shifting Roads

Between the outer hamlets of Talanthar and Corvessa’s reed villages lie the ghost-roads — paths that alter course overnight.
Some lead to the same tavern every traveller swears is elsewhere.
Others end in pools reflecting stars not yet risen.
These liminal routes act as neutral ground: smuggling trails, meeting places, sometimes graveyards for the lost.

VII. The Modern State of Affairs (F.A. 487)

1. Corvessa’s Obsession Persists

The Grand Crusade has become a matter of politics as much as piety.
Every monarch promises “preparation for the Final Cleansing” as a tool to rally nobles and divert unrest.
The Faith avoids endorsing it yet tolerates the language to maintain influence.
Young knights train in “Forest Doctrine” — the art of fighting in terrain none have ever mastered — and every generation dreams of being the one to “fell the heretical tree.”

Behind the oaths and oratory, the truth remains: they do not seek conquest, but atonement.
They wish to erase the reminder of failure that grows green beyond their reach.

2. Talanthar’s Eternal Vigilance

Talanthar’s high council remains patient but unyielding.
They have seen the crusade come a dozen times before, and they know it will come again.
Scouts maintain outposts hidden in the canopy and caves.
The forest is their fortress; time their ally.
The halfling tribes, still laughing, sharpen knives and polish trophies of gold and bone.
The old magi whisper: “The next charge will feed the roots anew.”

3. Imperial Observation

The Empire notes this balance as both curse and blessing.
Corvessa’s zeal keeps its focus eastward, leaving its southern flank compliant and dependent.
Talanthar’s isolation ensures it will never become an aggressive regional power.
Thus, the unending war that neither side can win has become the very mechanism that preserves the Imperial peace.

I. Key Geographic and Sacred Sites of Talanthar

1. The City of Talanthar Proper

The twin rivers Aerendel and Myrn carved a deep gorge where they meet, a labyrinth of terraces, stalactite bridges, and shimmering watercourses that form the beating heart of the Greenwood Federation.
Half above and half below the surface world, its districts cling to living stone; root-bridges span chasms where trees grow from rock and hang from ceilings.
Night glows with cultivated luminescence — glow-mushrooms, lich-lamps, and hanging gardens of light-berries.
No two streets remain the same shape for long: the rock shifts, guided by old runes and the will of Vaelthir itself.

2. Vaelthir, the World Tree

At the gorge’s center, rising through rock and light, stands the Vaelthir Crown — trunk thicker than the Imperial Forum, branches that pierce the clouds and roots that lace the Under-Grove.
Every birth, oath, and death within the Greenwood leaves a drop of blood at its base, so that “the forest may know the name.”
During the Corvessan Crusades, the bark absorbed the blood of thousands — the scar still gleams silver under moonlight.

3. The Under-Groves and Cavern Settlements

Entire communities live beneath the ground where rivers run through colossal chambers.
Crannog fortresses rise from underground lakes; rope bridges span sinkholes large enough to swallow castles.
Mushroom forests, pallid willows, and silver-rooted birches form subterranean biomes of their own.
These places hum with quiet song — the breath of the forest’s spirit moving through stone.

4. The Thornward Flats

The eastern frontier, scarred by centuries of invasion.
A plain of mud, thorns, and reeds that swallow cavalry whole.
Monuments of rusting helms mark where Corvessa’s finest Lances fell.
Talanthar’s druids allow no settlement here; the land itself is considered a living graveyard.
Ghost-lights drift over pools that once held the bodies of knights and raptors alike.

5. Skyperch Cliffs and Griffin Eyries

Jagged limestone ridges overlooking the border.
Home to the Skyguard Orders — aerial knights mounted on griffins and great eagles.
Signal-fires and runestone perches allow instantaneous warnings across hundreds of miles.
Legends tell that when the Corvessans advanced, griffins dropped molten resin and burning sap upon their ranks.

6. Moonveil Glades

Misty forest valleys where witch covens and moon-elves dwell.
Here reality bends gently — paths repeat, moons double in reflection, and spirits dance in silver dew.
Imperial observers are forbidden entry; none who disobeyed have returned whole.

7. The Halfling Joy-Wilds

Stretching southward of the main forest, a web of rolling meadows and tangled copses where wild halfling tribes roam.
They live by hunt, feast, and song; their raptor-mounted warbands are beloved by Talanthar but feared by all others.
A tavern may appear for a week on a certain glade and vanish the next, yet always seem to stand exactly where weary travelers most need it.

II. Sites of Note in Corvessa Relevant to the Conflict

1. Rivenscar Fortress

Primary frontier citadel opposite the Thornward Flats.
Built of black stone quarried from the first Crusade’s ruins.
Its walls bear the carved names of every fallen knight of the Pattern; new names are added yearly despite no new wars officially declared.
The Faith’s bells toll nightly to “keep the forest awake.”

2. Cindraleth, the Paper City

Where the Codex of the Pure Pattern was written — a theological document still cited by zealots who demand the forest’s cleansing.
Ink-mills there grind vines into pulp as a symbolic domination of nature.
Talanthari scholars mock it as “the city that eats its own words.”

3. Bloodmartyr’s Crossing

Ruined triple bridge near the site of the Thornward Disaster.
Haunted by crimson mists; no Corvessan will cross it without prayer.
Pilgrims still visit to leave silver lilies for their ancestors — most vanish before dawn.

IV. Trade and Exchange (Official and Otherwise)

Though officially hostile, the Greenwood and the River Kingdom are economically interlocked through intermediaries and necessity.

1. Authorized Exchange via Imperial Brokerage

Metals & Tools: Mined in Corvessa’s western hills, smithed in Imperial ports, and traded under neutral seal to Talanthar.

Medicinals & Rare Herbs: Gathered by Talanthari druids, shipped through Imperial merchant guilds to Corvessa for apothecaries.

Spirits & Vintages: Corvessan wines exchanged for Greenwood meads, often via smugglers sanctioned after the fact.

Fine Art & Scripture: Corvessan illuminated manuscripts traded for Vaelthiri living-wood carvings coveted by Imperial collectors.


2. The Hidden Trade

Smuggling through the Myrn Caves remains common.
Ex-legionnaires run caravans of unmarked goods — iron, silk, even relics stolen from battlefields.
Some whisper that Corvessan blood-mages secretly seek Vaelthiri sap for forbidden rites of regeneration.

3. Toll Havens and Moving Taverns

Three neutral inns operate within shifting forest borders:

The Silver Stag: Appears during spring thaw; hosts treaties and assassinations alike.

Mother Glinna’s Cup: Run by a halfling matron; safest passage for Imperial traders if they bring sweet pastries.

The Red Root: Serves both sides but demands a blood tithe from all guests — a single drop for the forest’s memory.

VI. Current Intelligence Assessment

1. Imperial Strategic Value

Talanthar: Stable buffer; self-sufficient, rarely expansionist.

Corvessa: Loyal client but volatile; potential flashpoint if crusade rhetoric rekindles.
Imperial policy therefore favors status quo preservation.


2. Risks

Rise of zealot youth orders in Corvessa could ignite renewed holy war.

Increasing Talanthari cooperation with the Empire’s northern Loom Cities threatens Corvessan monopoly on river trade.

Forest anomalies spreading westward may destabilize the Thornward Flats frontier.

VII. Concluding Imperial Recommendation

The Empire should maintain:

1. Strict neutrality between both powers.


2. Encouragement of controlled trade through Imperial guilds.


3. Active intelligence on Corvessan noble musters and Faith rhetoric.


4. Limited cultural exchanges — music, art, and scholarship — to humanize the Greenwood without provoking zealotry.
