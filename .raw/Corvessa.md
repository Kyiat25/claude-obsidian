The Kingdom of Corvessa – Geographic & Demographic Survey

Year 487, Fourth Age
Prepared under Seal of the Crown and Second Estate


---

I. Territorial Expanse

Total Area: ~72,000 square miles (roughly the size of modern France’s southern half, or the Low Countries combined).

Borders:

North & East: The Greenwood/Talanthar frontier — a hostile, contested border of forest edge and marsh flats.

South: The Ivora Marches and the Imperial borderlands, open trade corridors and river crossings into the Empire proper.

West: Low hill ranges that slope down toward the sea; smaller freeholds and Imperial client ports.



Corvessa is a river kingdom, its lifeblood flowing from the Ivora River — a 900-mile waterway that begins in the foothills of the east, snakes through vine-draped valleys and fortified crossings, and empties into the coastal marshlands to the west. Every major town and castle sits either on the Ivora itself or on its tributaries.


---

II. Regional Geography

1. Upper Ivora Hills

Terrain: Terraced vineyards, rolling ridges, and noble estates.

Economy: Vineyards, saffron fields, and horse-breeding pastures.

Defenses: Hill-castles with commanding river views.

Culture: Nobility-heavy; famed for their most “pure” vintages and ancestral lances.


2. Middle Crossing Lands

Terrain: Wider valleys where tributaries join the Ivora.

Economy: Market towns, paper mills, smithies.

Defenses: Fortified bridges (often domed, stone-arched, or towered), toll stations, and road castles.

Culture: Merchant class flourishes here, balancing noble pride with Imperial trade.


3. Lower Ivora Flats

Terrain: Wide floodplains, reed banks, and rich soil.

Economy: Fisheries, mounted shellfish hunts, river grain, tuber farms.

Defenses: Dike-forts and river-watch towers.

Culture: More peasant and levy-heavy; a breadbasket region sustaining the nobility above.


4. The Western Marches

Terrain: Hills thick with goldwood and ironwood.

Economy: Timber, forestry, luxury crafts (paper, lacquered wood, armory inlays).

Defenses: Watch-forts along logging roads, warden orders guarding merchant convoys.

Culture: Pragmatic, less fanatical; known for strong ties with Imperial merchants.



---

III. Population

Total Population: ~3.2 million (one of the larger non-Loom City polities in Elarion).

Urban Population: ~480,000 (15%) in walled towns, bridge-cities, and noble fortresses.

Rural Population: ~2.7 million (85%) spread across villages, vineyards, forests, and river hamlets.


Breakdown by Class:

High Nobility (Royal line, dukes, marquises): ~1,200 individuals.

Lesser Nobility (knightly houses, landed barons): ~45,000 individuals.

Clergy of the Faith (priests, inquisitors, monasteries, theurges): ~160,000.

Commoners (farmers, vintners, herdsmen, hunters, fishermen): ~2.5 million.

Mercenaries, Foreign Traders, Artisans: ~80,000.



---

IV. Geophysical Features

Rivers: The Ivora is the great artery, with more than a dozen navigable tributaries. Barges, fishing fleets, and knight-bearing ferries dominate the water.

Forests: Heavily managed goldwood and ironwood groves — considered sacred resources. Unlike wild forests, these are pruned, patrolled, and turned into avenues for cavalry.

Hills & Valleys: Fertile loam ideal for vines, tubers, and millet. Hills make natural fortress sites; valleys funnel cavalry charges.

Climate: Warm temperate, akin to southern France and northern Iran — hot, dry summers and mild, rainy winters.

Natural Hazards: Spring floods along the Ivora; summer droughts; occasional pest blights in the vineyards.



---

V. Strategic & Cultural Geography

Military Corridors: Roads are deliberately cut wide and straight through the landscape, prioritizing cavalry maneuverability over trade.

Sacred Architecture:

Bridge-fortresses often double as shrines.

Hill-castles feature statues of saints of the Pattern, sometimes overlooking conquered villages.


Faith Geography: Shrines and statues mark every major river bend, declaring divine dominion over the land.



---

VI. Kingdom Character

Corvessa is a land of order imposed on nature: vineyards march in lines across hills, rivers are tamed with bridges and levees, forests are harvested in measured stands. Its geography mirrors its culture: rigid, martial, proud, and always straining to prove its purity against the “chaos” of Talanthar and the Old Forest.

Geography: A valley to the south-east, where an ancient Imperial road (paved in glyph-stone) branches off the main Ivora track.

Infrastructure:

Function: Moves grain, mercenaries, and scribal goods between Corvessa and the Marches. Less ceremonial, more commercial.

Dockyards and warehouses with mixed Imperial–Corvessan administration.

Function: Allows Imperial merchants to bypass the Middle Ivora entirely, importing wine and paper straight into the wider sea trade.

Corvessa & Talanthar – A State of Enmity


---

I. Historical Memory

Centuries of Clashes:
Corvessa has launched six major campaigns into the Greenwood (126, 298, 314, 403, 451, and 482), all of which ended in failure or stalemate.

The Breaking at Mossveil Gorge (298 4A): Still seen in Talanthar as their greatest triumph, in Corvessa as their greatest martyrdom.

Thornward Flats (482 4A): The freshest wound. Corvessa calls it the Martyrdom at Thornward, Talanthar mocks it as The Day of Laughing Shadows.



---

II. Political Relationship

Talanthar’s View:
Corvessa is an arrogant neighbour, blinded by Faith zeal, forever sending its nobles to die in predictable charges. They see Corvessa’s bridge-fortresses as scars in the land.

Corvessa’s View:
Talanthar is a heretic state, clinging to pagan rites around the World Tree of Vaelthir. Its druids, witches, and halfling raiders are seen as “forest vermin” rather than soldiers.

No Diplomacy:
There is no standing embassy, no trade, no treaties. The Greenwood is considered enemy land.

Permanent War-Readiness:
Border fortress of Rivenscar exists solely to watch Talanthar; the Faith blesses its garrison annually.



---

III. Religious Dimension

Corvessan Doctrine:

Talanthar’s faith is blood-tainted and wicked.

The World Tree of Vaelthir is seen as a “false idol” demanding sacrifices.

Every Corvessan knight’s axe is sworn to one day cut it down.


Talanthar Doctrine:

Corvessa’s Faith is a corruption of the Weave, forcing scripture upon a living cycle.

They mock Corvessa’s Codex as proof the Bound Pattern is a cage, not a weave.



This is not just political conflict — it is eschatological rivalry: each side believes the other embodies what must be destroyed for their own vision of the Weave to endure.


---

IV. Cultural Hostility

Songs & Propaganda:

Corvessan taverns sing of the Root Cutters — a prophesied charge that will finally fell the World Tree.

Talanthari bards recite the Four Failures (and now the Fifth at Thornward) to laughter and applause.


Education:

Corvessan children are taught from the Codex that elves, witches, and halflings are faithless tricksters.

Talanthar children are raised on tales of knights drowning in mud, mocked for their vanity.




---

V. Military Reality

Corvessa:

Heavy cavalry unmatched in armour, training, and noble devotion.

Doctrinal inflexibility makes them predictable in forested or broken terrain.


Talanthar:

Specialist small-unit orders (rangers, griffin riders, druids, witches, halflings).

Few in number, but never defeated in their own forest.



The result: stalemate through centuries. Corvessa cannot break into the Greenwood; Talanthar cannot survive a full plains campaign into the Ivora valley.


---

VI. Current Year (487, 4A)

Corvessa:
Still recovering from the Thornward disaster. Half of its knightly youth are dead, yet zeal remains unbroken. The Codex of the Pure Pattern frames Thornward as a test of faith, not a defeat.

Talanthar:
Emboldened, celebrating annual festivals of the Thornward victory. Preparing for the “Fifth Failure” to be written in Corvessan blood if they try again.

Empire’s View:

Keeps Corvessa as a client buffer state but sees their fanaticism as dangerous.

Tolerates Talanthar’s independence, preferring to avoid direct entanglement.




---

⚔️ In short:
Corvessa and Talanthar are locked in a holy war without peace, their borders bristling with fortresses and groves.
They do not talk. They do not trade. Every crossing of the Thornward Flats risks another bloodletting.

The Axe and the Vine – Corvessa’s Cultural Signature

Weapon of Honour

While most knightly cultures favour the sword, Corvessa’s symbol is the axe.
This choice is no accident — it is rooted in their vow to one day chop down Talanthar’s great forests, especially the World Tree they view as the heart of “pagan corruption.” Carrying an axe is both a martial choice and a political statement.

Forms:

Heavy cavalry carry long-hafted war axes designed for sweeping strikes from the saddle.

Foot knights and Faith Guard favour bearded axes for hooking shields and catching blades.

Ceremonial axes, richly gilded and engraved with Faith iconography, are used in religious processions and oaths of knighthood.


Symbolism:

The axe represents both war and cultivation — chopping down enemy forests and trimming the kingdom’s own prized groves.

In heraldry, crossed axes signify a knight who has sworn the Forest-Vow against Talanthar.




---

Cultivated Forests

Corvessa maintains some of the finest managed woodlands in Elarion, a mark of both wealth and national pride. These are not wild woods — they are pruned, ordered, and worked into the kingdom’s economy like vineyards.

Goldwood – prized for its pale, radiant grain; used in ceremonial furniture, parade armour inlays, and royal architecture.

Ironwood – dark, dense, and strong as steel; used for weapon hafts, shields, and bridge gates.

Vine-trimmed Groves – where rows of grapevines climb between carefully spaced trees, combining viticulture and forestry.



---

Economic Strength

Corvessa’s management of forest and vine fuels a prosperous artisan economy:

Paper – made from fine pulped goldwood and vine fibres, considered the best in the Empire for illuminated manuscripts and religious texts.

Wine – rich, aged in ironwood casks; known for deep colour and complex spice notes.

Carved Goods – goldwood and ironwood carving is a Corvessan specialty, often exported to Imperial courts.

Armaments – ironwood hafts for lances and axes are sought after across the continent.



---

Cultural Integration

This intertwining of axe and vine permeates Corvessan identity:

A knight might pledge to “harvest the heretic and the grape in the same season.”

Festivals pair wine-tasting with axe-throwing contests.

Religious processions bless both the cavalry’s axes and the year’s first barrels of wine.

Got it — here’s how the Prophecy of the Felled Root and the Four (now Five) Great Failures have shaped Corvessa’s culture over centuries, turning them into the kingdom you’re envisioning: proud, martial, devout to the point of fanaticism, and utterly unwilling to change.


---

I. Cultural Identity Forged in Defeat

Every failure against Talanthar has paradoxically strengthened Corvessan belief in their divine mission.

Defeat becomes proof of destiny — clergy teach that each loss is a “Season of Shadow” from the prophecy, and that the final, victorious charge is still to come.

The fallen are exalted as martyrs of the Pattern, their names inscribed in cathedral walls, knightly halls, and even on weapons.

Knights wear mourning crests into battle against Talanthar, symbolising vengeance owed to the dead.



---

II. The War Machine as a Cultural Ritual

Because the prophecy requires that the final battle be fought by the Hooves of the Faithful, heavy cavalry has become sacred:

The River Lances and other knightly orders aren’t just military — they are walking relics, living saints-in-waiting.

Even when proven vulnerable (Mossveil Gorge, Thornward Flats), the doctrine of the massed charge is preserved as holy writ.

Annual Lance Blessings in spring mark the start of the campaigning season, where priests consecrate armour and horses with patterned blood-runes.



---

III. Fanaticism in Faith

The Bound Pattern in Corvessa is interpreted through a lens of exclusive purity:

All other forms of worship are not just wrong — they are active heresies that must be destroyed.

Ancient rites like blood offerings to World Trees are considered wicked sorcery; Talanthar’s Great World Tree is painted as the greatest blight on the Pattern.

Clerics openly preach that the Weave itself calls Corvessa to uproot the Greenwood — and until that’s done, the world is unfinished.



---

IV. Social Stratification Through the Lance

Nobility & Knightly Class: All highborn youth are expected to serve in heavy cavalry; military failure is personal shame, victory is divine proof.

Second Estate (Faith): Paladin orders, clerical councils, and diviners act as both spiritual guides and military command — they decide which campaigns are “prophecy-aligned.”

Wizards & Theurges: Magic is respected if it supports the prophecy — especially divination, for “reading” the coming of the final battle. Arcane schools in Corvessa lean toward divination, abjuration, and battle magic.

Commonfolk: Levies and squires are drawn from lower ranks; service in campaigns against Talanthar earns social elevation.

Mercenaries: Viewed as useful but lesser; often brought in from Kel Varyan, northern continents, or other Loom-Cities to bolster numbers.



---

V. Festivals, Art, and Storytelling

The prophecy and defeats are omnipresent in daily life:

The Day of the Broken Hooves: Annual commemoration of Mossveil Gorge, marked with solemn processions and the recitation of names.

The Blood-Spire Plays: Passion plays dramatizing the eventual fall of the Great Root and the erection of the Pattern-Spire.

Public Statues: Monumental bronze works depict knights trampling roots, burning branches, and raising the Faith’s banners.

Songs & Oaths: Ballads like “Seven Days’ Ride” keep the vision of the final battle alive, sung in taverns and cathedrals alike.



---

VI. Political Consequences

The prophecy’s hold over the populace means:

Any monarch who does not pursue some form of campaign against Talanthar risks being branded faithless.

Even in peacetime, the military budget remains enormous — to keep the heavy cavalry in readiness for “the day the Root falls.”

Alliances are chosen based on whether they help or hinder the Greenwood Crusade.



---

VII. The Arrogance Core

Centuries of framing every war — even catastrophic losses — as divine steps toward a predestined victory have created a uniquely dangerous mindset:

They cannot accept reform — to change cavalry doctrine, faith structure, or prophecy interpretation would mean admitting the past centuries were wrong.

They see every battle as holy theatre — tactical adaptability is often sacrificed to make sure the charge is glorious.

They believe no one else understands the Pattern’s true will — even allies in the Faith of the Bound Pattern are seen as lesser interpreters.
