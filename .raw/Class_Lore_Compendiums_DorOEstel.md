# 1. BARBARIAN
## FURY'S CHILDREN: THOSE WHO REMEMBER THE FIRST RAGE

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Birth in Chaos**

Barbarians claim no divine patron for their rage—they embody something older. When Soltharion's first flames birthed the War of Elemental Becoming, certain mortals *resonated* with that primal fury. Not as worshippers, but as echoes. The first barbarians didn't learn their power—they *remembered* it from before their own consciousness, channeling the universe's violent birth directly through their flesh.

> *"The gods war. The elements clash. And we? We are the echo of that first collision, the scream that reality made when it learned to hurt."* — The Scar-Song, oldest orcish oral tradition

**First Age (~4,500-1,800 years ago) - The Rage Codified**

As the gods consolidated power and claimed creation, barbarians became living refutations of divine monopoly. Orcs developed **Scar-Law**, where rage became ritualized testament—each scar a word in a language older than speech. Goliaths in the Skyholds learned to channel storm-fury directly, their rage becoming weather itself.

The elven **Woven Reverence** practitioners viewed barbarian rage with suspicion—here was raw force that bypassed the careful structures of the Weave. Yet even they couldn't deny that barbarian fury seemed to *stabilize* wild magic zones, as if rage burned clean what careful working might corrupt.

**Second Age (1,800-1,100 years ago) - Rage as Rebellion**

During the age of heroes and rebellion, barbarians found themselves at the forefront of mortal uprising. The **Unleashing of the Drow** proved that even calculated elven discipline could birth unstoppable fury when sufficiently betrayed. Barbarians became symbols of righteous wrath against divine overreach.

Notable during this age: **Krag Stormfury**, a goliath whose rage literally tore holes in the sky, creating the first documented **wild magic zones** that persist to this day. Whether this was destruction or creation remains debated.

**Third Age (1,100-0 years ago) - The Pattern's Fear**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern viewed barbarian rage with deep suspicion. Uncontrolled emotion threatened Thread stability. The **Structured Glyphic Code** of Kel Varyan explicitly forbade rage-channeling within city limits. Yet Threadbearers noticed something troubling: in areas where barbarians regularly trained, Thread transitions occurred *more smoothly*. Rage, properly channeled, seemed to *lubricate* reality's gears rather than grinding them.

**Ashkarra's Fall** devastated barbarian communities. The dragonborn empire had employed thousands of "rage-priests" who channeled fury into defensive wards. When the Day of Twelve Shadows came, these barbarians died attempting to *burn away* the Eldritch corruption with pure rage. Their failure haunts the class to this day.

**Fourth Age (Present) - Rage Against Unraveling**

With Thread instability increasing and Eldritch Moons appearing more frequently, barbarians find renewed purpose. Their rage seems uniquely suited to combating reality's unraveling—wherever barbarians fight, the Weave holds. Some theorize that fury itself is a fundamental force, one of the **Primary Glyphs** that mortals cannot read but can embody.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors:** Barbarians feel physically *wrong* near World Anchors, as if their fury is being artificially suppressed. Many report nausea or migraines within a mile of a Loom-City's Anchor. Conversely, when Threadbearers die (as in the current crisis), barbarians often experience involuntary rage episodes—their fury filling the stabilization void.

**Threads:** Contrary to Pattern dogma, barbarian rage *doesn't* destabilize Threads—it harmonizes with them in unexpected ways:
- **Fire Thread:** Rage burns hotter, manifests as actual flame
- **Earth Thread:** Fury becomes rocklike endurance, immovable force
- **Water Thread:** Rage flows like tide, overwhelming in waves
- **Air Thread:** Fury manifests as storm, scattering and reforming
- **Spirit Thread:** Most dangerous—rage touches **ancestral memory**, channeling fury of the dead

**Leylines:** Barbarians can *feel* leylines like others feel wind. Powerful barbarians instinctively fight along leyline paths, their rage flowing through the world's energy channels. This makes them devastating in leyline-dense regions but exhausting to fight in leyline-dead zones.

**Glyphs:** Barbarians typically cannot read glyphs, but their rage can **shatter** poorly-constructed Secondary Glyphs through sheer force. Some barbarian traditions tattoo glyphs into their flesh, channeling rage through symbolic structure—dangerous but devastating.

**World Trees:** Near World Trees, barbarian rage becomes *clarified*—stripped of confusion, distilled to pure purpose. Many barbarian rites of passage occur in World Tree groves, where initiates learn to channel fury without losing themselves. The corruption of World Trees causes barbarians physical pain.

**Flaws:** Barbarians embody **Wrath** most obviously, but the deepest fury comes from **Pride**—the cosmic wound that drives the universe to prove itself. The Thaumaturge's Flaw system and barbarian rage are distant cousins, both drawing on reality's fundamental imperfections.

**Spirits:** Barbarians can commune with elementals and nature spirits through rage-trance. Where druids negotiate, barbarians *challenge*—and spirits respect the honesty of fury. Many totemic barbarians bear spirits within their rage, creating hybrid consciousness during battle.

### Societal Perceptions

**Urban Civilizations** (Kel Varyan, major trade cities): Fear and control. Barbarians must register, submit to monitoring. Seen as necessary evils—powerful but dangerous. Often relegated to designated combat zones outside city limits.

**Military Cultures** (Corvessa, Veltharyn): Respected but contained. Valuable shock troops but must operate within command structures. Given honors in victory, scapegoated in defeat.

**Traditional Societies** (Orcs, Goliaths, Halflings): Sacred roles. Barbarians are spiritual warriors, not mere fighters. Their rage connects communities to primal truth.

**Elven Cultures**: Complex. Wood elves respect barbarian honesty. High elves fear their chaos. Drow execute them as abominations of control.

**Druidic Circles**: Honored as nature's immune system. Barbarians clear corruption through fury where careful magic fails.

### Source of Power

**The Philosophical Debate:**

*Barbarian Position*: "Rage existed before gods. We channel the universe's birth-scream."

*Divine Claim*: "Fury is gift from war-gods, proof of divine favor."

*Scholarly Theory*: "Rage accesses Primary Glyphs through emotional bypass of consciousness."

*Druidic Wisdom*: "Fury is the Weave defending itself through mortal vessels."

**The Truth** (DM Knowledge): Barbarian rage accesses the **Primal Glyphs**—one of the nine Fundamental Forces woven by Astralor. It predates gods and will outlast them. Rage is reality's *immune response* to wrongness, manifested through mortal flesh.

When barbarians rage, they become briefly what the universe was in the Loomless Time: pure force without structure, necessary violence without malice.

**Legendary Figures:**

**Krag Stormfury** (Second Age): Goliath whose rage tore reality, creating first wild magic zones. Some say he still rages in the spaces between.

**Ur-Skarn the Witness** (First Age): Orc who bore 10,000 scars, each a testified truth. Her body was living law-book, her rage proof against any lie.

**Ashkarra's Last Fury** (Third Age): Unnamed dragonborn who died attempting to rage away the Day of Twelve Shadows. Failed, but bought minutes for evacuees.

### Philosophy of Power

>

— **The Philosophy of Fury**, attributed to Ur-Skarn but spoken by ten thousand voices

# 2. BARD
## WORD-WEAVERS: THOSE WHO SING THE WORLD INTO BEING

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Before Song Was Language**

Bards claim their power predates language itself—that they wield the **song** reality used to dream itself awake. In the chaos before form, when Astralor worked the nine Fundamental Forces, the universe hummed. Some mortals learned to *harmonize* with that hum, not as magic but as **agreement** with existence.

The first bards weren't performers—they were listeners who heard patterns in chaos and repeated them back, reinforcing reality's dream of itself.

> *"In the beginning was the Word, they say. Wrong. In the beginning was the Song, and the Word was what happened when the Song forgot how to harmonize."* — Loremistress Yavanna, College of Creation

**First Age (~4,500-1,800 years ago) - The Song Becomes Language**

As memory stabilized and gods consolidated, bards became civilization's foundational infrastructure. They were historians, yes, but also *reality-anchors*—their songs holding memory stable enough for culture to exist.

The elven **Woven Reverence** recognized bards as sacred: those who could sing the Pattern without reading glyphs, who intuited harmony through art. Dwarven **Deep Law** was first a series of songs before it became carved text—the rhythm holding the meaning when words failed.

Notable: **Elensar the Hundred-Tongued**, an elf who could speak every language that had ever existed and three that hadn't been invented yet. His songs literally *created* dialects that persist to this day.

**Second Age (1,800-1,100 years ago) - Songs of Rebellion**

During the Age of Heroes, bards became living weapons. The **Unleashing of the Drow** was first sung into possibility by Vel'Zyren's corrupted bards before it became manifest. Conversely, resistance movements used counter-songs to disrupt divine authority.

The **Ballad Wars** of this age saw entire battles fought through competing songs, with casualties manifesting as forgotten memories, linguistic shifts, or retroactive historical changes. Reality itself became negotiable through sufficient bardic consensus.

**Third Age (1,100-0 years ago) - The Pattern's Music**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to codify bardic power into its structures. The **College of Lore** in Kel Varyan became semi-official state institution, tasked with creating "historically accurate and Thread-stable" performances.

But bards resisted. The **College of Whispers** formed in opposition, proving that the Pattern's "official history" was riddled with lies. Every song they sang revealed contradictions, every performance exposed falsified records.

**Ashkarra's Fall** was witnessed by 300 bards who survived. Their accounts—sung simultaneously across the continent—created a **memorial echo** in the Weave itself. To this day, bards who sleep near Ashkarra's ruins dream the fall in perfect detail.

**Fourth Age (Present) - Songs of Unraveling**

With Thread instability and memory corruption increasing, bards find themselves fighting a losing battle. How do you preserve history when the past keeps changing? How do you sing truth when reality itself lies?

Some bards have begun singing **hypothetical songs**—not what happened, but what *should have* happened, attempting to retroactively stabilize reality through consensus narrative.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors:** Bards can *hear* World Anchors—a deep thrum beneath reality's surface. Near healthy Anchors, songs carry further and last longer. Near dying Anchors (like Ashkarra's ruins), songs echo strangely, arriving at listeners' ears *before* the bard sings them.

**Threads:** Bards harmonize with Threads instinctively:
- **Fire Thread:** Songs become rallying cries, spreading like flame
- **Earth Thread:** Performances root deep, remembered for generations
- **Water Thread:** Music flows unpredictably, finding listeners who need it
- **Air Thread:** Songs travel impossibly far, reaching unintended audiences
- **Spirit Thread:** Performances echo into memory itself, becoming "more real" than events they describe

**Leylines:** Bards use leylines as amplification networks. A skilled bard singing at a leyline nexus can be heard hundreds of miles away. The greatest performances in history occurred at leyline-dense locations, their power magnified geometrically.

**Glyphs:** Bards don't typically study glyphs formally, but their songs *contain* glyphs—auditory symbols that bypass conscious understanding to affect listeners directly. A well-crafted bardic performance writes **temporary glyphs** onto listeners' souls.

**World Trees:** Near World Trees, bardic songs take on eerie property—they're remembered *perfectly* by everyone who hears them, becoming oral tradition instantly. The corruption of World Trees causes bardic memory to decay, with songs forgetting themselves mid-performance.

**Flaws:** Bards embody **Envy**—the cosmic wound that drives creation to improve, compare, compete. Their power comes from making others *want* what the bard describes. The greatest performances create **cultural envy**, reshaping civilizations through collective desire.

**Spirits:** Bards can call spirits through song, not commanding but *inviting*. Many bards carry spirit-audiences—ghosts who follow to hear performances, sometimes applauding at inopportune moments.

**Elven Cultures**: Deeply respected. Bards maintain cultural continuity across centuries. Some elven bards live 1000+ years, becoming living history.

**Druidic Circles**: Honored as allies. Bards help maintain the **Verdant Accord**—oral traditions that predate written history.

### Source of Power

**The Philosophical Debate:**

*Divine Claim*: "Inspiration comes from gods—muses, patrons, divine spark."

*Scholarly Theory*: "Bardic magic is group hypnosis weaponized through art."

*Druidic Wisdom*: "Bards remember the first song, when universe sang itself awake."

**The Truth** (DM Knowledge): Bardic power accesses the **Form Glyphs**—reality's self-image, what the universe believes about itself. When bards perform convincingly enough, reality changes to match the story. This is why bardic magic seems "softer" than wizard spells—it's *persuading* reality rather than commanding it.

**Legendary Figures:**

**The 300 of Ashkarra** (Third Age): Bards who witnessed the fall and simultaneously sang it continent-wide, creating memorial echo still audible today.

### Philosophy of Power

> *"The universe is narrative, not mathematics. Reality doesn't follow laws—it follows stories. We know this because we're the ones writing it.*
>
> *Every spell a wizard casts is a story they tell: 'I speak these words, make these gestures, and fire obeys.' They've convinced themselves, so reality agrees. But that's amateur fiction. We're professionals.*

>
> *But with great power comes great responsibility, they say. And they're right. Every song we sing, every story we tell, becomes a little more real. We're not just entertainers—we're architects of truth itself.*
>
> *So sing carefully. Sing truthfully. Because one day, reality will believe you. And then everyone else will too.*
>
> *That's not power. That's curse. That's sacred duty. That's art."*

— **The Performer's Paradox**, Loremistress Yavanna

# 3. CLERIC
## DIVINE CONDUITS: THE CHOSEN OF THE BOUND PATTERN

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Before Gods Became Gods**

Clerics, paradoxically, existed before they had gods to serve. The first "clerics" were those who felt divine presence in everything—in storms, in stone, in growth and decay. They channeled not deities, but **forces**—the Elemental Embodiments that would later be mistaken for creators.

When Vatharun stirred, certain mortals felt his movement. When Nereus gathered, some heard his call. These first faithful didn't worship—they *resonated*. They became living instruments for cosmic forces seeking mortal expression.

> *"The gods did not create us. We created them—or rather, we gave them shape through our need to believe. And in return, they made us real."* — Heretical text, author unknown, burned repeatedly

**First Age (~4,500-1,800 years ago) - The Divine Claim**

This age saw the transformation of clerics from force-channelers to priest-class. Gods organized into pantheons, made creation claims, demanded worship. The first true "clerics" emerged: mortals granted divine power in exchange for faith and service.

The **Faith of Woven Reverence** (elven) resisted this transition. Their "clerics" channeled the Pattern itself, refusing to credit individual gods. This created centuries of theological conflict still unresolved.

Notable: **Saint Tharivol the Questioning**, who received divine visions despite publically doubting divine creation-claims. His miracles proved gods granted power regardless of doctrine, causing massive theological crisis.

**Second Age (1,800-1,100 years ago) - The Fracture**

During the Age of Rebellion, clerical power *fragmented*. Gods contradicted each other. Clerics of rival pantheons received conflicting divine commands. Some concluded gods themselves didn't know truth—they merely *believed* they were creators.

The **Silence of the Twelve** occurred when twelve powerful clerics simultaneously lost all divine connection. When power returned three days later, they refused to explain what they'd experienced. Some say they learned gods' true origin and were struck mute for it.

**Third Age (1,100-0 years ago) - The Pattern's Priesthood**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern centralized clerical power under theological umbrella that *included* all pantheons. Clerics became infrastructure: maintaining Thread stability, interpreting divine will, administering sacraments.

But centralization bred corruption. By the time of **Ashkarra's Fall**, clerical hierarchy was more political than spiritual. The Day of Twelve Shadows killed 3,000 clerics attempting to channel divine power against Eldritch corruption. Their failure shook faith continent-wide.

**Fourth Age (Present) - Faith in Crisis**

The current Threadbearer assassinations have devastated clerical orders. Without stable Thread connections, divine power flows unpredictably. Some clerics manifest abilities they didn't pray for. Others lose power mid-ritual.

Worst: some clerics now channel power without being able to identify its *source*. Are they channeling gods? The Weave itself? Eldritch forces masquerading as divine? The uncertainty breeds terror in orders built on certainty.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors:** Clerics feel World Anchors as "divine presence"—warm, comforting, stabilizing. Near healthy Anchors, prayer comes easily and miracles manifest smoothly. Near dying Anchors, clerics experience "divine absence"—as if their god has abandoned them, even when power still flows.

This creates theological crisis: if World Anchors make gods feel "present," were gods ever separate from the Anchors? Or were clerics channeling the Thread system all along?

**Threads:** Divine power explicitly flows through Thread system:
- **Fire Thread:** Clerics gain offensive power, purification abilities
- **Earth Thread:** Healing amplifies, wards become unbreakable
- **Water Thread:** Divination sharpens, emotions intensify
- **Air Thread:** Mobility increases, messages carry further
- **Spirit Thread:** Most powerful but dangerous—direct ancestral/divine contact, memory corruption risk

**Leylines:** Clerics on leyline nexuses report "hearing divine voice more clearly." Skeptics note this suggests gods speak through leylines, not directly—implying divine power is actually **structured cosmic force**, not personal relationship.

**Glyphs:** Clerics learn Secondary Glyphs as "divine script"—holy symbols that channel power. The most heretical theory: glyphs work whether you pray over them or not, suggesting divine power is **glyphic manipulation mistaken for faith**.

**World Trees:** Near World Trees, clerics of nature deities feel stronger. But disturbing truth: clerics of ANY deity feel stronger near World Trees. This suggests divine power flows through World Trees regardless of pantheon—implying gods don't grant power, they *access* it from same source mortals could theoretically reach.

**Flaws:** Most clerics channel **Pride**—the cosmic wound driving existence to prove itself worthy. Their power comes from absolute certainty, unshakeable faith. But the deepest divine magic touches **all four Flaws**: Pride to believe, Wrath to act, Fear to submit, Envy to desire transcendence.

**Spirits:** Clerics work with spirits regularly—angels, demons, elementals, ancestors. But increasingly, clerics notice spirits seem more *informed* than gods about cosmic mechanics. If servants know more than masters, what does that reveal about divine hierarchy?

# 4. DRUID
## ROOT-SPEAKERS: THOSE WHO REMEMBER BEFORE GODS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Listeners**

Druids are the oldest mortal tradition—older than priests, older than wizards, older than the gods themselves. When Vatharun planted the first World Trees, certain mortals *heard*. Not with ears, but with something deeper: **Pattern-sense**, the ability to feel the Weave's structure before language existed to describe it.

> *"We did not learn the Verdant Accord. We remembered it. Some memories are older than the minds that hold them."* — The Root-Testament

**First Age (~4,500-1,800 years ago) - The Verdant Accord**

As gods organized into pantheons and made creation-claims, druids became living contradictions. They practiced magic before gods taught it. They remembered the Weave's formation. They had *evidence*—World Trees that predated divine intervention, leylines that needed no deity to function.

The **Verdant Accord** formalized druidic wisdom: reality self-organizes; consciousness emerges from pattern; gods are passengers in the same vehicle mortals ride. This made them dangerous. The **Great Persecution** began, with gods demanding druids accept divine authority.

Notable: **The Grove Burnings**, where dragonborn and early human converts destroyed sacred groves to prove gods' superiority. Each burning created a **scar in the Weave** still visible today.

**Second Age (1,800-1,100 years ago) - Survival Through Secrecy**

During the Age of Rebellion, druids went underground—literally. They established the **Root-Sanctums**, hidden chambers beneath World Trees where forbidden knowledge survived. The **Drow Betrayal** particularly devastated druids—Vel'Zyren's followers violated sacred groves during their descent, corrupting leylines for centuries.

The **Circle of Spores** emerged during this age—druids who embraced death and decay as sacred. Their philosophy: if gods claim creation, druids claim decomposition. Balance through accepted entropy.

**Third Age (1,100-0 years ago) - Accommodation and Compromise**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to absorb druidic traditions. The **Verdant Wards** were established—five official druidic circles "integrated" into Pattern theology. Many druids accepted this compromise. Others, the **Unbound**, rejected it as collaboration with lies.

**Ashkarra's Fall** vindicated druidic warnings. The World Trees had been "whispering" corruption for years—druids felt it, reported it, were ignored. When the Day of Twelve Shadows came, it was druids who evacuated survivors through Root-paths while clerics died attempting impossible divine intervention.

**Fourth Age (Present) - Vindication and Crisis**

The current Thread instability proves what druids always taught: the Thread system is **artificial**—an improvement over nothing, but inferior to the pre-Faith leyline networks. As World Trees corrupt and World Anchors fail, druids find themselves simultaneously vindicated and horrified.

Some advocate destroying all World Anchors, returning to "natural" leyline flow. Others warn this would cause immediate civilizational collapse. The Unbound whisper: "*Perhaps collapse is cleansing.*"

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors:** Druids feel World Anchors as **artificial pressure**—reality forced into unnatural stability. Near Anchors, natural magic becomes constrained, wild growth controlled. Many druids develop physical symptoms (migraines, nausea) near Anchors, as if allergic to manufactured order.

**Threads:** Druids recognize Threads as **simplification**—the infinitely complex leyline network reduced to five categories. This works, barely, but loses nuance. Druids can feel the 47 different "flavors" of Earth Thread the Pattern lumps together.

**Leylines:** Druids don't just *use* leylines—they **are** leylines in some metaphysical sense. Powerful druids develop root-like nervous systems that physically connect to leyline networks. Some lose ability to leave their sacred groves, becoming permanent leyline nodes.

**Glyphs:** Druids view glyphs as **corruption**—artificial symbols imposed on natural pattern. They practice **glyph-free magic**, channeling directly through organic structures. This makes druidic magic "cleaner" but less flexible than glyphic casting.

**World Trees:** The sacred heart of druidism. World Trees are not just sources of power but **teachers**, living libraries that remember the Loomless Time. Druids commune with Trees not through prayer but through **direct nervous system interface**—physically connecting their consciousness to root-networks.

The corruption of World Trees causes druids literal physical agony. Many develop stigmata-like wounds when Trees suffer. Some theorize druids and World Trees share ancestry.

**Flaws:** Druids reject the concept—they see "Flaws" as **Pattern assumptions**, not cosmic truths. What Pattern calls "Pride" druids name "growth." What Pattern calls "Wrath" druids name "defense." The Flaw system is theological framework imposed on neutral natural processes.

**Spirits:** Druids work with spirits as **equals**, not subordinates. Nature spirits, elementals, and fey recognize druids as kin. Most druid "spells" are actually negotiations—asking spirits for assistance rather than commanding reality.

# 5. FIGHTER
## THE TESTED BLADE: THOSE WHO PERFECT VIOLENCE

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Violence Without Art**

Fighters are the most honest class: they perfect the oldest profession. When Soltharion taught the first mortals that violence could solve problems, fighters learned to make violence *elegant*. Where barbarians embody rage and paladins sanctify combat, fighters simply... excel.

No magic required. No divine favor. No cosmic significance. Just skill, training, discipline, and will. In a world of gods and glyphs, fighters prove mortals can achieve mastery through pure effort.

> *"A wizard studies years to cast flame. I need two seconds and a well-placed blade. Who's truly powerful?"* — Commander Graven, Kel Varyan Imperial Marines

**First Age - The Warrior-Kings**

Early fighters were tribal champions, monster hunters, clan protectors. As civilizations formed, they became professional soldiers. The first martial traditions emerged—styles passed master to student, techniques refined across generations.

Notable: **The Seven Stances**, a dwarven martial art allegedly taught by Gaeldros himself. Practitioners claim no divine power, but stances "align with Stone-Law"—suggesting fighters unconsciously channel cosmic patterns through pure physical form.

**Second Age - The Age of Champions**

During the Rebellions, fighters proved themselves divine equals. **Kara Ironbreaker**, a human fighter, single-handedly killed a lesser deity through nothing but superior tactics. Her victory inspired the **Mortal Pride** movement—belief that mortals needed neither gods nor magic to achieve greatness.

The **First Martial Academies** formed during this age, codifying combat into teachable curriculum. Fighting became *science*, not just instinct.

**Third Age - The Professional Soldier**

As the Faith of the Bound Pattern established peace through Thread stability, warfare became ritualized. Fighters professionalized—knights, mercenaries, guards, duelists. The **martial class system** emerged:

- **Knights**: Noble fighters, status through birth and blade
- **Soldiers**: Professional fighters, skill through training
- **Mercenaries**: Pragmatic fighters, loyalty through payment
- **Gladiators**: Entertainment fighters, worth through spectacle

**Ashkarra's Fall** devastated fighter culture. The dragonborn empire employed millions of professional soldiers—all died or scattered when the city fell. Martial traditions were lost, techniques forgotten, entire fighting styles extinct.

**Fourth Age - Resurgence and Innovation**

The current crisis demands fighters. Magic becomes unreliable, clerics lose power, Thread instability disrupts wizardry—but a sword still cuts. Fighters find themselves newly valuable in a world where reality itself becomes uncertain.

Veltharyn leads fighter innovation, developing new techniques combining traditional bladework with magical technology. The **Artificer-Duelist** style merges martial and magical engineering.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Fighters feel nothing near World Anchors—no magical resonance, no divine presence. This absence proves something important: physical reality remains constant while magical layers fluctuate. Fighters are **reality's baseline**.

**Threads**: Thread alignments subtly affect martial performance:
- **Fire Thread**: Aggression increases, stamina surges
- **Earth Thread**: Defense improves, footing stabilizes  
- **Water Thread**: Fluidity enhances, adaptive tactics easier
- **Air Thread**: Speed increases, accuracy improves
- **Spirit Thread**: Instinct sharpens, muscle memory heightens

But fighters don't *channel* Threads—they're simply mortal animals affected by environmental conditions. This makes them more consistent than magic-users.

**Leylines**: Some fighters report "flow states" near strong leylines, but this might be placebo. Skeptical fighters note they perform equally well in leyline-dead zones—suggesting pure skill transcends magical geography.

**Glyphs**: Fighters learn to *destroy* glyphs through precise strikes. The **Glyph-Breaking Blade** style teaches hitting glyphs' structural weaknesses, shattering magical constructs through mundane violence.

**World Trees**: No special interaction. Fighters train in World Tree groves same as elsewhere. This neutrality proves fighters occupy different metaphysical space than magic classes.

**Flaws**: Fighters embody **all Flaws simultaneously**:
- **Pride** in skill perfection
- **Wrath** channeled through combat
- **Fear** overcome through discipline
- **Envy** driving competitive excellence

Unlike Thaumaturges who weaponize Flaws, fighters *transcend* them through training.

**Spirits**: Fighters mostly ignore spirits. Spirits mostly ignore fighters. This mutual disinterest creates philosophical question: are fighters beneath spiritual notice, or above it?

# 10. PALADIN
## OATH-BOUND: THOSE WHO MAKE PROMISES WEAPONS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Before Oaths Meant Power**

Paladins didn't exist in the Loomless Time—they require civilization to rebel against, codes to uphold, sacred vows to empower. They're civilization's defenders and critics simultaneously: holding society to ideals society itself forgets.

**First Age - The First Sacred Orders**

When gods organized and civilization formed, some warriors swore oaths beyond mere military service: *sacred promises* transforming belief into power. The first paladins discovered: conviction can reshape reality if sufficiently absolute.

The **Oath of Devotion** emerged first—dedicating oneself to gods, goodness, righteousness. But darker oaths followed: **Oath of Conquest** (domination), **Oath of Vengeance** (retribution), **Oath of Treachery** (self-interest masked as principle).

Notable: **Saint Thaldris the Unbreakable**—paladin whose oath never wavered despite witnessing divine lies. When confronted with evidence gods lied about creation, she responded: "*My oath is to principles, not persons. If gods betray principles, I oppose gods.*" She survived three divine assassination attempts.

**Second Age - The Oath Wars**

During Rebellions, paladins fragmented. Some sided with gods (Oath of Devotion). Others opposed them (Oath of Rebellion). Most discovered: oath-power flows from *conviction*, not correctness. Evil paladins proved as powerful as good ones—what mattered was absolute belief.

The **Oath of Redemption** emerged—paladins dedicated to reforming villains, saving corrupted souls. Their philosophy: evil is disease, paladins are cure. Controversial but effective.

**Third Age - The Pattern's Champions**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern tried monopolizing paladinhood—claiming only Pattern-sworn paladins were "real." The **Sanctified Orders** formed: military-religious paladins serving as Pattern's sword.

**Ashkarra's Fall** killed thousands of paladins attempting to hold the city through pure conviction. Their oaths weren't enough. This broke many surviving paladins—if oaths fail against sufficient horror, what's their purpose?

**Fourth Age - Oath-Crisis**

Current Thread instability creates unprecedented question: can paladins swear oaths to stabilize reality itself? The **Oath of the Bound Thread** emerged experimentally—paladins vowing to maintain Thread system regardless of cost. They channel power, but unclear if from gods, Threads, or conviction alone.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Paladins feel Anchors as **sacred duty fulfilled**—reality stabilized through deliberate effort mirrors oath-keeping. Near healthy Anchors, paladin powers intensify (their conviction validated by cosmic stability). Near dying Anchors, paladins feel personally responsible for failure.

# 12. RANGER
## BOUNDARY-WALKERS: THOSE WHO LIVE BETWEEN WORLDS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Scouts**

Rangers emerged from necessity: when mortals first left settlements to explore wilderness, someone had to know the way back. The first rangers were guides, hunters, survivors—those who learned to read land like others read glyphs.

But rangers became more than skilled outdoorsmen. They became **liminal beings**—existing between civilization and wilderness, neither fully wild nor fully tame. They speak to both cities and forests, trusted by neither, essential to both.

> *"The city calls us savage. The forest calls us corrupted. We call ourselves necessary. Someone must walk the boundaries. Someone must translate between worlds that refuse to speak."* — The Ranger's Oath

**First Age - Wilderness Guardians**

Elven rangers developed deepest tradition: **The Green Wardens**, who bonded with specific forests, becoming part guardian, part spirit. They could move through their territories unseen, sense disturbances, communicate with wildlife. Some never returned to civilization, becoming wholly wild.

**Second Age - The Divided Loyalties**

During Rebellions, rangers faced crisis: which side? Some served gods, tracking rebellious mortals. Others joined mortals, using wilderness knowledge to hide revolutionaries. Many served both, playing complicated games of loyalty and betrayal.

The **Beast-Bond Tradition** emerged—rangers forming supernatural connections with animal companions. Originally practical (wolves aid hunting), it became mystical (ranger and beast sharing consciousness). Druids claimed rangers were stealing their techniques. Rangers claimed they discovered it independently.

**Third Age - The Contained Wild**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to civilize ranging. The **Imperial Scouts** became official: licensed rangers patrolling designated territories. Wild rangers (unlicensed, serving no authority) were outlawed as bandits.

This created the **Ranger Divide**:
- **Pattern Rangers**: Served civilization, protected trade routes, hunted criminals
- **Wild Rangers**: Served wilderness, protected sacred groves, hunted civilization's expansion

Most rangers existed in grey area—technically licensed, actually wild.

**Ashkarra's Fall** barely affected rangers—few lived in cities. But it created the **Ashkarra Wasteland**, requiring new ranging techniques for corrupted territory. Rangers who entered seeking survivors often didn't return. Those who did came back... changed.

**Fourth Age - Wilderness Reclaiming**

Thread instability disrupts civilization more than wilderness. As cities struggle with magical failures, forests grow stronger. Rangers find themselves newly valued—guides through increasingly dangerous territories between failing urban centers.

# 13. ROGUE
## SHADOW-DANCERS: THOSE WHO EXPLOIT RULES BY IGNORING THEM

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Tricksters**

Rogues claim lineage from first mortal who realized: rules exist to be circumvented. Every system has vulnerabilities. Every authority has blind spots. Every lock wants to be picked.

The first rogues weren't criminals—they were survivors who noticed civilization creates as many dangers as it prevents. Laws protect the powerful, punish the weak. So the weak learned to move between the cracks.

> *"They built walls to keep us out. We learned to climb. They made laws to control us. We learned to hide. They established order to suppress us. We learned to dance in chaos. They call us criminals. We call ourselves free."* — The Thief's Anthem

**First Age - The Underworld Emerges**

As cities formed, crime formed. Where there's property, there's theft. Where there's law, there's lawbreaking. Where there's authority, there's rebellion disguised as survival.

The **First Thieves' Guilds** organized—criminals becoming infrastructure parallel to legitimate society. They provided services: smuggling, assassination, intelligence gathering, forbidden goods. Not chaos—alternative order.

Notable: **The Shadowmarket Accords**—treaty between Kel Varyan government and thieves' guild. Government agreed not to eradicate crime (impossible anyway); guild agreed to regulate itself, prevent excessive violence. Model copied across continent.

**Second Age - Professional Rebellion**

During the age of heroes, rogues became revolutionary infrastructure. Every rebellion needed spies, saboteurs, smugglers. Rogues provided expertise, training anyone willing to learn.

**Fourth Age - Chaos as Opportunity**

**World Anchors**: Rogues feel Anchors as **increased surveillance**—reality made too stable to hide in. Near Anchors, stealth becomes difficult (fewer shadows, less chaos to exploit). Rogues prefer operating between cities.

**Flaws**: Rogues embody **Envy and Pride**—envy driving them to take what others have, pride refusing to accept legitimate authority. Their power comes from weaponizing cosmic unfairness.

# 14. RUNESMITH
## SYMBOL-FORGERS: THOSE WHO WRITE REALITY IN STONE

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Before Symbols Were Power**

Runesmiths claim they discovered writing could reshape reality. The first runesmith noticed: when you carve symbol with sufficient intent, intention manifests. Marks become magic.

This predates formal glyphic systems—runesmiths were creating power-symbols before anyone understood glyphs existed. They stumbled into cosmic truth: reality reads its own descriptions and changes to match.

> *"We don't write *about* reality. We write reality itself. Every rune is command, every carving is law. The world reads our work and obeys."* — The First Inscription

**First Age - The Stone-Writers**

Dwarven culture formalized runesmithing. Their **Stone-Law** was literally carved—legal codes inscribed on mountain-stone, enforced by runes' inherent power. To break law carved in powered runes was to fight reality itself.

The **Rune-Wars** occurred when different schools developed contradictory runic systems. Runes saying "this door cannot open" versus runes saying "this door must open"—reality caught in logical paradox. Several cities destroyed when contradictory runes caused reality-collapse.

Notable: **Thalgrim Rune-Breaker**—dwarf who developed **anti-runes** that canceled other runes. This made him most dangerous mortal alive—he could unmake any runic power.

**Second Age - The Forbidden Runes**

During Rebellions, runesmiths developed **god-binding runes**—symbols designed to constrain divine power. Some worked, temporarily trapping lesser deities. Gods responded with the **Rune-Purge**: systematic destruction of runesmithing traditions.

Thousands of runesmiths died. Their rune-libraries burned. But runes, once learned, persist—carved into minds as permanently as stone.

**Third Age - Controlled Inscription**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to monopolize runesmithing. Only licensed runesmiths could create powered runes. Unlicensed rune-carving became capital crime.

This failed—runesmiths operated underground, creating **shadow-runes** (concealed carvings that activated only under specific conditions). Many buildings contained hidden runic defenses unknown even to owners.

**Ashkarra's Fall** occurred partly through runic failure. The city's defensive runes, carved over centuries, suddenly contradicted each other when all Eldritch Moons aligned. The resulting reality-collapse contributed to city's destruction.

**Fourth Age - Runes of Desperation**

Thread instability makes traditional magic unreliable. But runes, carved into permanent materials, remain stable. Runesmiths find themselves essential—carving Thread-independent protective runes as insurance against cosmic failure.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Runesmiths can **reinforce** World Anchors through sympathetic runes—carvings that mirror Anchor's stabilizing function. Some theorize World Anchors are themselves massive runes.

**Threads**: Runes interact complexly with Threads:
- Runes carved during specific Thread persist longer
- **Fire-Runes**: carved during Fire Thread, hold heat/light indefinitely
- **Earth-Runes**: carved during Earth Thread, become unbreakable
- **Water-Runes**: carved during Water Thread, adapt to circumstances
- **Air-Runes**: carved during Air Thread, affect distant locations
- **Spirit-Runes**: carved during Spirit Thread, hold memory/consciousness

**Leylines**: Runesmiths carve runes at leyline nexuses for maximum power. These **nexus-runes** affect entire regions, reshaping local reality permanently.

**Glyphs**: Runes ARE glyphs—just carved rather than cast. But runesmiths claim their art predates formal glyphic theory. Argument continues: did runesmiths discover glyphs first, or did glyphs inspire runesmithing?

**World Trees**: Carving runes onto World Trees is ultimate taboo—would corrupt Tree's natural function. But some heretical runesmiths theorize Trees themselves contain runes, written in growth-patterns rather than carved marks.

**Flaws**: Runesmiths embody **Pride**—cosmic wound driving existence to permanent meaning. Every rune says: "I was here, I made this, I matter." Runesmithing is immortality through inscription.

**Spirits**: Runes can **bind** spirits, trap them in objects, force servitude. This makes runesmiths dangerous to spirit-world. Many spirits hunt runesmiths preemptively.

### Regional Variations

**Elarion**

*Thar Vornhal - The Lawcarvers*
- Dwarven runesmiths carving legal/magical codes
- Their runes enforce contracts, bind oaths, punish violations
- Philosophy: law written in stone cannot be corrupted

*Veltharyn - The Forge-Runesmiths*
- Combine runesmithing with metalworking
- Create weapons/armor with permanent runic enhancement
- Most valuable craftsmen in Dór-o Estel

**Kerythra**

*Mountain Runemasters*
- Carve massive runes visible from miles away
- These territorial runes warn/ward/ward entire regions
- Philosophy: big runes, big effects

### Source of Power

**The Inscription Theory** (DM Knowledge):

Runes access the **Form Glyphs**—reality's self-description. When runesmith carves symbol with sufficient skill and intent, they're editing reality's source code. The universe reads the inscription and updates itself.

This makes runesmithing most permanent magic—carved reality doesn't fade.

### Philosophy of Power

> *"Wizards cast spells that fade. Clerics channel gods that fail. We carve truth into stone that outlasts civilizations.*
>
> *Every rune is argument with reality: 'You should work this way.' And if carved correctly, with proper technique and absolute conviction, reality agrees.*
>
> *This is not request. This is not prayer. This is command written in language reality cannot ignore.*

> *We are writing the world."*

— **The Carver's Creed**

# 15. SANGROMANCER
## BLOOD-WEAVERS: THOSE WHO MAKE LIFE INTO CURRENCY

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Sacrifice**

Sangromancy emerged from oldest observation: blood is life. Spill it, life ends. Share it, connections form. Manipulate it, power flows. The first sangromancers didn't discover blood magic—they noticed it was already happening.

Every injury, every birth, every death involved blood. Some mortals realized: if blood carries life, blood can **transfer** life. From one vessel to another. From mortal to spell. From self to Other.

> *"They call us monsters. But we're honest. Every magic costs. We simply pay in visible currency. Their spells cost souls, sanity, years of life—invisible debts. Ours? We bleed. At least we're honest about the price."* — The Crimson Testament

**First Age - The Blood Pacts**

Early sangromancy was communal—tribes sharing blood to seal alliances, warriors bleeding together to bond units. This evolved into **blood oaths** (promises sealed through shared cutting) and eventually blood magic (power channeled through deliberate sacrifice).

The **Sanguine Orders** formed—magical traditions teaching controlled bloodletting as spellcraft. They developed techniques: how much blood for how much power, which veins to cut, how to recover without dying.

Notable: **Velara the Red**—elf sangromancer who lived 2,000+ years by extending life through blood manipulation. She finally died when her accumulated blood-debt manifested as blood-plague killing entire valley. Her body dissolved into crimson mist.

**Second Age - The Forbidden Art**

During Rebellions, sangromancy became weapon. Blood magic could bind enemies, extract information through forced exsanguination, kill at distance through blood-curse. The gods responded with **Sanguine Persecution**—declaring blood magic inherently evil.

This drove sangromancy underground. The **Crimson Veil**—secret society preserving blood-magic traditions despite persecution. They developed **silent sangromancy**: blood magic without visible cutting, powered by internal bleeding.

**Third Age - The Taboo Science**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern officially banned sangromancy as Pattern-corruption. But privately employed sangromancers—their techniques too valuable to completely eliminate. The **Vashkora Veyzul Mirror Empire** openly practiced blood magic, considering it sophisticated art.

**Ashkarra's Fall** killed thousands of sangromancers attempting to channel city's collective lifeforce into protective barrier. The attempt failed—you cannot sacrifice entire population to save itself. The paradox killed them.

**Fourth Age - Necessary Evil**

Thread instability makes traditional magic unreliable. Blood magic, powered by life itself rather than external systems, remains stable. Desperate authorities quietly lift sangromancy bans, employing blood-mages for Thread-independent power.

This creates moral crisis: is accepting blood magic's effectiveness worth tolerating its methods?

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Sangromancers feel Anchors as **life-pressure**—concentrated vitality stabilizing reality. Near Anchors, blood magic intensifies dangerously—small spells become overwhelming. Many sangromancers develop nosebleeds near Anchors from involuntary power surge.

**Threads**: Blood magic interacts strangely with Threads:
- **Fire Thread**: Blood burns when spilled, cauterizes immediately
- **Earth Thread**: Blood crystallizes, can be shaped like stone
- **Water Thread**: Blood remains fluid indefinitely, can be transported miles
- **Air Thread**: Blood becomes mist, aerosol magic
- **Spirit Thread**: Most dangerous—blood carries ancestral memory, becomes sentient

**Leylines**: Sangromancers can **poison** leylines by bleeding onto nexus points. Corrupted leylines carry blood-taint, spreading disease, madness, or unwanted magic for miles.

**Glyphs**: Sangromancers write **blood-glyphs**—symbols drawn in blood that hold power proportional to blood's significance. Virgin blood, warrior blood, innocent blood, guilty blood—all work differently.

**World Trees**: Bleeding near World Trees is taboo—Trees absorb blood, sometimes developing disturbing "thirst." Several World Trees show corruption specifically from sangromantic contamination.

**Flaws**: Sangromancers embody **Wrath and Fear**—wrath accepting violence against self, fear of mortality driving desperate life-extension. Their power comes from weaponizing most primal terror: death through exsanguination.

**Spirits**: Blood-spirits (entities formed from spilled blood, vengeful ghosts of exsanguination victims) plague sangromancers. Many develop parasitic spirit-infections requiring constant management.

### Regional Variations

**Vashkora**

*Veyzul Mirror Empire - Blood Aristocracy*
- Openly practice sangromancy as high art
- Social status determined by blood-purity and magical potency
- Regular blood-duels establishing hierarchy
- Philosophy: blood is truth—cannot lie, cannot hide, cannot fake
- **Mirror-Witches**: use blood-scrying for intelligence gathering
- **Blood-Psions**: extract memories from blood samples

*Dragonborn Remnants*
- Surviving sangromancers from Ashkarra
- Channel draconic bloodlines for enhanced power
- Guilt-driven—many died attempting to save city through blood magic
- Philosophy: blood-debt must be repaid

**Elarion (Underground)**

*The Crimson Veil*
- Secret society preserving blood-magic traditions
- Operate in every major city despite bans
- Sell services to desperate: life-extension, healing, revenge
- Philosophy: necessity justifies taboo

**Mirror Chain**

*Coral-Blood Traditions*
- Island sangromancers combining blood and ocean magic
- Sacrifice to sea-spirits through ritual bleeding
- Can call sharks/predators through blood-scent magic

**Underground**

*Drow Blood-Theocracy*
- Vel'Zyren demands regular blood sacrifice
- All drow practice minor sangromancy
- Social control through enforced bleeding
- Philosophy: pain proves devotion, blood proves loyalty

**Near-Universal Revulsion**: Most cultures consider sangromancy evil—visceral, horrifying, unnatural. Even when effective, morally suspect.

**Vashkora Exception**: Veyzul Empire respects blood-mages, considers them sophisticated practitioners.

**Desperate Acceptance**: When dying, people hire sangromancers. When disease spreads, authorities employ them. Hypocrisy universal.

**Criminal Associations**: Many sangromancers work as assassins, torturers, forbidden healers. Profession attracts darkness.

**The Life-Force Theory:**

Sangromancy channels life-energy directly—bypassing gods, glyphs, Threads. Blood is concentrated vitality. Spilling it releases power. This makes sangromancy Thread-independent but morally fraught.

Blood magic accesses the **Primal Glyphs**—life force itself as fundamental cosmic element. Every living thing contains fragment of original creative energy. Sangromancers extract and redirect this.

# 16. SHADOWBINDER

> *"Light reveals. Shadow conceals. Both are tools. We prefer the second."* — The Umbral Principle

As civilizations built cities around light (torches, lanterns, magic), shadows multiplied. Every light source creates shadows. The brighter the civilization, the darker its corners.

The **Umbral Orders** formed—practicing shadow-magic not as evil but as alternative. They developed **shadow-walking** (teleporting through connected shadows), **shadow-crafting** (creating solid constructs from darkness), and **shadow-binding** (imprisoning enemies in frozen shadow).

**Second Age - The Darkness Rebellion**

During the age of heroes, shadowbinders became essential to revolution. Every rebellion needs secrecy. Shadows provided it. The **Night-Striders**—shadowbinder guerrillas sabotaging divine forces under cover of darkness.

Gods responded with the **Eternal Vigil**—magical lights that never extinguish, illuminating formerly-dark spaces. This created the **Light-Shadow Wars**: shadowbinders destroying lights, gods creating new ones.

**Third Age - Controlled Darkness**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to eliminate all shadow through comprehensive illumination. The **Great Lighting**—project to install magical lights throughout Loom-Cities, eliminating darkness.

**Ashkarra's Fall** occurred during the **Day of Twelve Shadows**—when all Eldritch Moons eclipsed sun simultaneously. Shadowbinders felt it approaching, warned authorities, were ignored. The brief total darkness allowed Eldritch forces to manifest. Shadowbinders evacuated thousands through shadow-paths while others died in light.

**Fourth Age - Shadows Lengthening**

Thread instability affects light-magic disproportionately. Eternal lights flicker, fail, die. Darkness returns. Shadowbinders, who never depended on Thread-stability, thrive in growing gloom.

Some theorize: if Thread system completely fails, eternal night returns. Shadowbinders will inherit the dark.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Shadowbinders feel Anchors as **artificial illumination**—reality made too bright, too stable, too defined. Near Anchors, shadows become weak, pale, unable to hide. Shadowbinders develop light-sensitivity near Anchors.

**Threads**: Shadow magic exists *between* Threads—in spaces where Thread influence weakens:
- All Threads create shadows (absence defines presence)
- Spirit Thread creates deepest shadows (memory's dark)
- Fire Thread creates sharpest shadows (bright light, deep dark)
- Optimal: Thread-transitions, when reality briefly unstable

**Leylines**: Shadowbinders use leylines as **shadow-highways**—darkness flows along ley-paths like water in riverbed. Can travel vast distances through connected shadow-networks.

**Glyphs**: Shadowbinders write **anti-glyphs**—symbols that absorb light, create darkness, cancel illumination-magic. These are inverse of normal glyphs—defining through negation rather than assertion.

**World Trees**: World Trees cast deepest, most permanent shadows—ancient darkness that predates civilization. Shadowbinders can hide indefinitely in World Tree shadow. Some never leave, becoming shadow-spirits themselves.

**Flaws**: Shadowbinders embody **Fear**—cosmic wound driving existence into hiding, seeking safety in invisibility. Their power comes from accepting darkness others flee.

**Spirits**: Shadow-spirits (entities formed from absence, living holes in reality) work with shadowbinders. These spirits are terrifying—they're not present, they're *absence* made conscious.

### Regional Variations

**Elarion**

*Kel Varyan - The Shade-Spies*

- Officially don't exist, unofficially essential
- Can infiltrate anywhere shadows reach
- Philosophy: information flows through darkness

*Talanthar - The Night-Wardens*
- Forest shadowbinders using tree-shadow for guerrilla warfare
- Corvessan crusaders fear Talanthar's darkness more than its druids
- Can make entire armies invisible in forest shadow
- Philosophy: forest creates shadow, we become shadow

**Underground**

*The Hollowdeep Masters*
- Shadowbinders who rule eternal darkness
- Most powerful of their kind—perfected in absence of light
- Can create pocket dimensions of pure darkness

- Philosophy: darkness is truth, light is deception

Shadow magic doesn't access Fundamental Forces—it accesses *absence* of Fundamental Forces. It's anti-magic, negative space, reality defined by what isn't there.

# 17. SHAMAN
## SPIRIT-SPEAKERS: THOSE WHO TRANSLATE BETWEEN WORLDS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Mediums**

Shamans emerged when first mortals realized: reality has layers. Physical world is visible, but spirit-world underlies it. Spirits everywhere—in stones, rivers, storms, dead. Some mortals could perceive this, communicate with it.

The first shamans were translators between worlds that coexist without understanding each other. Physical beings needed spirit-help. Spirits needed physical anchors. Shamans facilitated exchange.

> *"The world is not dead matter. The world is alive, conscious, speaking. We listen. We translate. We mediate. And both worlds become richer for the dialogue."* — The First Teaching

**First Age - Spirit-Guides**

Early shamans were tribal leaders, healers, judges—authorities because they could ask spirits for guidance. They developed **spirit-walking** (sending consciousness into spirit-realm while body remained), **spirit-binding** (calling spirits into physical objects), and **ancestor-speaking** (communing with tribal dead).

The **Totem Traditions** formed—each shaman bonding with specific spirit-type (bear-spirits, crow-spirits, storm-spirits, ancestor-spirits). These bonds defined shamanic identity.

Notable: **Vorga the Many-Spirited**—orc shaman who simultaneously channeled 47 different spirits, becoming living parliament of consciousness. She spoke with 47 voices, offered 47 perspectives, made decisions through internal debate. Eventually, spirits refused to leave. She became permanent spirit-vessel, losing individual identity.

**Second Age - The Divine Conflict**

During Rebellions, shamans faced crisis: gods demanded exclusive worship. But shamans served spirits, not gods. Some gods claimed spirits were their servants; spirits disagreed violently.

The **Spirit-Wars**: shamans siding with spirits against divine authority. Some shamans died. Some spirits died. Gods won politically but couldn't eliminate spirits (spirits are everywhere, always).

**Third Age - Tolerated Alternatives**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern couldn't suppress shamanism—too integrated into traditional cultures. Instead, the Pattern **reinterpreted** shamanism: spirits were "lesser divine servants," shamans were "informal clerics."

Shamans rejected this but couldn't prevent interpretation. The **Unbound Shamans**—practitioners refusing Pattern affiliation, maintaining pure spirit-communion.

**Ashkarra's Fall** devastated spirits more than mortals. Thousands of spirits died when World Anchor collapsed. Shamans felt every death—spiritual genocide causing mass shaman madness. Many still haven't recovered.

**Fourth Age - Spiritual Crisis**

Thread instability affects spirit-world catastrophically. Spirits becoming confused, aggressive, sometimes insane. World Tree corruption corrupts spirits. Eldritch contamination creates twisted spirit-abominations.

Shamans struggle to mediate between increasingly hostile spirit-world and desperate physical world. Both sides demanding impossible assistance.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Shamans feel Anchors as **spirit-constraints**—artificial order suppressing natural spiritual chaos. Near Anchors, spirit-world becomes muted, distant, difficult to contact. Shamans prefer operating outside Loom-Cities.

**Threads**: Each Thread attracts different spirits:
- **Fire Thread**: Fire-spirits, passion-entities, aggressive ancestors
- **Earth Thread**: Stone-spirits, ancestor-ghosts, enduring entities

**Leylines**: Shamans view leylines as **spirit-highways**—paths spirits naturally travel. Leyline nexuses are spirit-gathering points, where shamans can contact hundreds of entities simultaneously.

**Glyphs**: Shamans write **spirit-glyphs**—symbols visible only to spirits, communicating across worlds. These glyphs fade quickly in physical realm but persist indefinitely in spirit-realm.

**World Trees**: Near World Trees, spirit-world becomes crystal-clear. Shamans can physically *see* spirits, converse normally, even touch them. Many shamanic rituals must occur in World Tree groves.

**Flaws**: Shamans embody **Fear and Envy**—fear of death driving spirit-communion, envy of spirits' immortality/freedom. Their power comes from refusing to accept mortality as final.

**Spirits**: Shamans ARE spirit-authorities. They command, negotiate, befriend, battle spirits. Most shamans develop permanent spirit-companions dwelling in their souls.

**The Mediation Truth** (DM Knowledge):

Shamans don't channel power—they **broker** it. Spirits have power but lack physical presence. Physical beings have presence but lack spirit-power. Shamans trade: offering spirits physical anchor in exchange for spiritual assistance.

This makes shamanism symbiotic magic—cooperation rather than domination.

# 18. SORCERER

**The Loomless Time - The First Anomalies**

Sorcerers didn't choose magic—magic chose them. When Astralor wove the nine Fundamental Forces into reality, some mortals were born with fragments of those forces embedded in their souls. Not learned, not granted—**inherited**.

The first sorcerers terrified everyone, themselves included. Children manifesting elemental power through tantrums. Adolescents accidentally reshaping reality through emotional surges. Adults struggling to control forces they never asked for.

> *"I didn't learn to set things on fire. I was born burning. The question isn't how I use magic—it's how I stop using magic."* — Confessions of a Fire-Touched

**First Age - The Bloodline Mysteries**

Different bloodlines manifested different powers:
- **Dragon-Blooded**: Descended from dragon-mortal unions, manifesting scales, breath weapons, draconic arrogance
- **Fey-Touched**: Faerie ancestry causing reality-bending, charm magic, seasonal mood swings
- **Shadow-Born**: Void-touched bloodlines channeling darkness, absence, entropy
- **Divine-Sparks**: Distant godly ancestry manifesting divine magic without requiring faith
- **Elemental-Veined**: Pure elemental forces running through bloodstream

Notable: **The Sorcerous Emperors**—dragonborn dynasty that ruled Ashkarra through inherited fire-magic. Every emperor was sorcerer. This concentrated power terrifyingly.

**Second Age - The Breeding Wars**

During Rebellions, sorcerous bloodlines became strategic resources. Kingdoms arranged marriages to produce powerful offspring. The **Eugenics Academies** formed—institutions studying magical inheritance, attempting to engineer perfect sorcerers.

This created the **Sorcerous Revolts**: children refusing to be breeding stock, bloodlines destroying themselves rather than serving as magical livestock.

**Third Age - Controlled Lineages**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to regulate sorcerous bloodlines through **mandatory registration**. All sorcerers must report ancestry, submit to genetic monitoring, obtain permission to reproduce.

This failed spectacularly. Sorcerers concealed their nature, had children secretly, fled to regions beyond Pattern authority. The **Lost Bloodlines**—sorcerous families that disappeared rather than submit.

**Ashkarra's Fall** devastated dragonborn sorcerous lineages—the strongest concentration of inherited magic ever assembled. When the city fell, these bloodlines died or scattered. Some theorize dragonborn sorcerous concentration itself attracted Eldritch attention.

**Fourth Age - Wild Awakening**

Thread instability triggers **spontaneous sorcerous manifestation**—mortals with no magical ancestry suddenly developing innate powers. No one understands why. Some theorize: Thread chaos activates dormant potential in everyone.

This creates crisis: how do you regulate magic appearing randomly in previously-mundane families?

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Sorcerers feel Anchors as **resonance**—their internal magic vibrating in harmony with Anchor's stabilizing field. Near healthy Anchors, sorcerous power becomes controlled, refined, easier to manage. Near dying Anchors, sorcerous magic goes wild, uncontrollable.

**Threads**: Sorcerers *are* Threads in microcosm—their bloodlines carry elemental forces:
- **Fire-Blooded**: Stronger during Fire Thread, manifest flames unconsciously
- **Earth-Touched**: Denser during Earth Thread, become physically heavier
- **Water-Veined**: More fluid during Water Thread, bodies partially liquid
- **Air-Born**: Lighter during Air Thread, sometimes float
- **Spirit-Bound**: Most dangerous during Spirit Thread—consciousness fragments across timelines

**Leylines**: Sorcerers instinctively navigate toward leylines—their internal magic drawn to external flows. Powerful sorcerers develop leyline-sensitivity as acute as druids', though less understood.

**Glyphs**: Sorcerers don't typically *study* glyphs—they **embody** them. Their magic manifests as living glyphs written in flesh, blood, soul. Some sorcerers' skin shows faint glyphic patterns when using power.

**World Trees**: Near World Trees, sorcerous bloodlines "awaken"—dormant powers manifest, latent abilities surface. Many sorcerous families conduct awakening ceremonies in World Tree groves, triggering children's magical inheritance.

**Flaws**: Sorcerers embody **Pride**—cosmic wound driving existence toward self-assertion. They didn't earn power, but claim it anyway. This creates dangerous entitlement: "I deserve power because I have power."

**Spirits**: Sorcerers often attract **bloodline-spirits**—entities connected to their magical ancestry. Dragon-blooded attract draconic spirits. Fey-touched draw faerie attention. These spirits can be mentors, parasites, or ancestors.

**The Inheritance Mystery:**

Sorcerers channel power through **bloodline connection** to Fundamental Forces. Their ancestors (divine, draconic, fey, elemental) embedded cosmic fragments into genetic code. Each generation dilutes power, but never completely eliminates it.

**The Truth** (DM Knowledge):

- **Civilized Tamers**: Worked with trained animals in urban contexts

**World Anchors**: Tamers feel Anchors as **separation anxiety**—artificial order creating barrier between tamer and beast-consciousness. Near Anchors, empathic bonds weaken. Companions become more "animal," less "partner."

**Glyphs**: Tamers learn **beast-glyphs**—symbols allowing communication across species barriers. These glyphs transcend language, conveying pure meaning any conscious creature understands.

**World Trees**: Near World Trees, beast-bonds achieve clarity—tamers can share companion's senses completely, experience world from beast perspective. Many tamers conduct bonding ceremonies in World Tree groves.

**Flaws**: Tamers embody **Envy and Fear**—envy of animal freedom/simplicity, fear of loneliness driving them toward beast-companionship. Their power comes from refusing human isolation.

# 21. THAUMATURGE
## FLAW-SHAPERS: THOSE WHO WEAPONIZE REALITY'S IMPERFECTIONS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Discovering the Cracks**

Thaumaturges emerged when mortals noticed: reality has **flaws**. Not bugs to fix—features to exploit. The universe contains fundamental imperfections (Pride, Wrath, Fear, Envy) woven into its structure. Some mortals learned to channel these cosmic wounds directly.

The first thaumaturges didn't create Flaws—they recognized them. When Pride drives universe to prove worth, when Wrath defends boundaries, when Fear seeks control, when Envy desires improvement—reality itself channels Flaws. Thaumaturges simply amplify natural processes.

> *"The universe is broken. Fundamentally, beautifully, productively broken. We don't fix it. We use it."* — The Flaw-Speaker's Manifesto

**First Age - The Salutor Pacts**

Early thaumaturges discovered **Salutors**—extraplanar entities embodying Flaws. These beings existed in spaces between reality's cracks, feeding on cosmic imperfections. The first thaumaturge made a deal: if Salutor granted power, thaumaturge would create more Flaws to feed it.

This created symbiotic relationship: Salutors providing reality-manipulation abilities, thaumaturges generating emotional/behavioral Flaws through their actions. The more Pride, Wrath, Fear, Envy a thaumaturge created, the stronger their Salutor connection.

Notable: **Tharivol the Crack-Maker**—first recorded thaumaturge who bonded with all four primary Salutors simultaneously. His existence created reality-distortions wherever he traveled. Cities near him experienced increased crime, paranoia, arrogance, rage. When he died, relief wave passed through continent—like infected wound finally draining.

During Rebellions, thaumaturges faced moral reckoning: their power required creating negative emotions/behaviors in others. Every spell fed Salutors, who demanded payment in suffering.

This split thaumaturgy into factions:
- **Pragmatic Thaumaturges**: Accepted ethical cost, used power for "greater good"
- **Ascetic Thaumaturges**: Paid Flaw-costs themselves, refusing to harm others
- **Hedonistic Thaumaturges**: Embraced corruption, maximized Salutor feeding

**Third Age - Suppressed Knowledge**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern banned thaumaturgy completely—teaching/practicing became capital crime. The **Salutor Purges**: systematic execution of thaumaturges, destruction of grimoires, eradication of knowledge.

But Salutors can't die—they're cosmic principles made conscious. When thaumaturges died, Salutors simply waited, knowing more would discover them eventually.

**Ashkarra's Fall** involved massive thaumaturgic working—attempts to channel Eldritch Moons' power through Flaw-manipulation. This backfired catastrophically, possibly causing rather than preventing catastrophe.

**Fourth Age - Desperate Resurrection**

Thread instability makes traditional magic unreliable. But thaumaturgy, powered by fundamental cosmic Flaws rather than Thread system, remains stable. Desperate authorities quietly employ thaumaturges despite bans.

This creates ethical nightmare: accepting thaumaturgic help means accepting Flaw-amplification. Cities hiring thaumaturges experience increased crime, paranoia, social dysfunction—but gain Thread-independent power.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Thaumaturges feel Anchors as **Flaw-suppressors**—artificial order reducing cosmic imperfections. Near healthy Anchors, thaumaturgic power diminishes (fewer Flaws to exploit). Near dying Anchors, power intensifies (Flaws multiplying as order collapses).

**Threads**: Thaumaturges exist *outside* Thread system—their power predates and transcends it. Thread alignments affect them minimally, making them valuable during Thread chaos.

**Leylines**: Thaumaturges use leylines to **spread Flaws**—channeling Pride/Wrath/Fear/Envy along ley-paths, infecting distant regions. Dangerous: creates cascading social dysfunction.

**Glyphs**: Thaumaturges write **Flaw-glyphs**—symbols that amplify cosmic imperfections in observers. Reading these glyphs creates immediate emotional response (arrogance, rage, terror, jealousy).

**World Trees**: Near World Trees, thaumaturgy becomes extremely dangerous—Trees amplify all magic, including Flaw-manipulation. Minor thaumaturgic spell near World Tree can corrupt entire forest.

**Flaws**: Thaumaturges ARE Flaw-specialists—their entire power system revolves around weaponizing cosmic imperfections. They embody all four Flaws simultaneously while trying to resist corruption.

**Spirits**: Salutors are thaumaturges' primary spirits—extraplanar entities feeding on Flaws. But thaumaturges also attract **corruption-spirits**: entities born from accumulated negative emotions.

*Secret Thaumaturges*

Thaumaturges access the **Universal Glyphs** through *inversion*—channeling not what reality is, but what reality fails to be. The cosmic Flaws (Pride, Wrath, Fear, Envy) are wounds in the Fundamental Forces themselves—places where creation's ambition exceeded its capability.

Salutors are entities that evolved to feed on these wounds, existing in conceptual spaces between what reality is and what it wishes to be.

# 22. WARLOCK
## PACT-BOUND: THOSE WHO TRADE SOUL FOR POWER

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Bargains**

Warlocks emerged when mortals realized: powerful entities want mortal servants, mortals want power—trade possible. The first warlock made simple deal: "Grant me strength. I'll serve you."

Both parties profited. The entity gained physical-world agent. The mortal gained supernatural power. Arrangement repeated across history, each time unique, each time binding.

> *"I didn't earn my power. Didn't inherit it. Didn't discover it. I bought it. And I'm still paying."* — The Warlock's Confession

**First Age - The Patron Pantheon**

Early warlocks bonded with various entities:
- **Archfey**: Capricious fey lords granting reality-bending power for entertainment
- **Fiends**: Devils/demons trading power for souls, service, corruption
- **Great Old Ones**: Eldritch entities granting knowledge that destroys sanity
- **Celestials**: Angels demanding righteous service in exchange for holy power
- **Primordials**: Ancient elementals requiring environmental service

Each patron type demanded different payment, granted different abilities, left different scars.

Notable: **The Thirteen Doomed**—first recorded warlocks who attempted to serve thirteen different patrons simultaneously. All went mad within days. Their screaming created permanent audio-echo at pact-site, still audible 4,000 years later.

**Second Age - The Contract Wars**

During Rebellions, warlocks became common—desperate mortals trading anything for power to survive. The **Pact-Plague**: thousands simultaneously contracting with fiends, creating hell-on-earth as devils collected payments.

This led to the **Warlock Codes**: standard contract templates preventing worst exploitation. Still binding, still dangerous, but less likely to end in eternal damnation.

**Third Age - Regulated Servitude**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern attempted to ban warlock pacts—interfered with Pattern theology. But couldn't prevent mortals from contracting with entities beyond Pattern authority.

Instead, the **Licensed Warlocks**: practitioners who registered pacts, submitted to monitoring, proved their servitude didn't threaten Thread stability.

**Ashkarra's Fall** involved multiple warlocks attempting to break pacts simultaneously—reasoning that city's destruction released all contracts. This failed. Patrons simply relocated surviving warlocks, continuing collection.

**Fourth Age - Desperate Pacts**

Thread instability drives desperate mortals toward warlock pacts. When magic fails and gods go silent, patrons still answer. Entities outside Thread system offer Thread-independent power—for price.

Current surge in pact-making creates concern: what happens when thousands of debt-payments come due simultaneously?

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Warlocks feel Anchors as **competing authority**—reality stabilized artificially versus patron-granted power. Near Anchors, some pact-abilities weaken (patron's influence constrained). Near dying Anchors, pact-bonds intensify (patron senses vulnerability).

**Threads**: Warlock power mostly bypasses Threads—flowing directly from patron to servant. But Thread alignments affect patron moods:
- **Fire Thread**: Fiend-patrons become aggressive, demanding
- **Water Thread**: Fey-patrons become capricious, playful
- **Spirit Thread**: All patrons become more present, sometimes physically manifest

**Leylines**: Patrons use leylines as **communication channels**—contacting warlocks across impossible distances. Strong leylines allow clearer patron-contact, sometimes enabling physical manifestation.

**Glyphs**: Warlocks learn **pact-glyphs**—symbols written into their souls during contract-making. These glyphs bind warlock to patron, enable power-transfer, enforce contract terms.

**World Trees**: Pacts made near World Trees become unbreakable—Trees witness contracts, enforce terms through natural law. Smart warlocks avoid World Tree pacts; desperate ones seek them (reliability worth inflexibility).

**Flaws**: Warlocks embody all Flaws through patron-service:
- **Pride**: Believing they outsmarted patron
- **Wrath**: Channeling patron's anger
- **Fear**: Terror of contract breach
- **Envy**: Desiring patron's own power

**Spirits**: Patrons ARE spirits (or gods, or demons, or eldritch entities—terminology varies). Warlock existence proves: spirits grant power if sufficiently motivated.

### Regional Variations

**Elarion**

*Corvessa - The Sanctified Pacts*
- Warlocks serving celestial/divine patrons
- Contract with Pattern-approved entities
- Considered holy servants, not soul-sellers
- Philosophy: all clerics are warlocks who won't admit it

**Vashkora**

*Veyzul Contract-Aristocracy*
- Noble houses maintaining hereditary pact-bloodlines
- Children inherit parents' contracts
- Born already bound, already indebted
- Philosophy: power worth any price

**Underground**

*Drow Vel'Zyren Warlocks*
- All drow technically warlocks—bound to goddess
- Most powerful pact-servants alive
- Pay in pain, suffering, service
- Philosophy: servitude is purpose

### Source of Power

**The Binding Truth** (DM Knowledge):

Warlock pacts create **karmic exchange**—mortals trade future (soul, service, freedom) for present (power, knowledge, ability). Patrons access mortal world through warlock connection; warlocks access patron's power through same link.

This makes warlocks living bridges between planes—neither fully mortal nor fully spiritual.

### Philosophy of Power

> *"They judge us for selling souls. But everyone sells something. Time. Freedom. Integrity. We're just honest about transaction.*
>
> *I made deal. Knew the cost. Accepted the terms. Gained power. Now paying price. Simple. Clear. Fair.*
>
> *They say I'm damned. Maybe. But I'm also powerful. Effective. Useful. And when Thread system fails and gods go silent, my patron still answers.*
>
> *Who's really damned? Me with my contract? Or them with their pretense of independence while serving systems they don't understand?*
>
> *At least I read the fine print."*

— **The Pact-Holder's Truth**

# 23. WIZARD
## SCHOLARS OF THE ARCANE: THOSE WHO STUDY MAGIC AS SCIENCE

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - The First Questions**

Wizards emerged when mortals asked: *How does this work?* Not "who grants power" or "what should I believe"—simply "what are the mechanisms?"

The first wizards were researchers, experimenters, theorists. They observed magical phenomena, hypothesized explanations, tested predictions. Magic as science, not faith.

> *"Clerics pray and receive. Sorcerers manifest and accept. We *understand*. And understanding is greatest power."* — The First Principle

**First Age - The Glyphic Revolution**

Early wizards discovered **Secondary Glyphs**—mortal-accessible symbols that channel cosmic forces. This changed everything. Magic became reproducible, teachable, improvable.

The **First Academies** formed:
- Elven **Living Library**: Preserving knowledge in elder-memory
- Dwarven **Rune-Colleges**: Studying glyphic theory systematically
- Human **Arcane Universities**: Teaching magic as academic discipline

Notable: **Elensar Glyph-Reader**—elf who discovered glyphs weren't invented but *discovered*. They existed before mortals, waiting to be recognized. This suggested: magic follows laws, and laws can be learned.

**Second Age - The Magical Revolution**

During Rebellions, wizards proved mortals could reshape reality without divine permission. The **Arcane Uprising**: wizards matching clerics, sometimes defeating them, through superior understanding.

This created the **Battle of Ontologies**: clerics claiming power from gods, wizards claiming power from understanding. Both worked, suggesting multiple valid paths to same result.

**Third Age - The Academy Age**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern embraced wizardry—systematized magic aligned with Pattern philosophy. The **Great Academies** (Kel Varyan's Prisada Arcana, Veltharyn's institutes, Thar Vornhal's theory-colleges) became civilization's intellectual centers.

**Ashkarra's Fall** killed thousands of wizards attempting final defense through coordinated spellcasting. The **Ritual of Twelve Thousand**: every wizard in empire casting simultaneously to repel Eldritch corruption. Failed catastrophically—coordination impossible at that scale.

**Fourth Age - Theory vs. Reality**

Thread instability challenges wizard worldview: if magic follows consistent laws, why does it fail unpredictably? Two responses:
- **Conservative Wizards**: Laws still apply, we just don't fully understand them yet
- **Revolutionary Wizards**: Laws are breaking down, new theories needed

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Wizards can *measure* World Anchors precisely—creating instruments that detect Thread strength, stability indices, failure probability. This makes wizards essential infrastructure—they predict Anchor death before it happens.

**Threads**: Wizards understand Threads as **energy states**—different configurations of cosmic forces. Their spells work by channeling current Thread-alignment into desired effect.

**Leylines**: Wizards map leylines mathematically, creating **leyline calculus**—equations predicting magical flow. Some wizard schools teach navigation through pure mathematics.

**Glyphs**: Wizards ARE glyph-specialists—their entire tradition revolves around understanding/manipulating Secondary Glyphs. Advanced wizards attempt decoding Primary Glyphs (mostly fatal).

**World Trees**: Wizards study World Trees as **natural laboratories**—perfect examples of magic functioning without mortal intervention. Understanding Trees might unlock magic's deepest secrets.

**Flaws**: Wizards embody **Pride and Envy**—pride in intellectual achievement, envy driving competitive research. Their power comes from refusing to accept ignorance.

**Spirits**: Wizards work with **knowledge-spirits**—entities embodying information, research, discovery. Some wizards become so absorbed in study they become knowledge-spirits themselves.

### Regional Variations

**Elarion**

*Kel Varyan - The Bureaucratic Mages*
- State-employed wizards maintaining Thread system
- Academic credentials required for advancement
- Most organized, least creative wizards
- Philosophy: magic serves society through structure

*Veltharyn - The Artificer-Theorists*
- Combine wizardry with engineering
- Create magical technology, not just spells
- Most innovative wizards alive
- Philosophy: magic is tool, improve tools constantly

*Corvessa - The War-Mages*
- Military wizards specialized in combat magic
- Extensive evocation/abjuration training
- Philosophy: magic is weapon, master weaponry

### Source of Power

**The Understanding Truth** (DM Knowledge):

Wizards access all nine Fundamental Forces through **intellectual comprehension**. They're not channeling divine power or inheriting bloodlines—they're directly manipulating cosmic source code through understanding its syntax.

This makes wizards potentially most powerful class—unlimited by patron, god, or bloodline. Limited only by intelligence and lifespan.

### Philosophy of Power

> *"Clerics pray. Sorcerers inherit. Warlocks contract. We *learn*.*
>
> *Every spell in my book represents hours of study, weeks of practice, months of theory. I earned each incantation. Proved each formula. Understood each principle.*
>
> *This makes me slower than sorcerer, less reliable than cleric, weaker than warlock. Initially.*
>
> *But I improve. Every day, every study session, every experiment. Sorcerers peak. Clerics plateau. Warlocks stagnate. I progress.*
>
> *Because understanding compounds. Today's discovery builds on yesterday's. Tomorrow's breakthrough uses today's foundation.*
>
> *They have power. I have knowledge. And knowledge, given sufficient time, surpasses all power."*

— **The Wizard's Pride**

# 24. WITCH
## TRADITION-KEEPERS: THOSE WHO REMEMBER THE OLD WAYS

### Origins Across the Ages

**The Loomless Time - Before Magic Had Rules**

Witches are oldest magical tradition—practicing magic before it was systematized, categorized, or understood. When first mortals discovered they could affect reality through will, word, and symbolic action, witchcraft began.

The first witches learned through tradition: this herb cures, this symbol wards, this chant calls rain. Not theory—practice. Not understanding—results.

> *"Wizards ask 'why does this work?' We ask 'does this work?' If yes, sufficient. Why is luxury for those with time."* — The Witch's Pragmatism

**First Age - The Folk Traditions**

Early witches were community healers, wise women/men, cunning folk. They practiced **sympathetic magic** (affecting things through symbolic connection), **herbalism** (mundane and magical), **divination** (reading patterns), and **curse-craft** (directed malice).

Different cultures developed different witchcraft:
- **Corvessan Hedge-Witches**: Rural practitioners preserving pre-Faith traditions
- **Kel Varyan Sea-Witches**: Coastal magic workers controlling weather/tides
- **Talanthar Greenwitches**: Forest practitioners indistinguishable from druids
- **Drow Shadow-Witches**: Darkness and poison specialists

Notable: **The Thirteen Mothers**—first recorded witch coven, establishing principle: individual witches are powerful, but covens are exponentially stronger.

**Second Age - The Persecution**

During Rebellions, witches faced systematic suppression. **The Burning Times**: religious authorities executing witches as heretics, destroying traditional knowledge, forcing practice underground.

But witchcraft survives through oral tradition—knowledge embedded in songs, stories, recipes. Can't burn what lives in memory.

**Third Age - The Hidden Art**

The Faith of the Bound Pattern officially banned witchcraft (inferior to glyphic magic, they claimed). But couldn't eliminate it—too embedded in rural communities, too useful for everyday problems.

The **Twilight Practice**: witches operating semi-legally, tolerated when useful, persecuted when convenient.

**Fourth Age - The Return**

Thread instability revives witchcraft—when sophisticated magic fails, traditional methods persist. Suddenly, hedge-witches who've been quietly practicing for centuries become essential.

### Interaction with Cosmic Systems

**World Anchors**: Witches feel Anchors as **artificial constraint**—imposed order suppressing natural magic. Witchcraft works best outside Loom-Cities, in wild places where old ways persist.

**Threads**: Witches use Threads intuitively, without formal understanding:
- Harvest under proper Thread for maximum potency
- Cast hexes when Threads align malevolently
- Brew potions during Thread-transitions

**Leylines**: Witches identify leylines through dowsing, not mathematics. Build shrines on nexuses through instinct, not measurement.

**Glyphs**: Witches use **folk-glyphs**—traditional symbols passed down generations. Often identical to Secondary Glyphs, discovered independently through trial-and-error.

**World Trees**: Witches revere World Trees as sacred—gather materials from Tree-groves, conduct rituals in Tree-shadow, defend Trees against corruption.

**Flaws**: Witches embody **Envy**—preserving old ways while watching new ways gain prestige. Their power comes from refusing to abandon tradition despite marginalization.

**Spirits**: Witches work with **familiar spirits**—small entities (cats, crows, toads, owls) serving as magical assistants, scouts, companions.

### Regional Variations

**Elarion**

*Corvessa - The Hedge-Witches*
- Rural practitioners preserving pre-Pattern magic
- Officially illegal, tacitly tolerated
- Provide healing, love-charms, curse-breaking
- Philosophy: old ways work, don't need approval

*Talanthar - The Greenwitches*
- Forest-magic specialists
- Indistinguishable from druids to outsiders
- Work with plants, animals, natural spirits
- Philosophy: forest is first tradition

**Kerythra**

*Nomadic Wise-Women*
- Traveling witches serving multiple clans
- Maintain knowledge through oral tradition
- Never write anything down (prevents theft/destruction)
- Philosophy: memory is stronger than paper

**Vashkora**

*Desert Crone-Circles*
- Elder witches who've survived decades in harsh conditions
- Most respected witches alive
- Keep pre-dragonborn traditions
- Philosophy: survival proves wisdom

**Mirror Chain**

*Sea-Witches*
- Ocean magic specialists
- Control weather, calm storms, call fish
- Essential to island survival
- Philosophy: ocean teaches, we learn

### Source of Power

**The Traditional Truth** (DM Knowledge):

Witchcraft accesses all Fundamental Forces through **accumulated cultural wisdom**. Traditions that work persist; traditions that fail are forgotten. Across millennia, effective magic separates from superstition through brutal evolutionary pressure.

This makes witchcraft empirically valid—it works because it's been tested across thousands of generations.

### Philosophy of Power

> *"Wizards study in towers. Clerics pray in temples. We practice in kitchens.*
>
> *Our magic is humble. Practical. Unglamorous. We cure illness, protect homes, ensure harvests. Not dramatic. Not prestigious. But necessary.*
>
> *They theorize about Primary Glyphs. We brew tea that actually helps. They debate Thread mechanics. We deliver babies safely. They chase cosmic truth. We solve daily problems.*
>
> *Who's more useful? Scholar understanding everything, accomplishing nothing? Or practitioner understanding little, accomplishing much?*
>
> *We are old. Older than their academies. Older than their temples. Older than their theories.*
>
> *And we'll be here after they're forgotten. Because practical always outlasts prestigious."*

— **The Witch's Wisdom**

# CONCLUSION: THE TWENTY-FOUR PATHS

These twenty-four classes represent every way mortals have discovered to channel, manipulate, or embody power in Dór-o Estel. From the barbarian's primal rage to the witch's traditional wisdom, from the paladin's absolute conviction to the thaumaturge's flaw-exploitation—each path offers unique perspective on reality's nature.

Some classes (clerics, paladins, warlocks) rely on external entities. Others (wizards, monks, fighters) depend on personal development. Some (druids, shamans, rangers) work with nature. Others (inventors, runesmiths, tattooists) manipulate it. Some (psions, shadowbinders, sangromancers) embrace darkness. Others (bards, sorcerers, magi) synthesize multiple traditions.

But all twenty-four share common truth: power requires sacrifice. Time, blood, freedom, sanity, morality—everyone pays. The question isn't whether to pay, but what currency you'll accept and what price you're willing to become.

In the current Thread crisis, as reality itself becomes uncertain and the Pattern's authority weakens, these twenty-four paths may be all that prevents civilization's complete collapse. Or they may be what tears it apart.

Time will tell. The world watches. The Ages continue.

And the classes endure.
