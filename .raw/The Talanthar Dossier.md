PART B — FIELD JOURNAL OF LEGATE M. VHAILEN (486–487 F.A.)

(Personal copy, sealed under Imperial cipher. Extracts transcribed posthumously by order of the Collegium Archivum.)

I. The Greenwood Threshold

> Day 31 of the Fourth Month
The first sight of Talanthar’s border was not a gate, nor a wall, but a forest that decided I could enter.
The two fortresses, Vaelwatch and Stonebrook, stand upon high cliffs where the twin rivers Thalven and Aris plunge into misted ravines. Between them lie rope bridges, griffon aerie towers, and the ruins of older Imperial siegeworks—overgrown, unacknowledged, and strangely silent.
I was permitted through not by order, but by consensus. A Moon-elf ranger and a halfling elder conferred, both listening to the trees before answering my request.
They spoke the same words: “The forest says yes, today.”




---

II. The Descent

> Day 35
The rivers do not flow as rivers should. They vanish into stone, reappear in air, and tumble into dark mouths that breathe light instead of shadow.
My escort guided me down steps carved into the living rock. What I expected to be caverns were instead cathedrals—vast halls veined with glowing lichen, suspended gardens of vines and crystal roots.
Bridges of wood and bone crossed black water where enormous fish drifted like ghosts. Children dangled fishing lines woven of spider-silk that gleamed in pale gold.
I had come to learn of a wild heresy. I found instead a city as deliberate and beautiful as any built by Imperial hand.




---

III. The City of Talanthar

> Day 39
The city does not stand upon the land—it grows through it.
Towers of carved petrified wood rise from the cliffsides; homes cling to stalactites like lanterns; the air hums with bioluminescent vines that sway as though stirred by unseen currents.
The twin rivers merge in the chasm’s heart, forming an underground lake called the Mirror of Vaelthir, where the World Tree’s roots descend and vanish into darkness.
There is sound everywhere: bells carved of crystal mushrooms, the low chanting of druids tending to glowing fruit groves, and distant griffon cries from above the cliffs.
The people are clean, educated, and disciplined—but not in any Imperial sense. Order here is organic, alive, adaptive. There are no paved streets, yet everyone seems to know the path they must take.




---

IV. The Outlying Hamlets

> Day 44
Beyond the core caverns, small hamlets dot the forest—clusters of round homes carved into tree bases or stone hollows. They hunt, fish, and tend flocks of pale forest goats.
Each hamlet has a stone circle, a druid’s post, and a bloodwell—a small basin of moss-covered stone where offerings are made to the land.
The people speak of the forest as one might of a beloved ancestor—sometimes patient, sometimes cruel, but always listening.
Strangers who tread without offering vanish. Those who offer wrongly are “forgotten.”




---

V. The Truth of the ‘Wild’

> Day 50
Corvessa calls this place heresy, barbarism, witchcraft.
I call it discipline beyond our comprehension.
Every tree, every creature, every spell has purpose. Nothing is wasted. Children learn both healing and combat as equal duties. Hunters whisper apologies to their prey. Blood feeds the land, and the land returns health, metal, even light.
I saw an elf mend a shattered bridge with song, watched halflings race raptors through fungal forests, and witnessed a druid calm a storm by cutting her palm and pressing it to the earth.
If this is uncivilized, then perhaps civilization has misunderstood itself.




---

VI. The Departure

> Day 60
I leave tomorrow by the river path. The forest hums louder every night—an awareness, a farewell.
My escorts told me the forest now “remembers” me.
That is not reassurance.
I find myself whispering back when the leaves move.
And sometimes the echoes answer.
