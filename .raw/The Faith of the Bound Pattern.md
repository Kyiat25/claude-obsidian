# **The Creed of the Fivefold Loom**
 _As held by the Faithful of the Grand Loom in the Cities of Light_

> _"Before fire burned, before earth rose, before the stars sang, there was the Weave. And from it came the gods—not as kings, but as voices. Some loud. Some soft. Some... wrong."_  
> — From the [[Book of Threads]]_, Chapter I, Verse 1

---
## 📖 **The Five that Spun the World**
**The Common Creation myth**

Long ago—so long that even [[elves]] forget—the world was not made by gods, but by **[[The Weave]]**: a great tapestry of **threads and glyphs**, hidden patterns and eternal cycles.

Then, from the Fivefold Loom, the **first great forces** arose. These were **not yet gods**, but **primal powers**:

1. **The Hammer** struck sparks—**Fire** was born.
2. **The Wheel** turned endlessly—**Water** flowed.
3. **The Loom** pulled tight—**Spirit** wove memory into time.
4. **The Breath** gave motion—**Air** danced.
5. **The Stone** stood firm—**Earth** formed.

The Fivefold became **The World**.

Each force birthed **pantheons**—gods formed by belief, conflict, and myth. They did not agree, but **their discord created balance**.

This is why, say the priests, **magic flows**, **cities rise**, **storms come**, and **death waits**.

---
## **📜 What the Faith Believes of Glyphs**  

The clergy teach that **the Elemental Glyphs are the divine script**—the Pattern itself made visible. While certain symbols tied to memory, identity, or destiny (often called _soul-marks_ or _threads of self_) appear in rituals or prophetic trances, these are not seen as part of any deeper system.  

All other glyphic speculation—such as whispered layers beneath the Pattern—is heresy or madness.

## 🕊️ **Icon of the Loom Eternal**
**Religious Symbol**
### Visual Description

**Central Motif**:

- A **circle of interwoven threads**, forming a **five-pointed spiral**—each point represents one of the Five Pantheons (Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Spirit).
- The **threads never meet at the centre**—they curve around an **empty core**, representing the unknowable Primal Glyphs and the space where **Eldritch corruption seeps**.

**Thread Colours** (suggested for visual renditions):

- 🔥 Fire: Deep red-orange strand, angular and jagged.
- 🌊 Water: Blue spiral, looping and fluid.
- 🌍 Earth: Dark stone-gray line, thick and unmoving.
- 🌪 Air: Pale silver swirl, broken in sections, lightly jagged.
- 🌌 Spirit: Violet-white filament with star-like glimmers.

**Outer Ring**:

- Inscribed with **five primary glyphs**—not readable runes, but abstract divine symbols representing Order, Change, Memory, Power, and Fate.

**Hidden Mark**:

- At the bottom-right, just outside the main spiral, is a **fractured sixth glyph**—a cracked mark or spiral fragment. It is the **Fray**, the sign of Eldritch corruption.
- **Some faithful carve it deliberately**, to remind worshippers that the Loom must be guarded.

---
### **Symbolic Meaning**

> _"All threads circle the void. But none may enter it."_

- **The Spiral** = The Pattern of the Weave, ever turning.
- **The Threads** = The Five Pantheons—equal, interwoven, never dominant.
- **The Hollow Center** = The Primal Void and the lurking threat of unraveling.
- **The Outer Ring** = The visible world, magic, and mortal understanding.
- **The Fray Mark** = A whispered warning: the gods are not alone.
---
### 🛐 **Common Uses in Society**

- **Printed on banners** in guild halls and high temples.
- **Stamped into wax seals** of priestly decrees.
- **Worn as pendants** by Weave-Readers—each thread color worn according to their domain.
- **Etched into magical tomes** to protect against glyph corruption.
- **Tattooed** on devoted monks—spiraling from their shoulder inward toward the heart.

## 🕯️ **In-World Stylisation (Temple Art, Scrollwork, Vestments)**

### **Scrollwork / Prayerbook Design**

- **Drawn with gold ink** on midnight-blue vellum.
- The five spiralling threads are made of **thin calligraphic strokes**, textured differently:
    - 🔥 _Fire_: Red with soot-smudge edging
    - 🌊 _Water_: Indigo with silver droplets
    - 🌍 _Earth_: Black ink pressed deep into the page
    - 🌪 _Air_: Iridescent ink that shifts in light
    - 🌌 _Spirit_: Faint purple with starburst dots along its path
- The center is left **untouched**, often painted matte black or gilded in black pearl.
- **Marginalia glyphs** are placed around the edges—no two scribes use the same glyphs, reflecting the unknowable nature of divine truth.

---
### **Cathedral or Temple Iconography**

- Carved into the **stone altar front**, with **inlaid mosaic threads** made of enchanted glass.
- In major temples, the icon is rendered in **stained glass** above the altar, and during sunrise, each color shines upon the central dais.

---
### **Weave-Reader Vestments**

- Robes feature the **five-thread spiral embroidered over the heart**, with the hollow center a patch of untouched fabric.
- High Weave-Readers wear cloaks edged in the **glyph-ring**, the frayed glyph appearing only on ceremonial robes or when Eldritch corruption is feared.
- The **color of their sash** corresponds to their chosen Thread (e.g., crimson for Fire, silver for Air).

## 🕊️ **The Cults of The Loom**
**The Religion of The People**

In the cities of men—especially the great city of **[[Kel Varyan]]**, the Citadel of Spires—the dominant belief is that **all gods are threads**, and **mortals are the needle**.

This gave rise to the **Cult of the Loom**—a **unified temple faith** that allows worship of any pantheon as long as it fits within the **Great Pattern**.

The faith is divided into **Five Sanctums**, one for each Element and Spirit:

| **Sanctum**                           | **Domain** | **Role in the Faith**                           |
| ------------------------------------- | ---------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| 🔥 _[[Sanctum of the Ember-Thread]]_  | Fire       | Represents ambition, rule, and forging destiny. |
| 🌊 _[[Sanctum of the Deep Thread]]_   | Water      | Embodies cycles, healing, endurance, and truth. |
| 🌪️ _[[Sanctum of the Storm-Thread]]_ | Air        | Reveres change, freedom, rebellion, and fate.   |
| 🌍 _[[Sanctum of the Rooted Thread]]_ | Earth      | Stands for law, memory, death, and defense.     |
| 🌌 _[[Sanctum of the Woven Thread]]_  | Spirit     | Honors magic, fate, dreams, and soul.           |

Each city may emphasize a different sanctum based on history, but **no single thread is to be pulled alone**, or **the Pattern may tear**.

## 🕳️ **The Whispered Heresy – The Threads that Unweave**

Though the Cult of the Loom teaches that **all threads have a place**, there are some that do **not belong**. These are not part of the Fivefold Loom.

They are **the Frayed Threads**, the **Null Threads**, the gods of **Unbeing**.

Worship of these **Eldritch beings** is outlawed—but **not uncommon**.

Many know the signs:

- A **child forgotten by their parents** though they were just spoken of.
- A **temple with no doors**, but everyone remembers praying there.
- A **name that echoes in dreams**, though no one alive bears it.

These whispers belong to **[[the Cult of the Null Reflection]]**, the heretics who say the Weave **can be unmade**.

> _“Some say they are evil. Others say they are older than the gods. The Loom says only this: cut not what you do not understand.”_

## 🌆 **Religion in City life – Gods of the Streets and Stars**

For the average human, gods are **practical**. They pray for things that matter:

- **[[Ignis]]**, for strength to rule or build.
- **[[Nereus]]**, for luck at sea or in childbirth.
- **[[Aelys]]**, for protection from unstable magic.
- **[[Zephir]]**, when whispering rebellion in a tavern.
- **[[Torvax]]**, for a good death.

Temples are **multi-faith**, with shrines to each Pantheon. Priests are known as **"[[Weave-Readers]]"**, and wear robes patterned with **interlocking glyphs**. Each priest focuses on a “thread” or domain.

# The Faith of the Bound Pattern

A **dominant, city-spanning religion** practiced widely across Loom-Cities.

- **Worships all five elemental pantheons** (Fire, Water, Earth, Air, Spirit).
- Recognizes the **Weave** and its five glyphic layers (Primal, Universal, Elemental, Form, Personal) as divine structure.
- Led by **[[Threadbearers]]**, one from each elemental alignment, rotating leadership in cyclical order.
- Rooted in **pragmatic harmony, magical balance**, and **symbolic reincarnation**.
- Supported by gods like **[[Aelys]], [[Thamur]], [[Ignis]], [[Thirian]], [[Nyzara]]**.

---
### **Elemental Pantheon Cults**

Each element has its own pantheon and religious sects, often localised.

- **[[The Fire Pantheon]]**: Worships gods like **[[Ignis]]**, **[[Soltharion]]**, and **[[Tharamis]]**—focused on creation, ambition, and order.
    - Fire's religion promotes civilisation, rulership, and sometimes harsh law.
- **[[The Water Pantheon]]**: Worships **[[Nereus]]**, **[[Vaelora]]**, and others—centers around secrets, endurance, and inevitability.
    - Often reflects memory, patience, and deep truths.
- **[[The Earth Pantheon]]**: Includes **[[Thamur]]**, **[[Vatharun]]**, **[[Gaeldros]]**—earth worship is about sealing, endurance, and holding back ruin.
    - Has an ancient, containment-focused structure; highly ritualistic.
- **[[The Air Pantheon]]**: Led by gods like **[[Zephir]]**, **[[Veydras]]**, and **[[Astria]]**—champions freedom, chaos, and fate-defiance.
    - Worship is often informal or tied to wanderers, rebels, and visionaries.
- **[[The Spirit Pantheon]]**: Includes **[[Aelys]]**, **[[Nyzara]]**, **The Restbringer**, and **Echoed Mother**—a mystical religion focused on fate, memory, death, and rebirth.
    - They are **guardians of the Weave**, especially in spiritual and magical rituals.

# 📜 **The History of the Faith of the Bound Pattern**

> _“Threads fray. Threads return. The Pattern holds.”_  
> — Inscription upon the Crown of Threads in Kel Varyan

---
## 🧶 **Loomless Time – That Which Was Not Yet Woven**

Before there were Threads, there was motion. Mortals lived by instinct, gods by will, magic by fracture. The Weave existed but was not named. The Faith did not exist.

Some of its symbols—spirals, braids, echoes—can be seen in cave markings and early leyline art, but no one then knew what they meant. These were the **first fractures of remembrance**, scattered through myth like unanchored stitches.

---
## ⚔️ **First Age – The Age of Savageness and Heresy**

> _“In this age, gods fought blindly. Mortals bled meaning. Magic cracked the world.”_

The world fractured under divine arrogance and mortal desperation. Elemental cults warred over the skies and seas. Spirit-worship turned into blood-rites and madness. Arcane glyph-cults arose, barely understanding the forces they invoked.

The Weave was **manipulated, not honoured**. Its Threads pulled in all directions, unanchored. The **pantheons warred for supremacy**, believing themselves **masters** of the Weave rather than **its passengers**.

In this age:

- **The First Leyline Corruption** occurred (later hidden by the Earth Pantheon).
- **Cities rose and collapsed** without memory.
- Prophecy fractured, often leading to destruction.

There was no unified Faith. Only heresies and violent revelations.

Some proto-spiral cults may have existed in caves, ruins, or whispers—but they were hunted down or consumed by their own contradictions.

---
## 🔥 **Second Age – Threads Found in Ash and Fire**

> _“A pattern emerged in the dying light of fallen cities.”_

The Faith began as a **scattered glyph-cult**, first recorded in burned scrolls recovered from the ruins of a proto-city near what would become [[Veltharyn]]. These early followers were called **Threadseekers**—scholars, weavers, and war-orphans who saw repeating signs in ruins, rituals, and sky-markings.

Their belief: **divinity is pattern**, and **the Weave binds even gods**.

Their doctrine was heretical to the old empires, especially the Dragonborn of Ashkarra. Yet small groups of humans, halflings, and memory-priests among the elves carried the **spiral sign** in secret.

> The Spiral became a symbol not of power—but of consequence.

These early cults remained fragmented, often reabsorbed into druidic circles, arcane guilds, or banned entirely. Yet they endured. They grew in power and influence.

---
## 🪨 **Third Age – The Anchorless Spiral**

> _“The Pattern seeks balance. We pulled too far, and it tore.”_

In the wake of magical collapse and divine uncertainty, the Threadseekers’ doctrines grew. Their glyphs matched unstable leyline pulses. Their myths matched forgotten prophecies. Their **pattern-recognition** helped rebuild collapsed cities, and many survivors turned to their guidance.

The Faith formalized, naming its five Threads, finding and then sanctifying what would become the [[World Anchors]]. Great works of labour, art and magic. [[Loom-Cities]] rose in [[Ashkarra]], [[Kel Varyan]], [[Veltharyn]], [[Selveran]],and [[07_Geography/02_Cities/Thar Vornhal]]—but the understanding of the Pattern remained immature.

The religion started gaining traction, influence within the [[Humans]] kingdoms and then the dual state religion of the [[Dragonborn Empire]]. With this, they started perscuating what they saw as heresy and this involved the felling of the [[07_Geography/04_World Trees/World Trees]]

Then came the **[[Fall of Ashkarra]]**. The capital of the the [[Dragonborn Empire]]

Twelve Eldritch Moons. The **Third Thread** (triple Spirit alignment). The First Hollow Breach. And the **shattering of a World Anchor**.

The Pattern tore. The Faith nearly died with it.

Only in the finding and founding of **[[Aeravae]]**—a city rooted in Water and Spirit—held firm. From its depths, a new **World Anchor** was forged using forgotten glyph-rites and memory-stones. Its emergence marked the **Rebinding**.

The Faith restructured around this survival. The Test of Threads was codified. The Fray was named. The sanctums reorganised. The Age of Humans arose

---
## 🌙 **Fourth Age – The Verdant Loom**

> _“The Weave is our law. The Loom is our life. The Thread must hold.”_

The Faith of the Bound Pattern is now **the dominant religion** in Dór-o Estel.

Its structure defines:

- The **calendar** (measured in Cycles and Years of the Verdant Loom),
- The **laws of the Loom-Cities**,
- The role of **Threadbearers**,
- The interpretation of **prophecy and memory**,
- The balance between **elemental power** and mortal agency.

Each city aligns to a Thread. Each sanctum watches for Fray. The world moves forward by the Loom’s rhythm—even those who do not believe still mark time by its Cycles.

But cracks remain.

The memory of Ashkarra haunts the Pattern. Threadbearers sometimes vanish. Echoes flicker. And the Eldritch moons rise more often now.

> _“The Pattern endures… but for how long?”_

### 🪡 **The Unrooting – Ashkarra and the Breaking of Memory**

In the final century of the Third Age, the Woven Reverence watched in silence as the world’s balance was shattered.

The cities of fire rose too high. The Weave was pulled taut by Spirit’s overreach. **Ashkarra**, once seen as distant and arrogant, aligned to Spirit **three times in a row**. The Spirit Threads, once symbols of renewal and memory, were warped by obsession and power.

When the **World Tree beneath Ashkarra** was severed in a ritual of dominion, the Woven Reverence called it what it was:

> _“A murder of root and rhythm.”_

The Eldritch Moons rose. The Hollow Breach opened. The **world almost ended**.

And yet the Faith, so often dismissed by the old elven orders as overly structured, **stood where others fell**. The Reverence saw, then, not a rival—but a wounded sibling weaving too fast.

---
### 💧 **The Shared Replanting – Aeravae and the Deep Loom**

When all else faltered, what would become the **city of [[Aeravae]]**—sung into the bones of river and echo—held.

But it did not hold alone.

The Woven Reverence:
- Called back the Echo-Weavers from the coastal groves.
- Awoke the drowned spirit-stones of the Luminous Wells.
- Offered the **Echo of Selmira** herself to stabilize the collapsing Spirit Thread.

It was **[[elves]]**, **[[halflings]]**, **[[humans]]**, and even **[[dwarves]] of [[the Rooted Silence]]** who helped raise Aeravae’s anchor—not as a city of dominance, but of **balance**.

[[The Woven Reverence]], once reluctant to entangle itself in the urban spirals of the Faith, **wove itself into the rebirth**.

### Internal Theological Tensions 
The cooperation required to found Aeravae created fractures within Faith doctrine: 
- **The Traditionalist Position:** 
	- World Anchors are the "proper" system, Trees are primitive relics 
	- The Faith brought order to chaotic natural forces 
	- Elven cooperation was necessary but doesn't validate their theology 
- **The Reformist Position:** 
	- Trees prove the Weave predates divine authority 
	- Aeravae's success vindicates cooperation over dominance 
	- Faith must humble itself or repeat Ashkarra's mistakes 
- **The Syncretist Compromise (Current Doctrine):** 
	- Both systems are sacred manifestations of the Pattern 
	- Trees are "earth-blessed" and Anchors are "spirit-refined" 
	- Cooperation strengthens rather than weakens the Faith

# 🧵 **The Threadbearer**

> “They do not rule. They _are_ the rhythm. The breath between spells. The pause before fate. The one the Weave has chosen to carry its Thread.”

### 🔹 **Role and Function**

- Each **Threadbearer** represents **one of the five elemental Threads**: **Fire, Earth, Air, Water, and Spirit**.
- A new Threadbearer emerges at the start of a **Cycle** to guide and protect a [[World Anchor]]—a period of time during which one elemental force dominates the structure of the Faith, the elemental magic of that Loom-City, and often global magical balance.
- Threadbearers act as **spiritual arbiters, advisors to rulers, defenders of the Weave, and custodians of leyline balance.**
- Their role in each of the [[Loom-Cities]] is dependant on the city itself, each having its own cultural role for the Threadbearer

Each Threadbearer is **spiritually woven to their city's [[World Anchor]]**. They do not control it—but through them, the Anchor dreams. When it shakes, so do they.

---
## 🔁 **The Cycle of Threads**

- The elemental attunement of a [[World Anchor]] will change at random and therefore the  Threadbearer elemental representation in a city will be at random, not known until after the new cycle, but there is always 5 threadbearers to represent the five threads:  
- Cycles are unpredictable in length—**some last days, others over a century**, but no Cycle may exceed 137 years.
- A Cycle ends either **with the death of a Threadbearer**, **a major omen**, or **by the judgment of the Weave itself.**
- A world anchor can attune back to back to the same thread, but three times in a row has only happened once, the [[Fall of Ashkarra]].

---
## 🌠 **The Beaming: How a Threadbearer Is Chosen**

- A **Beaming** is the event where a new Threadbearer emerges.
- The chosen individual is **not selected by mortals or gods**, but by the **Weave itself**.
- Signs of a Beaming include:
    - A visible **manifestation of their Thread’s element** (e.g., fire spiraling around them, the earth cracking at their feet).
    - Prophetic dreams, leyline surges, or the sudden collapse of magical barriers.
- Once chosen, the Threadbearer is instantly recognised by all major religious authorities—even those from rival faiths.
- Surviving Threadbearers feel the compulsion to find the new Threadbearer. Some resist or fail—but the Pattern calls them, whether they obey or not.
- The confirmation of a new Threadbearer is a great celebration in the [[Loom-Cities]], with most cities changing directly or subtly to reflect the elements of their new Threadbearer
---
## ⚖️ **Powers and Influence**

- Threadbearers are **immune to most forms of magical coercion** due to their harmony with the Weave.
- They can:
    - Stabilise or disrupt **[[leylines]]**.
    - Call upon **elemental manifestations**.
    - Interpret or reject **glyphic prophecy**.
    - Initiate **city-wide rites** to protect the Pattern or sever corrupted bonds.
- While they do not hold political control, their **word is binding** on all religious matters.
- They seem to be attuned to the [[World Anchor]] in the loom city... somehow...

---
## 🕳️ **The False Weaving and the Test of Threads**

- After a catastrophic event involving a **false Echoed Weaving** (a corrupted Threadbearer aligned with the Eldritch), a **hidden test** was instituted:
    - The Weave itself now **tests all new Threadbearers** in secret.
    - Only upon passing does their element **fully manifest**, confirming their legitimacy.
    - This test is _not known to the Threadbearers themselves_—failure leads to magical implosion or madness.

---
## 🧬 **The Echoed Weaving**

- A **special term** used to denote the **Threadbearer who serves during the Spirit Cycle**.
- The Echoed Weaving is considered **closest to the truth of the Weave**, and often **records** or **reweaves** parts of its design.
- Their role is **the most dangerous and mysterious**, as Spirit cycles are most vulnerable to **Eldritch corruption** and **prophetic overload**.

---
## 🏛️ **Threadbearers in the Cities**

Each **Loom-City** includes a seat for its active Threadbearer. Depending on the city:

- They may **co-rule**, serve as **prophets**, **lawgivers**, or **ritual leaders**.
- In cities like **[[07_Geography/02_Cities/Thar Vornhal]]**, **[[Veltharyn]]**, or **[[Kel Varyan]]**, they act as both **guardian and spiritual mirror** for that cycle’s alignment.

---
## 🔗 **Threadbearer Unity – The Last Call of the Pattern**

> _“Five voices, woven separate. But when the Anchor trembles, they speak as one.”_  
> — _Fragment from the Loom of Echoes_

- Threadbearers are **fiercely independent**, bound to their own city and Cycle.
- **They do not convene, deliberate, or interfere** in one another’s Cycles—**except in one case**:
    > If a **World Anchor is at risk**—from Eldritch corruption, overdrawn magic, or cosmic fracture—the Weave itself **stirs their Threads in unison**.
- When this happens, the Threadbearers perform **the Last Call**—a silent communion through the Pattern itself.
- Each responds with their **full authority, power, and influence** to defend the Anchor. This moment is sacred and overrides all politics or faith divisions.
- **If one fails to answer**, their Thread dims across the Weave, and they are known as a **Frayed Hand**—a title of shame whispered only in sacred silence.

---
## 🩸 **Threadbearer Death and Succession**

- A Threadbearer’s death is marked by:
    - A **momentary unraveling of the local Weave**.
    - A **symbolic fracture**—e.g., silent flame, still tides, unbreaking storm.
- Their soul is **absorbed back into the Weave**, and the **next Beaming** begins shortly afterward.
- In rare cases, a Threadbearer’s **soul remains partially woven into reality**, becoming an **Echoed Witness**.
- All surviving Threadbearers knowing immediately and their attunement to the [[World Anchors]] weakened but feeling the call and location of the new Threadbearer. 
	- Their job is to find the new threadbearer or die trying. 
	- Most cultures and religions will automatically help them
	- This is a period of great danger as the weave is less stable, and the [[Drow]] will hunt and try to capture the old Threadbearers
- If they survive and find the new Threadbearer, they will be honoured as treated by all civilised people similar to holy men or royals. Most do not travel but retreat to quiet lives.

# **Racial and Cultural Views**

- **[[Elves]]**: Believe they were **woven into the Weave** before the gods. Their faith emphasizes memory, fate, and quiet reverence of Spirit and Air gods.
- **[[Dwarves]]**: Revere their divine forgers like **Thamur**, but do not worship them—see them more as **builders and ancestors**.
- **[[Orcs]]**: Mostly **reject religion**, claiming they were shaped by the gods' mistakes. Honor **strength, survival, and defiance**.
- **[[Gnomes]]**: Were **unleashed**, not created. Their beliefs focus on **innovation, risk, and defying limits**, often in friction with divine structure.
- **[[Dragonborn]]**: Follow a **hierarchical divine order** built by gods like **[[Soltharion]]**, **[[Vorexian]]**, and **[[Xethar]]**. Their religion is imperial, rigid, and secretive—intended to prepare for Soltharion’s return.
- **[[Halflings]]**: Venerate **joy and freedom**, with patronage from gods like **[[Zephir]]** and nature spirits. They avoid structured worship.
- **[[Humans]]**: Are theologically flexible. They worship **multiple pantheons or create new faiths** depending on culture and circumstance. Often incorporate elements of the Bound Pattern, but many fringe beliefs exist.

### **The Eldritch Cults** (Forbidden Religions)

- **Worship of the Eldritch Gods** like **[[Nhal’Zereth]]**, **[[Ul'Naresh]]**, and **[[Vael'Akir]]**.
- Often hidden, fragmented cults that reflect **madness, paradox, and forbidden truths**.
- Rare but dangerously powerful—cities purge these cults when found.
- No unified Eldritch religion exists—each god warps worship into its own echoing corruption.

---
## 🧑‍🌾 **Humans** – _"The Faith of the Bound Pattern is ours—and yet not ours alone."_

- **Integration:** [[Humans]] likely **originated or popularised the Faith**, blending their flexible pantheon and philosophical ambiguity into a unifying doctrine for trade cities and multi-race empires.
- **Cultural Fit:** Perfect. Humans already worship gods from all pantheons in local combinations. The Five Threads give them structure without removing freedom.
- **Social Norms:** Human priests, scholars, and politicians often **serve as the primary Weave-Readers**. Most city policies, temples, and guilds align with Pattern doctrine.
- **Faith Language:** "The gods are threads; we are the needle."

> **Verdict:** Central architects and most devoted adherents.

---
## 🧝 **Elves** – _"We were woven before the gods took credit. The Pattern merely confirms what we already knew."_

- **Integration:** [[Elves]] don’t _convert_ to the Faith, but they **recognize it as a poetic echo of their oldest truths.** Many Elves see the Weave and the Pattern as **parallel metaphors**.
- **Cultural Fit:** Strong, though they treat the Faith as _derivative_. Elven scholars and mages may even **discuss the Pattern in refined terms**, referencing it in public while still privately following their own ancestral memory.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, they’ll quote the Faith to show fluency, but defer to **their First Song** as the older, truer form.
- **Faith Language:** "The Pattern is remembered; not newly discovered."

> **Verdict:** Politely aligned, quietly superior.

---
## 🧔‍♂️ **Dwarves** – _"We don’t follow the Pattern, but we respect the loom."_

- **Integration:** [[Dwarves]] **do not worship** as others do. But they **acknowledge the metaphor** of threads and structure. Some even believe the gods who wove the first Dwarves did so using the same Loom.
- **Cultural Fit:** Moderate. The Faith feels **overly poetic** to most Dwarves, but **the idea of an unbreaking Pattern resonates** with their obsession for permanence and tradition.
- **Social Norms:** Dwarves in cities may **carve the Thread sigils** onto bridges or buildings to gain city approval, without engaging in ritual.
- **Faith Language:** "The Thread holds. The stone endures."

> **Verdict:** Honored from a distance. Respected in cities, not revered.

---

## 🧙‍♂️ **Gnomes** – _"A thread? A pattern? Wonderful! But what happens if we cut it and re-stitch it sideways?"_

- **Integration:** [[Gnomes]] treat the Faith as a **beautiful hypothesis**. They adore the imagery and language but rarely adhere to it dogmatically.
- **Cultural Fit:** High in terms of curiosity, low in spiritual alignment. The Faith is sometimes **debated as if it were a magical theory** rather than religion.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, Gnomes will **quote the Pattern to avoid offending** humans or elves, but **tinker with its meanings** in private circles.
- **Faith Language:** "If the gods are threads, then what happens when you re-weave them?"

> **Verdict:** Culturally fluent, spiritually irreverent.

---

## 🧝‍♂️ **Half-Elves** – _"We are the needle between two threads."_

- **Integration:** This religion offers **deep symbolic belonging** to [[Half-Elves]]. They are literally **the uniting path between different pantheons**, just as the Faith teaches.
- **Cultural Fit:** Perfect. The metaphors of **interwoven identity and diverging paths** speak directly to Half-Elven experience.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, Half-Elves are often **valued as priests, diplomats, and interpreters of the Pattern**, balancing competing religious tensions.
- **Faith Language:** "We walk the seams."

> **Verdict:** Ideal spiritual and social home. Often core adherents.

---

## 🧛‍♀️ **Half-Drow** – _"We pretend to believe. So they don’t ask what we really are."_

- **Integration:** [[Half-Drow]] do **not believe** in the Faith, but in cities, they **perform it flawlessly**—citing threads, sanctums, and Weave-Readers to survive.
- **Cultural Fit:** Low to none, emotionally. But **socially essential**, and used as camouflage or misdirection.
- **Social Norms:** If they quote a Creed or wear a thread-charm, it is **always calculated**, never sincere.
- **Faith Language:** "Which thread leads them away from the truth?"

> **Verdict:** Weaponized fluency. Pure survival strategy.

---

## 🧝‍♀️ **Drow** – _"Your Loom is flawed. The Web cannot be undone."_

- **Integration:** Zero. The [[Drow]] follow **only Vel’Zyren**. The Faith of the Pattern is heresy of the highest order.
- **Cultural Fit:** Absolutely incompatible. The concept that fate is shaped by multiple gods directly contradicts Vel’Zyren’s **singular control of fate and secrets**.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, Drow may **fake politeness**, but usually show **quiet disdain**. Outsiders know not to push.
- **Faith Language:** "The Pattern is prey. The Web is predator."

> **Verdict:** Theocratic rejection. Unassimilated.

---

## 🧙 **Dragonborn** – _"Purpose is not a pattern. Purpose is decree."_

- **Integration:** Mild tolerance at best. The Faith of the Pattern contradicts their belief in **absolute hierarchy and divine engineering.**
- **Cultural Fit:** Low. The [[Dragonborn]] believe they were **crafted**, not woven—designed for a **singular purpose, not an evolving thread**.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, they **acknowledge the Faith** as a local belief but **do not recognize its authority.** At best, it's a metaphor for the castes.
- **Faith Language:** "The Pattern is a reflection of Order, nothing more."

> **Verdict:** Acknowledged politely, dismissed spiritually.

---

## 🍂 **Halflings** – _"The Pattern? Oh, we know it. But we don’t let it tell us how to dance."_

- **Integration:** Comfortable but selective. [[Halflings]] enjoy the **joyful parts of the Faith**, especially its artistic imagery and poetic interpretation of life.    
- **Cultural Fit:** Strong in metaphor. Weak in hierarchy. They’ll quote it over drinks and honor its traditions, but they **rarely revere it as law.**
- **Social Norms:** In cities, they’re seen as **folk-keepers of smaller sanctums**—Weave-houses full of laughter, stories, and food.
- **Faith Language:** "Every Thread has its song. Ours just happens to be a jig."

> **Verdict:** Playful participants. Spiritual neighbors, not zealots.

---

## 🧌 **Orcs** – _"The Pattern is a chain. We choose to break it."_

- **Integration:** Rejection with fierce independence. [[Orcs]] see the Faith as **another attempt to impose destiny**—which they inherently reject.
- **Cultural Fit:** Very low. They were not “woven” or “shaped”—they were **consequences of divine failure**, and **freedom from fate** is their birthright.
- **Social Norms:** In cities, Orcs may nod politely, or quote a thread **sarcastically**, but they often avoid any public affirmation.
- **Faith Language:** "The Pattern is something others follow. We forge new warpaths."

> **Verdict:** Respectful hostility. They won't burn your temple—but they won’t step inside it either.
