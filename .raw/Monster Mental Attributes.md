# Monster Mental Attributes: Sanity & Focus

> _"Even the mightiest dragon knows the chill of doubt when its hoard burns. Every beast understands the moment when courage becomes terror."_

---

## Overview

In standard D&D, monsters are one-dimensional combatants. A goblin fights with the same resolve whether it's part of a victorious warband or the last survivor watching its chieftain fall. An orc spellcaster maintains concentration with Constitution, as if physical toughness were the only measure of mental discipline. A dragon faces existential horror with a Wisdom save, as if primal awareness were the same as psychological resilience.

Mental attributes - Sanity and Focus - exist for player characters in the Dynamic Combat System. But if only PCs have minds that can break, only PCs have morale that can shatter, only PCs have concentration that depends on mental clarity... then the psychological warfare is one-sided. The Breaking Points system becomes toothless. Fear effects lose meaning. Mental combat becomes binary pass/fail with no gradation.

**Monster Mental Attributes** changes this fundamentally.

This system gives enemies the mental attributes they need to interact meaningfully with the psychological warfare of the DCS. When a goblin warband watches their chieftain fall, their low Sanity scores make them vulnerable to Breaking Points - they scatter in fear. When a dragon caster loses Focus from magical backlash, their concentration falters just like a PC's would. When an ancient lich faces cosmic horror, their impossibly high Sanity reflects centuries of exposure to the unthinkable.

### Why Monsters Need Mental Attributes

The Dynamic Combat System introduces several mechanics that require mental statistics:

**Morale and Breaking Points** - The Bloodied, Breaking Points & Rally system requires Sanity saves when leaders fall or casualties mount. Without Sanity scores, every monster has the same morale resistance. A terrified goblin and an ancient dragon would be equally likely to break. That's not just unrealistic - it removes a tactical dimension from combat.

**Rally Attempts** - Enemy leaders attempt Focus-based Rally checks to restore their troops' courage. Without Focus scores, every leader is equally inspiring. A charismatic warlord and a brutish ogre would be identical. Players lose the ability to target weak-willed leaders strategically.

**Fear Effects** - Spells like Fear, Cause Fear, and abilities like Frightful Presence target enemy psychology. In standard 5E, these use Wisdom saves - conflating primal awareness with psychological resilience. With Sanity, fear effects target the appropriate mental attribute. A wise but timid creature can be scared. A foolish but brave creature can hold firm.

**Mental Attacks** - Charm, domination, psychic damage, and mind-affecting spells all benefit from clearer mental statistics. Focus represents resistance to magical mental intrusion. Sanity represents resistance to psychological trauma.

**Concentration** - Monster spellcasters maintain concentration with Constitution in standard 5E. This makes physical toughness the sole measure of magical discipline. With Focus, a frail but disciplined wizard can maintain spells better than a hulking but distracted brute.

**Eldritch Encounters** - When monsters face cosmic horror, corruption, or sanity-shattering phenomena, they need Sanity scores to measure their resistance. Without them, a peasant and a demigod are equally vulnerable to madness.

### Design Philosophy

**Monsters are not all the same.** A goblin should break when its leader falls. A devil should fight with iron discipline. A celestial should be almost impossible to frighten. A dragon should have the mental fortitude of an ancient, wise being. A beast should operate on instinct, easily distracted but immune to existential dread.

**Mental attributes should reflect creature nature.** Beasts have low Focus (easily distracted) but resistance to philosophical fear (they don't contemplate mortality). Undead have variable Sanity based on their intelligence (zombies have none, liches are nearly unbreakable). Dragons have legendarily high mental stats reflecting their age and wisdom.

**Calculation should be simple but meaningful.** DMs shouldn't need complex math for every goblin. The system provides quick defaults for immediate use, and detailed calculations for important encounters. Type-based modifiers ensure creature flavor without micromanagement.

**Integration should be seamless.** Mental attributes plug directly into existing DCS mechanics. Breaking Points use Sanity saves. Rally uses Focus checks. Concentration uses Focus. Fear effects use Sanity. No new subsystems - just giving monsters the stats they need for systems that already exist.

This system ensures enemies feel psychologically distinct, players have new tactical options, and the mental warfare of the DCS becomes truly two-sided.

## SYSTEM 1: Quick Assignment Rules

For immediate play without detailed calculation, use these streamlined rules. These provide functional mental attributes in seconds, perfect for improvisation or unimportant encounters.

### Universal Monster Defaults

**Sanity Default:** 10 + Wisdom Modifier

**Focus Default:** 8 + Mental Stat + CR/4 (rounded down)

- Mental Stat = Highest of Int, Wis, or Cha modifiers

These defaults work for about 80% of monsters and require minimal math at the table.

---

### CR-Based Minimums

Even the weakest creatures need baseline mental resilience. Use these minimums to ensure mental attributes scale appropriately:

|**CR Range**|**Minimum Focus**|**Minimum Sanity**|
|---|---|---|
|0-2|6|8|
|3-8|8|10|
|9-16|10|12|
|17+|12|14|

**Application:** If your calculated mental attribute falls below the minimum for that CR, use the minimum instead. This prevents legendary creatures from having embarrassingly low mental stats.

---

### Quick Reference by Type

For even faster assignment, use these type-based shortcuts:

|**Type**|**Sanity Modifier**|**Focus Modifier**|**Notes**|
|---|---|---|---|
|Beast|+0|-2|Instinct over thought|
|Humanoid|+0|+0|Standard calculation|
|Giant|+2|-1|Simple but determined|
|Dragon|+4|+4|Ancient minds|
|Fiend|+2|+1|Corrupt intelligence|
|Celestial|+6|+3|Divine clarity|
|Undead|Special|Special|See detailed rules|
|Construct|N/A|+2|Mechanical focus|
|Aberration|Special|Special|Alien psychology|

**How to Use:**

1. Calculate base Sanity/Focus using Universal Defaults
2. Add the Type Modifier from the table
3. Check against CR Minimums
4. Done

**Example:** CR 5 Orc (Humanoid)

- Sanity: 10 + 1 (Wis) + 0 (type) = 11
- Focus: 8 + 1 (Cha) + 1 (CR/4) + 0 (type) = 10
- Both exceed minimums (8 and 10), so we're done

## SYSTEM 2: Standard Calculation System

For more nuanced and accurate mental attributes on important enemies, boss fights, and recurring NPCs, use this detailed calculation system.

### Step 1: Base Scores

**Sanity Base Formula:**

Sanity = 8 + Wisdom Modifier + CR Bonus + Type Modifier

**Focus Base Formula:**

Focus = 6 + Highest Mental Stat + CR Bonus + Type Modifier

- Mental Stat = Best of Int, Wis, or Cha modifiers

**Why These Bases?**

- 8 and 6 are intentionally lower than PC starting points (10)
- Most monsters are not heroic individuals - they're rank-and-file
- Bosses and leaders get large bonuses that push them above PCs
- This creates psychological asymmetry that favors heroic PCs

---

### Step 2: CR Bonus Table

Higher CR creatures have experienced more, survived more, and developed greater mental resilience:

|**CR Range**|**Sanity Bonus**|**Focus Bonus**|**Reasoning**|
|---|---|---|---|
|0-1/4|+0|+0|Weak-willed creatures|
|1/2-2|+1|+1|Basic mental resilience|
|3-4|+2|+1|Experienced threats|
|5-8|+2|+2|Veteran combatants|
|9-12|+3|+2|Elite opponents|
|13-16|+3|+3|Master-tier threats|
|17-20|+4|+3|Legendary creatures|
|21+|+5|+4|Mythic beings|

**Design Note:** Sanity scales slightly faster than Focus at high CR. Ancient creatures have seen horrors that would break lesser minds. Focus represents discipline and control, which scales more linearly with experience.

---

### Step 3: Creature Type Modifiers

#### Beasts

- **Sanity:** +0 (instinctual, not contemplative)
- **Focus:** -2 (easily distracted, reactive)
- **Special Immunity:** Immune to existential fear effects (they don't contemplate mortality)

**Philosophy:** Beasts operate on instinct. They're easily distracted by food, mates, or threats. But they also can't be driven mad by cosmic horror - they lack the philosophical framework to comprehend it.

**Examples:** Wolf (Sanity 10, Focus 6), Bear (Sanity 12, Focus 5), Owlbear (Sanity 11, Focus 4)

---

#### Humanoids

- **Sanity:** +0 (baseline)
- **Focus:** +0 (baseline)
- **Special:** Full interaction with all mental systems

**Philosophy:** Humanoids are the baseline. They have the full range of mental strengths and weaknesses. They can be brave or cowardly, disciplined or scattered, wise or foolish.

**Examples:** Goblin (Sanity 8, Focus 7), Orc (Sanity 11, Focus 9), Bandit Captain (Sanity 12, Focus 11)

---

#### Giants

- **Sanity:** +2 (simple outlook, hard to shake)
- **Focus:** -1 (slow thinking, single-minded)
- **Special:** Advantage on saves vs confusion effects

**Philosophy:** Giants are simple but determined. Their straightforward worldview makes them resistant to psychological manipulation - what you see is what you get. But their lack of mental agility makes them poor at complex tactics.

**Examples:** Hill Giant (Sanity 12, Focus 8), Fire Giant (Sanity 14, Focus 10), Cloud Giant (Sanity 16, Focus 12)

---

#### Dragons

- **Sanity:** +4 (ancient wisdom, seen everything)
- **Focus:** +4 (legendary mental discipline)
- **Special:** Immune to fear effects from sources below CR 10

**Philosophy:** Dragons are among the most mentally formidable creatures in existence. Centuries of life grant perspective that makes them nearly unbreakable. Even young dragons have mental attributes rivaling veteran humanoids.

**Examples:** Young Dragon (Sanity 16, Focus 17), Adult Dragon (Sanity 22, Focus 21), Ancient Dragon (Sanity 28, Focus 26)

---

#### Fiends

- **Sanity:** +2 (alien mindset, evil clarity)
- **Focus:** +1 (supernatural will)
- **Special:** Resistance to charm effects

**Philosophy:** Fiends have alien psychology. Evil is not madness to them - it's clarity of purpose. Their supernatural nature grants willpower beyond mortal limits. But they can still break under extreme pressure.

**Examples:** Imp (Sanity 12, Focus 10), Bearded Devil (Sanity 14, Focus 13), Pit Fiend (Sanity 24, Focus 22)

---

#### Celestials

- **Sanity:** +6 (divine serenity)
- **Focus:** +3 (heavenly discipline)
- **Special:** Immune to despair effects, always pass Rally attempts

**Philosophy:** Celestials are the mental opposite of fiends - good incarnate with divine mental fortitude. They almost never break morale. When they choose to fight, they fight with absolute conviction.

**Examples:** Deva (Sanity 22, Focus 18), Planetar (Sanity 26, Focus 21), Solar (Sanity 30, Focus 24)

---

#### Undead

- **Sanity:** Special (see Undead Rules below)
- **Focus:** +1 (unliving persistence)
- **Special:** Immune to most fear effects

**Philosophy:** Undead psychology varies dramatically by intelligence. Mindless undead have no psychology at all. Intelligent undead range from twisted remnants to enhanced versions of their living selves.

See detailed Undead rules in Special Type Rules section.

---

#### Constructs

- **Sanity:** N/A (mindless) or +0 (sentient)
- **Focus:** +2 (programmed purpose)
- **Special:** Immune to emotional manipulation

**Philosophy:** Constructs are purpose-built. Mindless ones have no mental attributes at all. Sentient constructs have Focus reflecting their programmed discipline, but Sanity only if they're truly thinking beings.

See detailed Construct rules in Special Type Rules section.

---

#### Aberrations

- **Sanity:** Special (see Aberration Rules below)
- **Focus:** +3 (alien mental superiority)
- **Special:** Cause Sanity saves in others

**Philosophy:** Aberrations have alien psychology that breaks normal rules. Their minds don't work like mortal minds. They often cause sanity damage simply by being observed.

See detailed Aberration rules in Special Type Rules section.

## SYSTEM 3: Special Type Rules

Some creature types require special handling due to their unusual psychology.

### Undead Mental Attributes

Undead psychology varies dramatically by intelligence and origin:

|**Undead Type**|**Sanity**|**Focus**|**Special Rules**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Mindless**|N/A|N/A|Immune to mental effects|
|**Zombie/Skeleton**|4 (basic awareness)|6|No morale, follow commands|
|**Wight/Wraith**|8 + Cha mod (twisted purpose)|8 + Cha|Hatred sustains them|
|**Vampire/Lich**|12 + Int mod (preserved mind)|12 + Int|Undeath enhances mental power|
|**Death Knight**|14 + Wis mod (divine curse)|10 + Cha|Fallen honor drives them|

**Undead Morale:** Most undead ignore Breaking Points but may have **Turning Thresholds** instead. When turned, they function as if they failed a Breaking Point check.

**Design Philosophy:** Intelligent undead often have _enhanced_ mental attributes compared to their living selves. Death removes biological limitations. A lich's mind is sharper than it was in life. A vampire's focus is supernatural. But twisted purpose isn't the same as wisdom - their Sanity reflects their corruption.

**Examples:**

- **Zombie (CR 1/4):** Sanity N/A, Focus N/A - Mindless, follows last command
- **Wight (CR 3):** Sanity 11 (8 + 3 Cha), Focus 11 - Hunger for life sustains focus
- **Vampire Spawn (CR 5):** Sanity 14 (12 + 2 Int), Focus 16 - Supernatural predator
- **Lich (CR 21):** Sanity 32 (12 + 5 Int + 5 mythic + 10 centuries), Focus 30 - Transcendent intellect

---

### Aberration Mental Attributes

Aberrations have alien psychology that breaks normal rules:

|**Aberration Type**|**Sanity Calculation**|**Focus**|**Special Effects**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Mind Flayer**|15 + Int mod|12 + Int|Causes Sanity saves in 30ft|
|**Beholder**|18 (alien perfection)|16|Immune to surprise, flanking|
|**Aboleth**|20 (ancient knowledge)|14|Memory manipulation abilities|
|**Owlbear**|6 (beast-level, corrupted)|4|Rage when bloodied|
|**Rust Monster**|8 (simple alien instinct)|6|Single-minded purpose|

**Aberration Aura:** CR 5+ aberrations cause **Sanity saves (DC 12 + CR/3)** for creatures within 30 feet on first encounter. This represents the wrongness of their existence.

**Design Philosophy:** Aberrations fall into two categories. Simple aberrations (rust monsters, owlbears) are just alien beasts - corrupted animals with weird biology. Complex aberrations (mind flayers, beholders) have minds that are _superior_ to mortal minds in terrifying ways. Their psychology is so alien that understanding them is itself dangerous.

**Examples:**

- **Otyugh (CR 5):** Sanity 12, Focus 10 - Simple telepathic scavenger
- **Cloaker (CR 8):** Sanity 14, Focus 12 - Predatory intelligence
- **Mind Flayer (CR 7):** Sanity 21 (15 + 4 Int + 2 CR), Focus 20 - Hive mind horror
- **Beholder (CR 13):** Sanity 18 (fixed, alien perfection), Focus 16 - Paranoid genius

---

### Construct Variations

|**Construct Type**|**Sanity**|**Focus**|**Mental Rules**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Mindless**|N/A|N/A|Immune to all mental effects|
|**Animated Object**|N/A|4|Basic programmed responses|
|**Golem**|N/A|8|Single command focus|
|**Warforged**|10 + Wis|8 + Con|Artificial but thinking minds|
|**Modron**|14|16|Perfect logical discipline|

**Design Philosophy:** True constructs have no Sanity because they don't experience psychological trauma. But some have Focus representing their programmed purpose. Sentient constructs (warforged, some golems) have both Sanity and Focus because they're truly conscious.

**Examples:**

- **Animated Armor (CR 1):** Sanity N/A, Focus 4 - Simple "attack intruders" programming
- **Flesh Golem (CR 5):** Sanity 6 (tortured, confused), Focus 8 - Barely sentient
- **Warforged Soldier (CR 3):** Sanity 12 (10 + 2 Wis), Focus 12 - Fully conscious warrior
- **Monodrone (CR 1/8):** Sanity 14 (perfect order), Focus 16 - Lawful machine mind

## Integration with Combat Systems

### Morale and Breaking Points

**Sanity-Based Morale Saves:**

When enemy leaders fall or casualties mount, remaining enemies roll Breaking Point checks:

- **Roll:** 1d20 + Sanity modifier
- **DC:** 10 + (5 x casualty tier)
- **Critical Failure:** Immediate rout or panic
- **Success:** Fight on at -1 Momentum

**Why This Matters:** Low-Sanity enemies (goblins, beasts, low-level humanoids) break easily. High-Sanity enemies (dragons, fiends, celestials) almost never rout. This creates tactical distinctions between enemy types.

**Player Tactical Options:**

- Target low-Sanity enemies first to trigger cascading routs
- Kill leaders to force hardest Breaking Point checks
- Use fear effects on low-Sanity enemies for double impact

---

### Focus-Based Rally Attempts

**Rally Check:**

When enemy leaders attempt to restore morale, they roll:

- **Roll:** 1d20 + Focus modifier
- **DC:** Same as the failed Breaking Point
- **Success:** Allies reroll with advantage, restore Momentum
- **Failure:** Allies lose -1 Momentum, morale worsens

**Why This Matters:** High-Focus leaders (experienced captains, spellcasters, disciplined warriors) can Rally effectively. Low-Focus leaders (brutish thugs, distracted monsters) often fail and make things worse.

**Player Tactical Options:**

- Target high-Focus enemy leaders to prevent successful Rallies
- Damage enemy leaders' Focus before bloodied threshold
- Force enemy leaders to Rally repeatedly until they fail

---

### Fear Effects Integration

**Standard Fear Spells (Cause Fear, Frightful Presence):**

Instead of Wisdom saves, use **Sanity saves**:

- **Target rolls:** Wisdom save as normal (for mechanical compatibility)
- **If failed:** Also lose **1 Focus** until end of next turn
- **Frightened condition:** Imposes -2 to Focus-based rolls

**Existential Terror (Eldritch sources):**

These require **Sanity saves** instead of Wisdom saves:

- Failure causes **Sanity damage** and potential Corruption
- May trigger **Mental Trauma** in intelligent creatures
- Aberration auras, cosmic horrors, reality-bending phenomena

**Why This Matters:** Fear effects now interact with the mental attribute system. Brave but foolish creatures (high Sanity, low Wisdom) can resist fear. Wise but timid creatures (high Wisdom, low Sanity) succumb.

---

### Concentration and Spellcasting

**Monster Concentration Checks:**

When enemy spellcasters take damage while concentrating:

- Use **Focus modifier** instead of Constitution modifier
- **DC = 10 or half damage, whichever is higher**
- Low Focus monsters lose concentration more easily

**Why This Matters:** A frail but disciplined wizard (low Con, high Focus) maintains spells better than a tough but scattered brute (high Con, low Focus). This makes monster spellcasters feel more like casters and less like warriors who happen to cast spells.

**Player Tactical Options:**

- Target enemy spellcasters' Focus directly (psychic damage, mental attacks)
- Research enemy caster Focus scores before combat
- Prioritize damage on low-Focus enemy casters to break concentration

## DM Guidelines

### When to Calculate Mental Attributes

**Always Calculate For:**

- Any creature that might flee, rally, or lead others
- Boss fights and major encounters
- Recurring NPCs and named enemies
- Spellcasters (need Focus for concentration)

**Calculate If Time Permits:**

- Elite enemies in important battles
- Creatures with fear effects or frightening presence
- Intelligent monsters facing eldritch threats

**Skip Calculation For:**

- Mindless creatures (they have N/A anyway)
- Swarms and minions in large battles
- Random encounters with forgettable enemies
- Creatures that will die in 1-2 rounds

**Pro Tip:** Calculate mental attributes for leaders and spellcasters. Use type-based defaults for everyone else.

---

### Balancing Mental Attributes

**High Sanity Creatures:**

- Won't break easily under pressure
- Resistant to fear effects
- May overcommit to losing battles (don't know when to flee)
- Examples: Dragons, celestials, experienced veterans

**Low Sanity Creatures:**

- Break quickly when things go wrong
- Vulnerable to fear effects
- May flee when victory is still possible
- Examples: Goblins, beasts, inexperienced humanoids

**High Focus Creatures:**

- Reliable concentration for spellcasters
- Steady decision-making under pressure
- Can Rally effectively
- Examples: Disciplined soldiers, spellcasters, monks

**Low Focus Creatures:**

- Lose concentration easily
- Make poor tactical decisions
- Rally attempts often fail
- Examples: Beasts, thugs, distracted monsters

**Mismatched Stats Create Interest:**

- High Sanity, Low Focus = Brave but scattered (orc berserker)
- Low Sanity, High Focus = Cowardly but disciplined (cautious wizard)
- High both = Elite opponents (dragon, lich, celestial)
- Low both = Fodder (goblin, zombie, mindless beast)

---

### Using Mental Attributes in Play

**Don't Track Everything:**

- Focus on leaders and important creatures
- Use batch resolution for groups of identical enemies
- Default to "reasonable NPC behavior" for unimportant creatures

**Narrative First:**

- Use the numbers to support story, not replace it
- A high-Sanity creature doesn't fight stupidly, they fight bravely
- A low-Focus creature doesn't act randomly, they act impulsively

**Player Impact:**

- Let PC actions meaningfully affect enemy morale
- Celebrate tactical victories (breaking enemy morale without killing everyone)
- Make fear effects and mental attacks feel impactful

**Escalation:**

- Mental pressure should build throughout encounters
- First casualty: minor concern
- Leader bloodied: crisis point
- 50% casualties: Breaking Point triggers
- Leader dies: potential rout

---

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Over-Calculating:**

- Don't slow down play with constant mental math
- Use quick defaults for unimportant enemies
- Only calculate precisely for important encounters

**Ignoring Type:**

- Creature type should significantly affect behavior
- Undead don't break like humanoids
- Beasts don't fear like intelligent creatures
- Celestials almost never flee

**Forgetting Recovery:**

- Mental attributes should recover between encounters
- Focus recovers on short rest (monsters too)
- Sanity recovers slowly (but monsters shouldn't carry trauma between sessions unless narratively important)

**Missing Integration:**

- Mental stats should connect to existing systems
- Breaking Points use Sanity
- Rally uses Focus
- Concentration uses Focus
- Fear effects use Sanity

**Making Everything Immune:**

- Legendary resistance is not mental immunity
- Even dragons can break if pushed far enough
- Immunity should be rare and type-specific

---

## Large Group Combat Management

When running encounters with many creatures, use these streamlined approaches:

### Batch Resolution

**Morale Checks:**

- Group identical creatures for Breaking Point saves
- Roll once for each group, apply results to all
- Speeds up mass routs dramatically

**Rally Attempts:**

- One leader Rallies all similar troops
- Don't track individual Focus for rank-and-file

**Mental Attribute Assignment:**

- Use type defaults for groups
- Only calculate precisely for leaders and elites

### Priority Targeting

**Full Mental Attribute Tracking:**

- Leaders, elites, and boss monsters only
- Track their Sanity and Focus precisely

**Simplified Mental Stats:**

- Mooks use type defaults
- Apply Breaking Points to groups, not individuals
- Skip Rally attempts for leaderless groups

**Quick Decisions:**

- If it doesn't matter narratively, assume reasonable behavior
- Reserve detailed morale mechanics for dramatic moments

- Leader attempts: 1d20 + Focus modifier
- DC: Same as failed Breaking Point
- Success: Reroll morale saves with advantage

- Check when damaged: 1d20 + Focus modifier
- DC: 10 or half damage (whichever higher)
- Replaces Constitution modifier

- Standard fear: Wisdom save (compatibility)
- Existential terror: Sanity save
- Failure: Also lose 1 Focus

## Design Summary

**Core Principle:** Monsters need mental attributes to interact meaningfully with psychological warfare systems in the DCS.

**Quick Assignment:** Universal defaults work for 80% of monsters with minimal calculation.

**Standard Calculation:** Detailed system for important enemies provides nuanced mental profiles.

**Type-Based Modifiers:** Ensure creature psychology reflects creature nature without micromanagement.

**Special Rules:** Undead, Aberrations, and Constructs get unique mental frameworks.

**Integration:** Mental attributes plug directly into existing systems (Breaking Points, Rally, Concentration, Fear).

**Asymmetry:** Most monsters have lower mental stats than PCs, making heroic psychology a player advantage.

This system ensures enemies feel psychologically distinct, players have tactical options to exploit enemy morale, and the mental warfare of the DCS becomes truly two-sided. No longer are PCs the only ones with minds that can break - now the battlefield is as much a psychological contest as a physical one.

**Give your monsters minds worth fearing, and minds worth breaking.**
