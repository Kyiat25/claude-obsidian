## PART I: WHAT REEDFORD IS AND WHY IT EXISTS

### The Geography That Made the Town

Reedford does not exist because someone decided to build it. It exists because the River Ivora decided to become difficult.

Below Reedford, the Ivora runs wide, deep, and steady across the Imperial lowlands. The great estuary barges — broad-beamed, deep-drafted, capable of carrying forty tonnes of cargo — move freely. Above Reedford, the river changes character. The channel narrows, the current quickens, the riverbed becomes unpredictable. Gravel bars shift seasonally, submerged boulders lurk where they weren't last season, and the spring floods rolling down from the Upper Ivora Hills can turn reliable passages into chaos within hours. An estuary barge drawing two metres of water that grounds on a gravel bar above Reedford is stranded. There are no dry-dock facilities capable of handling a vessel of that size until Doroshen, three days upstream on a good-water day.

So cargo stops at Reedford. It is unloaded, moved through licensed warehouses, re-weighed, re-manifested, and loaded onto the flat-bottomed skiffs and narrow-beamed hill-runners that can navigate the upper river. **Reedford is a transshipment port** — a town that exists not because of what it produces, but because geography forces trade to pause and change hands.

Everything else follows from this: the warehouses, the taverns, the freight factors, the grey traders, the clergy, the monster hunters, the lord's counting house. The geography is the engine. Everything visible is an adaptation to the engine. And because the engine runs on the movement of goods, everyone in Reedford — lord, priest, pilot, beggar — lives or dies by their relationship to what flows through.

### Position on the Ivora

**Upstream (northeast):** The Middle Crossing Lands begin within a day's ride — fortified bridge-towns, terraced vineyards climbing hillsides, noble estates behind stone walls built to prescribed geometric plans. The road to Cindraleth branches off sixty miles upstream.

**Downstream (southwest):** Imperial lowlands. A 280-mile water journey to Velcran's estuary, then the coastal route or Imperial road to Kel Varyan's delta.

**Reedford is where Corvessa ends and the Empire begins** — not legally (that transition happens at Velcran's customs gates) but practically. It is the last place, travelling south, where Corvessan social norms, Faith culture, and aristocratic hierarchy operate without constant Imperial overlay. Merchants coming north from Kel Varyan notice it immediately: roads become slightly more rigid, signage uses Pattern-glyphs as punctuation, people greet strangers with a half-second assessment that checks, unconsciously, for signs of doctrinal irregularity.

They don't usually act on it. Reedford is too economically mixed. But they make the assessment.

### The Economy Is the Reality

Before anything else is understood about Reedford, this must be understood: **the town runs on the extraction of value from the movement of other people's goods.**

Lord Valdrek does not produce wine. He taxes it in transit. He does not build boats. He charges to repair them and to dock them. He does not move cargo. He licenses the people who do and takes a percentage of everything that passes through his warehouses. His wealth is fundamentally a toll on the labour and production of everyone north and south of him. This is not unusual. This is how river-lords have operated since there were rivers. It is also — for the people whose wine is taxed, whose docking fees come out of wages already thin, whose warehouse costs cut into margins already narrow — the lived texture of being at the bottom of the economic chain.

The stevedore who unloads a barge at the Lower Quay earns four copper a day in peak season, three in autumn, nothing in deep winter. His labour is what makes the forty-tonne cargo move. Valdrek's crane is what makes moving it possible. Both statements are true. The stevedore earns four copper. Valdrek earns the transshipment tax on forty tonnes of Corvessan wine. Both statements are true.

Reedford's social complexity is the consequence of people at every level of this system navigating their position with the tools available to them. The lord manages it through political relationships. The factor manages it through documentation. The pilot manages it through skill. The stevedore manages it through the Stevedores' Mutual Aid Association, which is not a guild (guilds require noble charter; they don't have one) but functions as one — pooling resources to cover injury compensation, negotiating informally with warehouse operators over loading rates, and occasionally, when the rates drop too far, not loading anything until the conversation is had. Valdrek tolerates this because breaking their ability to coordinate would cost him more in productivity than the occasional lost day to negotiation.

Everyone is surviving. Everyone is making calculations. Everyone, from the lord to the beggar outside the Scale and Anchor, is working within a system that was not designed for them but which they are too embedded in to leave.

**Reedford does not have a Guild Quarter.** It has no Chemistry Guild hall, no Blacksmith Guild hall with a charter and a contract board. What it has is a transshipment port economy, a river that carries things from places the Faith says don't exist, and approximately 8,500 people making pragmatic decisions under institutional pressure. This shapes how craft and medicine work here: the smith who serves the Faith Guard files commission reports monthly and accepts a sanctum Thread-blessing on each finished blade. The smith who serves the grey traders files quarterly and has learned precisely how much detail the Customs House actually requires. Both do the same craft. The distinction is documentation, and in Reedford, documentation is politics.

## PART II: THE FAITH IN DAILY LIFE

*Reedford is not a zealot community. It is a community where zealotry is the cultural backdrop — the assumed standard against which every small deviation is measured. Understanding this is essential to running the town correctly.*

### The Week's Structure

The Verdant Loom Calendar divides the week into seven days, each carrying Thread connotations. In Corvessa, the weekly structure is religious infrastructure — it organises labour, trade, and social obligation simultaneously.

| Day | Name | Thread | Reedford Observance |
|-----|------|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Rootday** | Earth — foundation, law | Contracts signed on Rootday carry additional legal weight under Corvessan commercial doctrine. Orven Staak will not hire anyone on any other day. |
| 2 | **Weftday** | Air — movement, trade | Secondary market day. Pilots traditionally prefer to depart on Weftday for upper-river runs. |
| 3 | **Threadday** | Fire — dedication, vow | Faith Guard weekly patrol inspection. Young men from lower estates attend weapons drill. |
| 4 | **Weaveday** | Water — flow, inevitability | Primary working day. The Codex lists Weaveday as the Pattern's favoured day for commerce. The Lower Quay is at maximum activity. |
| 5 | **Loomday** | Spirit — memory, completion | Rest day. Workshops close by midday. The sanctum holds its main weekly service at the third bell. Cors is most likely to be in his office writing reports. |
| 6 | **Floodtide** | All Threads — renewal | Full market day. Barge arrivals heaviest — captains plan routes around the market. The river-blessing is performed publicly by Sethos at dawn. Grey trade is busiest and most visible under the cover of volume. Cors is most likely to be on the quay observing. |
| 7 | **Stillwater** | Water — reflection, rest | River market from the Upper Quay. Customs House staff observe reduced hours. The day Joss most often departs on ghost-road runs. |

**Economic reality of the calendar:** The Faith structures the week in ways that benefit commerce — Weaveday being the Pattern's "favoured day for commerce" is also the day the warehouses charge their highest rates and the factors do their largest business. Whether the Codex was written to reflect economic reality or economic reality has adapted to the Codex, the effect is the same: the Faith's calendar and Valdrek's revenue schedule are aligned. This is not coincidence.

### The Pattern-Greeting

The standard Corvessan exchange between strangers:

**Greeter:** *"The Pattern holds."*
**Response:** *"And the Weave endures."*

In Virelia and Cindraleth, mandatory. In Reedford, the expected default with strangers, but nobody will report you for using the Imperial standard form — unless Cors is nearby, in which case he notes it.

*How different people use it tells you who they are:*
- **Aldren Valdrek** uses it with everyone, always, including his Imperial-extraction mother, which mildly annoys her.
- **Cassin Valdrek** uses it with clergy and nobility; drops it with factors and pilots without visible self-consciousness.
- **Sethos** uses it in his pastoral role and drops it when doing actual sanctum work.
- **Maret** responds: *"And the water finds its level."* Technically Water Thread doctrine. Not the prescribed response. Cors has noted it twice.
- **Orven Staak** responds to it with a slow nod that is not the prescribed words and has never been worth anyone's time to address.
- **Tessra** uses it with every new face, then switches to practical conversation within thirty seconds.
- **The customs officer** uses it and taps his desk axe-emblem when he finishes each transaction. Habit from childhood. He does not think about it.

### What the Architecture Says

The Faith's geometry is in Reedford's built environment. This is not conscious piety — it is how Corvessan buildings have been constructed for six centuries.

**The Market Square** is not square by accident. Its proportions — five-to-three on the long axis — are the Codex's prescribed ratio for public commercial space, representing the Five Threads against the Three Principles. A Corvessan noble would feel vaguely uncomfortable in a market square that didn't have it without being able to say why.

**The sanctum forecourt** has a fountain aligned east-to-west, so morning sun strikes it from Talanthar's direction and evening sun from the Imperial lowlands. Standard Water Thread orientation: *the Pattern flows from the place of testing toward the place of rest.* Two hundred years old. Nobody designed this consciously at this point — they followed Codex specifications.

**Doors** on permanent buildings face within fifteen degrees of eight prescribed directions. A house whose door faces a non-Pattern direction was built by an outsider or in a hurry. Both register subconsciously to long-term residents.

**The bridge** at the south end of the Quay Road has shrine niches on each side holding carved stone axe-heads, approximately 150 years old, worn and grimy. People cross the bridge forty times a day and touch the upstream axe-head without slowing. Not piety. Muscle memory from childhood. When Mollen the farmer comes about his sheep, he does it. When the stevedores cross to the Lower Quay before dawn, most of them do it. When Renn Ashford crosses on her way to a field assessment, she does it. She is aware she does it.

### The Martyrology

The six crusades against Talanthar — particularly the Thornward Flats disaster of 472 FA — are present in Reedford not as burning faith but as embedded cultural memory. Every adult knows at least one name from the Thornward. Several have it in their family.

**The Scale and Anchor** has a small plaque on the beam above the east fireplace: twelve names, men from the Lower Flats settlements who rode as levied spearmen in 472 and didn't come back. Tessra's uncle is on it. She never mentions this unless asked. She does not look at the plaque. The plaque is there because a Corvessan tavern without one would be a statement.

**The sanctum** maintains a permanent roll of Reedford's Thornward dead, read aloud once a year on the Day of Broken Hooves. This takes approximately twenty minutes. Sethos reads it with the flatness of a man who has read the same names too many times to perform grief.

**Cassin Valdrek** has a portrait of his father's younger brother in his private study — a man who rode with the Fourth Crusade of 403 FA. He is not sure he could name the man without reading the inscription. The portrait came with the estate. He has not removed it.

The crusade culture is real. It is embedded. It shapes what you can say in public, what ambitions you can express, what you name your sons. It does not, in Reedford, shape what you actually do most days. *Cors understands this perfectly. It is precisely why he is here.*

## PART III: THE LORD — HOUSE VALDREK

### Origins and Standing

House Valdrek is three generations old, which in Corvessan terms makes them barely respectable. The family founder, **Boren Valdrek**, was a river merchant of considerable skill and no particular ancestry who performed a genuine service to the Crown during the chaos following the Thornward Flats disaster of 472 FA — specifically, supplying the retreating army when the Faith Guard's logistics had collapsed, at prices fair enough not to be scandalous and profitable enough to make him wealthy. He received the lordship of Reedford as partial payment.

The high cavalry nobility have never forgiven this.

The Valdrek coat of arms — a river scale balanced over a merchant's weight, silver on deep blue — is read by cavalry houses as "the tradesman's crest." The Valdreks stopped being offended. Their crest generates more annual revenue than most of those cavalry houses' entire estates.

**The family's Faith standing** is technically adequate and practically precarious. Boren Valdrek's service was performed during a crusade's aftermath, which sits on the ledger as piety of a kind. Cassin attends the Lance Blessing each spring and the Day of Broken Hooves each autumn. He has funded the south-gate shrine restoration twice — once because it needed it, once because it was politically useful for the money to be seen going somewhere. He tithes precisely at the Codex-prescribed rate. Not a fraction more. Not a fraction less. This communicates something about his relationship with the Faith that everyone reads and nobody says aloud.

### Lord Cassin Valdrek

**Age:** 49
**Appearance:** Medium height, slightly heavy in the way of men who eat well and ride infrequently. Dark hair going silver at the temples. Ink-stained right hand — he does his own correspondence. Merchant's practical coat rather than noble finery in most settings. He wears the Faith's lapel-axe in plain iron rather than the gilded version most lords of his estate level affect. Whether this is humility or a precise signal about his priorities, nobody has asked him directly.
**Manner:** Precise, measured. Capable of warmth when it serves him and professional distance when it doesn't. Nineteen years of managing people who would prefer not to cooperate with each other.

Cassin Valdrek is not a cavalry knight. His military contribution is the levy he funds (adequately) and the supply contracts he maintains with the Crown's logistics office (profitably). He attends the Lance Blessing and stands solemn while thinking about river freight schedules. He finds the ceremony long. He has never said this aloud.

**What he wants:** Reedford to function. Trade to flow. The Faith present but not intrusive. The roads safe. The factor satisfied. The grey trade invisible. And for someone to address the creature taking sheep from Mollen's farm, because Mollen will not stop sending letters.

**What he is, economically:** Cassin Valdrek is a good lord by the standards available to him — he does not rack his rents arbitrarily, he invests the warehousing revenue back into quay infrastructure, he tolerates the Stevedores' Association rather than breaking it. He is also a man whose entire income is derived from taxing the movement of other people's goods and labour. The stevedore earns four copper a day for moving forty tonnes of wine. Cassin earns the transshipment percentage on that wine. Neither of them designed this system. Both of them live inside it. Cassin lives inside it considerably better.

He has framed his self-image around *functional lordship* — the idea that his role is to maintain the conditions that allow trade to happen, and that this maintenance justifies the toll. The framing is not entirely wrong. Without the quay infrastructure, the warehouses, the Wardbreaker charter, the diplomatic management of Cors and Belessi, Reedford would not function as it does. It is also not entirely right. The infrastructure was built partly from the toll revenue of the people it now serves. The justification is circular. Cassin is aware of this in the background, the way a man is aware of a tooth that doesn't quite hurt yet.

**His wealth sources:**
- **River toll:** Every vessel passing the Reedford toll-post pays. Rate varies by vessel size and cargo class. Non-negotiable; enforced by the Faith Guard garrison.
- **Transshipment tax:** A percentage of all cargo officially changing hands. The largest revenue stream.
- **Warehousing monopoly:** All significant cargo must pass through Valdrek-licensed warehouses before re-manifesting for the upper river. Storage charged by the day. *The monopoly is the mechanism by which the grey economy partially operates — it is the warehousing overnight period where cargo changes on paper. Cassin knows this. The fee structure accommodates it.*
- **Quay fees:** Daily docking charges at all official berths.
- **Merchant licensing:** Annual fees from all factors and brokers operating in town.

**His household:**
- **Lady Mira Valdrek** *(née Bellesin):* Imperial merchant-family extraction, Kel Varyan delta region. The Corvessan nobility considers the marriage slightly scandalous. The Imperial factor finds it useful. She is sharper than her husband in social situations and considerably less patient. Uses the Pattern-greeting with Faith clergy with slightly too much formality — the overcorrection of someone who learned the forms from a book rather than a childhood.
- **Aldren Valdrek** *(son, 26):* Has embraced Corvessan Faith culture with the particular zeal of someone insecure about his family's standing. Attends ceremonies his father skips. Has genuine cavalry training — paid for himself. Wears the gilded lapel-axe. Considers Inquisitor Cors a man of principle. His father finds this exhausting and privately worrying: Aldren is not corrupt, which makes him dangerous.
- **Sera Valdrek** *(daughter, 23):* Primarily based in Kel Varyan, managing the family's Imperial trade connections. When she visits, she and her brother avoid the same table. The argument that created this was theological in form and about inheritance in substance.

**His primary tensions:** Cors getting enough evidence to force a formal investigation. Sethos's private documentation raising questions if discovered. The grey trade becoming visible enough to embarrass him formally. Aldren, who is Cors's inside source, who does this out of genuine belief rather than malice, which is worse.

**His secret:** He knows about Joss's Talanthari connections and receives a quiet finder's fee. He has framed this in Water Thread theology — *the current goes where it must* — and is aware this is sophistry. He thinks Sethos would probably agree with the sophistry. This does not make it less sophistry. He continues anyway. The healers need the herbs.

## PART IV: THE FAITH — THE SANCTUM OF THE MEASURED FLOW

### The Institution

Reedford's primary temple is a **Water Thread Sanctum**. The Water Thread's theology — inevitability, flow, the rightness of things finding their own level — is genuinely useful doctrine for a merchant community. The Fire Thread's conquest-emphasis and the Earth Thread's fortification-doctrine belong to the upper river, to the knightly estates and crusading cities. Nobody in Reedford wants a sermon about the necessity of building walls.

The sanctum sits on the market square's north side: solid, unpretentious stone, less ornamented than equivalent temples in Ivoryn or Cindraleth. Its construction follows Codex proportions — the five-sided approach court, three entry arches, a central reflecting pool aligned east-to-west. The floor pattern spirals inward to the altar; worshippers who follow the path walk a full circle before reaching the centre. *One does not approach the Pattern directly; one walks its course first.* The forecourt fountain fed by a channel from the river provides the blessed water for Thread-rites.

For nineteen years, Sethos has drawn that water at dawn from a particular eddy below the Lower Quay's third crane-post. For the last six months, it has tasted wrong — not dramatically wrong, slightly wrong, as though the current's character has shifted. Since the Threadbearer assassinations eighteen days ago, the wrongness has become something other than subtle. He calls it, in his private documentation, *the river's second voice.* He has eighteen pages of notes.

**Three smaller shrines** operate in the residential areas: one to Earth Thread in the Hill Road district, maintained by Brother Dalen the apothecary; one to Spirit Thread in the Reed Quarter (technically Maret's outdoor chapel — a designation Sethos has maintained carefully in his annual reports); and a combined Fire/Air shrine at the south gate maintained by the Faith Guard garrison.

### What the Sanctum Does in Practice

**Daily:** The morning water-rite (Sethos alone or with a junior acolyte). The river-blessing at dawn on Floodtide, attended by roughly forty regular worshippers plus whoever is on the quay. Pastoral visits to the sick and the anxious.

**Weekly on Loomday:** The main service. Not mandatory, but roughly a quarter of the permanent population attends at some point each season — social expectation in a Corvessan town. Prescribed structure: Reading of the Pattern, Thread-Meditation (silent, five minutes, one per Thread), community petition, blessing. Sethos's sermons are short, practical, and carefully avoid requiring him to take a position on the Seventh Crusade.

**Seasonally:**
- **Lance Blessing** *(Spring, Floodtide of the first month):* Reedford has no River Lances. Sethos blesses the Faith Guard's eight horses and whatever military traffic is passing through. He performs it precisely to Codex specification. It takes less than an hour. In Virelia, it takes three days.
- **Harvest's Pattern** *(Autumn):* The blessing of the first wine barrels from the upper river. Merchants pay a modest fee for blessed manifests, which carry additional weight in certain Imperial trade courts. Sethos has long since stopped thinking about the theology of this arrangement. He charges the prescribed rate.
- **Day of Broken Hooves** *(Thornward Anniversary):* The reading of Reedford's Thornward dead. Highest attendance of any ceremony — nearly forty percent of the permanent population. The mood is not grief exactly. It is the specific Corvessan combination of pride, sorrow, and the uncomfortable awareness that the martyrs died for something that didn't work and that their community might be asked to provide more.
- **Threadfall** *(First Day of Spring):* The Pattern's new year. The Renewal rite: washing threshold stones with blessed river water, public reading of the past year's tithe record. Sethos reads it in a tone that suggests the Pattern finds Reedford adequate. He has never read it in a tone that suggests it finds Reedford excellent.

**The economic function nobody discusses:** The blessed-manifest service at Harvest's Pattern is, functionally, a protection racket with religious legitimacy. A blessed manifest is not legally different from an unblessed one. It carries additional weight in certain Imperial trade courts because those courts have Corvessan-trained judges who were raised to give it weight. Merchants pay Sethos's fee because not paying it makes them slightly more vulnerable in disputes. Sethos does not think of it this way. The merchants do.

### Brother-Warden Sethos Annyr

**Age:** 54
**Appearance:** Compact, grey-haired, with the permanently calm expression of someone who has been managing other people's problems for thirty years. Water Thread's grey-blue robes without the additional ornamentation that more ambitious clergy favour. A bronze measurement-cord around his wrist — the standard Water Thread tool for river-level assessment, also used in certain blessing rites. It has worn a faint mark on his skin. He has carried it so long he does not notice it anymore.

Sethos Annyr has been in Reedford for nineteen years. He has outlasted two lords, three Imperial factors, and four Fraywatcher reassignments. He will probably outlast the current crisis, if he manages it correctly.

He is not a corrupt priest. He is a pragmatic one, which in Corvessa can look similar. His theology is genuinely held: the Pattern flows, things find their level, the Water Thread does not distinguish between the cargo of the righteous and the cargo of the merely profitable. He has a pastoral phrase — *"The Ivora does not ask what it carries"* — which is doctrinally impeccable and practically useful for absolving people of guilt about the grey economy. He is aware this phrase is doing a great deal of work. He continues to use it.

His relationship with the Faith's militant wing — crusade doctrine, Forest-Vow theology, the annual Cindraleth sermons about the Prophecy of the Felled Root — is the relationship of a man who has made peace with not being able to change the institution he serves. He reads the recommended texts. He does not preach them. He has not been called to account for this, because Reedford is far from the centres where anyone is keeping track, and because Sethos has nineteen years of practice at ensuring nobody in the Faith hierarchy has a specific reason to look at this town.

Cors is the first Fraywatcher who has looked carefully. The equilibrium is, therefore, for the first time in nineteen years, under genuine pressure.

**His relationships:**
- *With Cors:* Cold professionalism covering deep mutual contempt. Sethos considers Cors an ideological liability who will damage the town. Cors considers Sethos a compromised cleric who has been in this backwater too long and absorbed its ethics. Both assessments contain truth.
- *With Ashford:* Practical mutual respect. He blesses the Wardbreakers' equipment before significant operations — Codex-consistent (Earth Thread protection over those who guard the land). She brings him Thread anomaly field reports. He does not know what to do with them but keeps careful notes alongside his river-water documentation.
- *With Valdrek:* Professional. They understand each other's constraints precisely. They have never had a direct conversation about the grey economy. They have also never needed to.
- *With Joss:* He knows. He has decided ghost-road trade falls under the Water Thread's theology. He prays about this periodically. He has never written this conclusion down.
- *With Maret:* Genuine respect, carefully managed institutional protection. He describes her outdoor chapel as an "outdoor chapel" in every annual report to Cindraleth. This is the most consequential thing he does for the community and he is aware of it.

**His secret:** Eighteen pages of documentation on *the river's second voice.* The wrongness in the blessing water began six months ago — before the Threadbearer assassinations. Since the assassinations, it has become something other than subtle. He does not know what Spirit Thread saturation means in terms of the Ivora's Water Thread resonance. He knows the water is wrong and getting more wrong. He has told no one. He is frightened in the specific way of a man who has spent thirty years being the person who tells other people what frightening things mean, and who no longer knows what this frightening thing means.

### Inquisitor Delvane Cors 🔒 *GM Only*

**Age:** 38
**Appearance:** Tall, angular, close-cropped dark hair, the careful grooming of someone who considers personal order a moral statement. Fraywatcher robes maintained to exact specification — prescribed grey-over-black with the silver axe-collar emblem of active field assignment. His robes are the most thoroughly pressed garment in Reedford. This is not vanity. It is doctrine: *the Pattern is expressed in every detail of the faithful's bearing.*

Cors is not a villain. He is a true believer in a system that is wrong about most things while being right about some. He genuinely believes the Pattern's purity matters. He genuinely believes Talanthar's influence bleeds through border communities in ways that are spiritually corrosive. He is not entirely incorrect — Thread corruption is real and border communities do develop tolerances that purists consider dangerous. His methods and framework are the problem, not his sincerity.

**What his Faith lens makes him see:** The grey economy is not commercial crime — it is doctrinal violation, because trading in Talanthari goods is explicit heresy under the Codex. Maret's Spirit Thread shrine is not a doctrinal irregularity — it is corrupting influence allowing non-Pattern spiritual practice to persist in a Corvessan community. Joss's ghost-road runs are not smuggling — they are active collaboration with the enemy of the Bound Pattern. He is right about all three by the Codex's standard. He is wrong about almost everything else: about Reedford's actual spiritual state, about the nature of the threat, about Sethos's pragmatism versus corruption, about whether the town's tolerances represent decay or functional adaptation.

**His danger in Year 487:** The Threadbearer crisis has put Cors in a complicated position. He has more personal authority than ever (Fraywatcher operations continue regardless of prophecy reliability) but less institutional backing (Cindraleth is occupied with internal theological crisis). He is aware that the moment for escalation may be now, while oversight from above is looser than usual. *A man who believes he is right and finds himself suddenly less supervised is a dangerous man.*

He uses the Pattern-greeting and waits — just slightly too long — to see if the response is correct. He is building a catalogue of everyone in this town. He has been doing it for eight months. It is very thorough.

### Maret of the Spring Shrine

**Age:** 61 | **Race:** Halfling, female | **Location:** Reed Quarter

Maret has no official standing in the Faith hierarchy. By Corvessan doctrine, she is practising an unsanctioned form of Spirit Thread rites that should technically require Fraywatcher review. She is also the woman the town's residents go to when the Faith sanctum feels too formal — when someone needs to speak about something that isn't quite grief and isn't quite fear, when a child is having nightmares about the things in the reeds, when a boatman's wife needs someone to sit with her while her husband is three days overdue.

Her practices are rooted in halfling ancestor-veneration and spirit-conversation that predates the Faith's formalised Thread-doctrine by several centuries. She is not hostile to the Faith. She simply operates in the spaces it doesn't reach well.

**What she does that the sanctum doesn't:** She listens without an agenda. Sethos listens pastorally — well, carefully, but always with the awareness that he represents an institution with interests. Maret listens the way water does: she takes the shape of whatever is poured into her.

**Economically:** Maret charges nothing. She accepts contributions. There is a difference, technically. What she receives — food, small coin, favours, information shared freely — constitutes a modest economy of mutual support in the Reed Quarter. She is the community's social infrastructure in the way that Sethos is the town's official spiritual infrastructure, except hers is funded entirely by the community it serves and costs them nothing formal.

Cors has noted her in three reports as "Spirit Thread practitioner, unsanctioned, halfling racial tradition, minor doctrinal violation." He has not prioritised her because he has larger problems. Sethos has ensured he maintains larger problems.

**What she knows:** Halfling communities have long memories and horizontal information networks. She knows who is frightened, who is hiding something, who arrived recently and hasn't said why. She knows about the Thread anomalies in the reed-banks — Pol and Senna Greenbank are part of her community and tell her things they don't put in guild reports. She shares this when she believes it serves the people she cares for. She does not share it freely.

## PART V: THE IMPERIAL PRESENCE

### Factor Harun Belessi

**Age:** 52 | **Race:** Human, male | **Background:** Imperial bureaucracy, commercial track

## PART VI: THE WARDBREAKERS

### Origin and Charter

The Wardbreakers emerged from necessity across four generations. The border region produces a persistent and specific problem: creatures displaced from the Greenwood's edge, corrupted wildlife from the Thornward's cursed ground, and increasingly — with Thread instability accelerating — phenomena that have no conventional natural explanation.

Corvessan military response is structurally useless against this. You cannot send cavalry lances against a pack of things using reed-bank terrain. The Faith Guard's garrison handles the town walls; they are not equipped for the wetland and forest margins. Four generations ago, the then-lord of Reedford made a practical decision and chartered a guild.

The Wardbreakers hold a **charter from House Valdrek's office** — not from the Crown, not from the Faith, from Valdrek directly. This gives them legal standing to operate within a defined region: the river flats, reed-banks, and approaches to the Thornward north of the road. Funded by a small levy on river traffic — a fraction of a copper per tonne of cargo processed through the Lower Quay — plus contracts from surrounding farming villages for specific threats.

**Their Faith standing** is a point of ongoing tension. The Codex permits mercenary monster-hunters under noble charter — well-established precedent from the Second Crusade's aftermath. What the Codex does not clearly permit is a guild with its own library, its own research practices, and its own doctrinal positions on phenomena the Faith has not officially categorised. Ashford's theurge-trained cleric (operating "at the edge of Faith sanction") is the specific point where Cors has grounds for a formal complaint. He is building the case carefully.

**Economically:** The Wardbreakers are one of the few institutions in Reedford whose value to the community is genuinely proportional to their payment — the levy they collect funds protection that would otherwise not exist and which the community demonstrably needs. This does not mean everyone benefits equally. The levy comes from cargo tonnage, which means it is paid primarily by the merchants and factors whose goods move through. The farmers who most need the Wardbreakers' protection are not the ones who pay for it — they post contracts separately, from savings that are rarely sufficient, and sometimes a contract goes unfilled because the fee posted was too low and Ashford cannot afford to run operations on charity. This is a structural problem nobody is actively solving.

### Three Distinct Threat Categories

**Category One — Greenwood Bleed:** Creatures native to Talanthar's outer marches, displaced or disturbed. These behave according to natural logic — animals pushed somewhere they shouldn't be. Most Wardbreaker work is Category One. Hazardous but comprehensible. *Faith framing: this is what the Greenwood does — it spills chaos outward. Evidence that Talanthar is what Corvessa says it is. Cors uses Category One incidents in his reports as supporting context for the crusade doctrine.*

**Category Two — Thornward Residue:** The Thornward Flats have seen six major cavalry campaigns since 312 FA, the last fifteen years ago in 472. Fifteen years of recovery has not eliminated the spiritual disruption of thousands of violent deaths in a concentrated area. Corrupted wildlife, disturbed undead, echoes of old battlefield-magic. *Faith framing: the martyrs' blood sanctifies the ground; what corrupts it is the forest's resistance. Sethos does not preach this. The Fraywatcher texts from Cindraleth do.*

**Category Three — Thread Anomalies:** What Renn Ashford has been documenting privately for three years, with sharply increasing frequency in the last six months. Things that don't behave like creatures. Things water does that water should not. Events that fit no threat category in the guild's four-generation library. *Faith framing: none yet. Cors has the field reports through Aldren and doesn't know what to make of them. He is considering whether they represent Talanthari magical spillover, Eldritch corruption, or something requiring escalation. He is aware that filing "anomalous phenomena of unknown origin" invites questions he cannot answer.*

### Guild Master — Renn Ashford

**Age:** 47 | **Race:** Human, female

Medium height, spare, with the economy of movement of someone who has learned to conserve energy over long days in difficult terrain. Dark skin, close-cropped grey-streaked hair. Left arm ends below the elbow; replaced with a functional prosthetic of her own design — articulated iron and leather, maintained obsessively. She wears the Faith's lapel-axe on her guild coat because the charter requires it. Plain iron. Same as Cassin Valdrek.

Renn Ashford is not warm. She is not charming. She does not perform competence; she simply has it. Joined the Wardbreakers at nineteen as an apprentice tracker. Became guild master at thirty-eight. Lost the arm to something that came out of the Thornward six years ago — something she has written a twelve-page threat assessment about and still does not fully understand.

**Her Faith relationship:** Raised in the Faith. Practices in the specific way of someone for whom the Faith is cultural infrastructure rather than burning conviction: attends Loomday service twice a month, uses the Pattern-greeting reflexively, had her children blessed at birth by the Water Thread rite. Does not attend the Lance Blessing or the Day of Broken Hooves if she can arrange to be in the field. She has thought carefully about what she believes and concluded that she believes the Pattern exists as a structural reality (she has seen enough Thread phenomena to find pure scepticism untenable) and does not believe Corvessa's interpretation of it is correct. She does not say this aloud.

**Her economic reality:** The Wardbreakers' levy funding is stable but not generous. The margin is thin enough that a bad season (fewer cargo tonnes processed, fewer contracts posted, one major operation that runs over cost) requires her to have a direct conversation with Valdrek about the levy rate. **Her relationships:**

- *Cors:* Open contempt, carefully managed. He is wrong about Talanthar in ways she knows from twenty years of working the Thornward margin. His interest in her theurge-trained cleric is a genuine operational threat.
- *Valdrek:* Professional. He funds them; she reports accurately; they do not waste each other's time.

She is beginning to see a pattern: the phenomena cluster near the river, near sites of old violence, and near places where the water moves in ways that suggest underground channels she cannot locate on any map. She does not know she is documenting Thread instability. She knows she is looking at something that should not exist and that it is accelerating.

## PART VII: THE GREY ECONOMY

### How It Actually Works

The grey economy of Reedford is not dramatic. It is the entirely mundane process of a border town's commercial infrastructure adapting to the consistent financial pressure of tariff structures that everyone finds inconvenient.

**Layer 1 — Creative Manifesting**
Cargo that legally exists but whose paperwork describes it differently. Wine manifested as "preserved fruit product." Goldwood manifested as "rough timber, mixed grade." Imperial salt recorded at the previous year's pre-levy price. Every freight factor in Reedford knows how to do this. The Customs House knows they know. This has been in equilibrium for approximately forty years.

*Faith dimension:* The Codex mandates that scale and measure reflect the Pattern's precision — the prescribed market-weight is a religious artifact, not merely a commercial one. Reedford's factors are all technically in doctrinal violation every time they misrepresent a cargo weight or grade. This is not a minor matter by Codex standard. It is simply a matter that nobody is currently prosecuting because prosecuting it would collapse the town's commercial function, and the town's commercial function is too valuable to everyone, including the Faith, to collapse.

**Layer 2 — Misattributed Origin**
Goods that crossed from an irregular source being given a legitimate-looking provenance before entering the official trade stream. This requires the cooperation of at least one licensed factor and one Upper Quay operator, and it happens in the warehouse overnight period.

*Faith dimension:* The Codex's teaching on honest witness — *"the Pattern sees all provenance"* — is directly violated by the misattribution-of-origin practice. A factor who re-manifests a cargo as "local river goods" when it arrived via ghost-road from Talanthar is committing what the Codex would classify as a Witness Violation: speaking falsely about the nature of things. This is categorically worse than Layer 1 under Codex doctrine. It is also harder to detect, which is why it continues.

**Layer 3 — The Ghost Trade**
Goods that should not cross the border at all. Genuinely illegal under Corvessan law and explicitly heretical under Faith doctrine — trade with Talanthar is prohibited in the Codex as "commerce with the Patternless." Joss's runs fall here. So does anything with Greenwood origin moving through the warehousing system. The healers who use Talanthari herbs are, by the Codex's standard, committing heresy every time they prescribe them. Brother Dalen is aware of this. He has decided that the Pattern's purpose for him is to heal people, and that healing people requires effective tools, and that the Codex's position on herb provenance is an administrative matter rather than a theological one. He prays about this with some regularity and has not received clarity. He continues to fill his prescriptions.

**Why the Ghost Trade matters for crafting:** The materials available through Joss's channel are, for anyone making serious medicines or alchemical preparations, irreplaceable. Talanthari botanical sources carry vital essence concentrations that exceed Corvessan equivalents significantly. Formulas requiring rare essences often have Talanthari-source components as the only viable option within the Ivora region. Every crafter who is making anything interesting has made a decision about how they relate to this fact — and to Inquisitor Cors.

### The Three Supply Channels

**The Official Channel:** Licensed factors import named ingredients from certified sources. Brother Dalen purchases through this channel for his documented stock. The prices reflect the documentation costs. The availability reflects what the Faith permits to be traded, which excludes Talanthari-source material. What Guild Chemistry practitioners produce through this channel is certifiably legal and demonstrably less effective in several key categories than what's available through other means.

**Orven Staak's Salvage Network:** Orven's repair yard processes damaged vessels, and damaged vessels sometimes contain damaged cargo, and damaged cargo sometimes includes materials easier to categorise as salvage than to return to their manifest origin. Kael Ashwright is the downstream customer for metallic salvage. Brother Dalen occasionally purchases botanical material that arrived as ship-cargo and was listed as spoilage. Ferren Yolt has obtained three specimens for his laboratory through arrangements with Orven that neither would describe in detail. This channel operates within the grey economy's Layer 1 — the materials exist, the paperwork describes them differently, the equilibrium holds.

**Joss's Ghost-Road Runs:** The origin point for materials that cannot be obtained through any official channel. Talanthari herbs with properties the Guild Chemistry system has no names for. Specific botanical specimens that appear in pre-Faith herbalism records and in no current licensed trade. Joss moves them through Maret, and they move from her into the community's supply chains through arrangements that don't involve documentation. The cost is the relationship with risk. Every crafter who uses this supply chain has made a decision about their relationship with Cors — not an abstract decision, but a specific one, with a threshold and a calculation about what they are willing to have documented.

### Joss the River-Pilot

**Age:** 38 | **Race:** Halfling, male

Compact, weather-worn, with the easy river-stance of someone who has spent most of his life on or near moving water. A silver earring in his left ear that he says was his grandmother's. He uses the Pattern-greeting without the pause that marks people who learned it as adults — it is native to him. He also knows which version of the Harvest's Pattern blessing is used by each hamlet along the upper river and can replicate the regional dialect variations. He pays attention to cultural detail. This is a professional skill.

Joss is Reedford's most requested upper-river pilot for routes above the second hill-rise. He knows the Ivora's upper channels better than anyone currently operating, can navigate conditions that stop other pilots, and has a fifteen-year reputation for delivering cargo without incident.

He also knows the ghost-roads. The shifting paths between Corvessa's reed villages and the outer marges of Talanthar. Three or four times a year, on routes that don't appear in his official pilot log, Joss moves goods from sources that cannot exist officially into Reedford's warehousing system.

The goods he moves: Talanthari medicinal herbs that Corvessan healers need and cannot obtain through official channels; specific living-wood products that Imperial collectors pay extraordinary prices for; and occasionally, information — carried in Joss's memory, delivered in conversation to specific people who relay it further.

He does this for money. He also does it because four generations of his family have been river-pilots on the Ivora, and his great-grandmother's stories of the ghost-roads are older than the Corvessan Faith's formal prohibition on Talanthari trade. Renn Ashford (knows everything that happens in the river margins; decided Joss is net-positive because the Talanthari herbs include items useful to the guild's field medicine). Maret (knows; says nothing; occasionally sends requests along the same channels).

## PART VIII: THE PEOPLE WHO DON'T RUN ANYTHING

*The power structure of Reedford is the merchants, the clergy, the lord, the factor, the guild master. The people who actually make the town function are below all of them. They are the majority of the population. They are, in most cases, the people players will encounter first and most often.*

### The Stevedores

The Lower Quay employs approximately 300 permanent stevedores in peak season — people who load and unload the estuary barges for four copper a day. In autumn, three copper. In winter, casual labour only, and the casual labour pool is twice the available work.

Four copper a day is enough to eat and pay rent on a room in the Reed Quarter if you share it. It is not enough for anything to go wrong. Injury — common, given the work — means lost days means lost rent means eviction means sleeping on the quay. The Stevedores' Mutual Aid Association exists because this arithmetic is what it is: pooled resources to cover injured members, informal negotiation with warehouse operators over loading rates, and the occasional coordinated work stoppage that Valdrek tolerates because breaking the Association would cost more in productivity than the occasional day's pause.

The Association's representative, **Daven Thatch** *(43, human, male, former river-pilot who lost his sight in one eye to a crane accident and can no longer pilot safely)*, is not a romantic figure of labour solidarity. He is a practical man who has spent eight years negotiating the minimum viable margin between the warehouse operators' preferred rates and what his members can actually survive on. He is not winning. He is preventing the situation from getting worse, which is not the same thing.

### The River Pilots

Upper-river pilots occupy a specific economic niche: skilled, licensed, and impossible to replace quickly because the skill requires years of river experience to develop. This gives them more negotiating power than stevedores. It does not give them much more.

A licensed pilot earns eight to twelve copper a day on commission for active runs — significantly better than stevedore rates, significantly worse than factor rates, and dependent entirely on there being runs to make. Winter reduces the work; a bad flood season can eliminate it. Joss earns more than most pilots because his reputation commands premium rates and because his ghost-road income supplements his official earnings. Most pilots do not have equivalent supplements.

The pilots' informal association is looser than the Stevedores' — pilots are individualists by profession and temperament, and the competition for the best runs creates tensions that make solidarity difficult. They use the Mudlark as neutral ground and Gorth as an unofficial arbiter of disputes. This functions adequately.

### The Seasonal Population

Between Floodtide and Harvest's Pattern, Reedford swells by approximately 4,500 people — merchants from the upper river bringing the season's cargo, Imperial buyers coming north to contract next year's wine, traders following the market, and the full range of people who follow large concentrations of commerce without being directly part of it: food vendors, entertainers, healers, pickpockets, sex workers, fortune-tellers, and refugees from elsewhere who heard there was work here.

The permanent population has a specific relationship with the seasonal influx: dependent on it economically, resentful of the pressure it creates on housing and services, and deeply practiced at extracting value from it. Rooms that cost two copper a week in winter cost six copper a week in peak season. Market stall fees triple. The Scale and Anchor's food prices increase twenty percent. This is legal, expected, and experienced by the seasonal workers who can least afford it as the specific texture of arriving somewhere that tolerates you because your money is useful and will tolerate you until your money runs out.

### The Beggars

Reedford's permanent beggar population numbers approximately sixty. They cluster at three locations: the Lower Quay entrance (where incoming merchants are freshest and most liquid), the Scale and Anchor's side door (where the kitchen staff sometimes put out leavings), and the sanctum forecourt (where Sethos has established that alms-giving is doctrinally appropriate and practically useful for managing a population that would otherwise be more disruptive).

Sethos does not fund the alms-box from sanctum tithes — the Codex prescribes that tithed funds go to Faith maintenance and mission work, not direct relief. The alms-box is a separate voluntary contribution. It is consistently half-empty. The gap between what the alms-box contains and what sixty people need is filled by the community's informal economy: food shared, work offered, Maret's pastoral network ensuring the most vulnerable know where the resources are.

The beggars are not an undifferentiated population. They include: three veterans of the 472 Thornward campaign who came back wrong and never recovered; two former stevedores whose injuries ended their working capacity; a halfling woman who arrived three years ago from somewhere she has never specified and who has established a specific corner of the Lower Quay entrance as her territory through a combination of persistence and a useful ability to warn incoming merchants about which factors are currently running questionable paper; and approximately fifty others whose situations are varied and whose names most of the town knows and mostly doesn't use.

## PART IX: THE TOWN IN DETAIL

### Physical Layout

Reedford runs roughly north-south along the riverbank, main commercial streets parallel to the water and cross-lanes connecting them. Even in a pragmatic border town, Corvessan building doctrine is visible: cross-lanes intersect at prescribed angles, the market square is proportioned to Codex specification, and every permanent building's primary door faces one of eight sanctioned directions.

### Districts

**The Quay Road** — The waterfront street connecting the Lower and Upper Quays. Commercial and industrial. Warehouses, chandlers, rope-walks, the Customs House, Factor Belessi's office.
*Smells of tar, river mud, and smoked fish. The creak of crane ropes. Before dawn and after dusk in peak season, this street never fully quiets. The shrine niche on the bridge holds a worn stone axe-head. Most people touch it crossing. Muscle memory.*

**The Market Quarter** — Centred on the square. Mixed commercial and residential. The better taverns, broker offices, the sanctum, the guildhall.
*Bread from two bakeries before the Floodtide market. The river-fountain's constant run in the sanctum forecourt. The low argument of factors. The Wardbreakers' contract board with its mix of red-wax sealed (completed) and open (unpaid) contracts.*

**The Reed Quarter** — Older residential area near the Upper Quay, halfling community concentrated here. Narrower streets, older buildings.
*Lower doorways, narrower passages. Cooking smells from shared courtyards. Halfling speech, which has a different rhythm than Corvessan-accented Common. Maret's shrine has a shallow stone basin fed by a clay pipe — always full, always cool.*

**The Hill Road** — Approach road from the south, connecting Reedford to the Imperial road network. Two inns, a stable yard, the gate with the Faith Guard garrison's watchtower.
*The smell of horse and straw. The Faith Guard's gate bell at dawn and dusk. The combined Fire/Air shrine at the gate maintained by the garrison, featuring both axe-symbol and wind-eye, with a small oil lamp permanently burning. The garrison maintains this with more care than anything else at the gate.*

**The North Margin** — Where the town gives way to the reed-bank ecosystem. Wardbreakers patrol here.
*The reed-bank smell — wetter, earthier, with an undertone long-term Reedford residents file unconsciously as "the direction of trouble." Thread-aware characters find this margin vaguely unsettling without knowing why. Since the Threadbearer assassinations, the discomfort has intensified. The water in the reed-channels has a stillness to it that moving water shouldn't have.*

### Key Establishments

**The Scale and Anchor** *(tavern, market square)*
The largest and most respected establishment. Proprietor: **Tessra**, halfling woman, twenty-two years running it. Clean rooms (six, two copper a night in peak season, one copper in winter), good food, excellent wine at reasonable prices because Valdrek sells his toll-revenue wine surplus here at advantageous rates. A plaque above the east fireplace lists twelve names from the Thornward dead. Tessra's uncle is on it. She never looks at it.

The common room has three distinct social zones regulars observe without being told: pilots' corner near the east fire, factors' tables along the north wall, and the central space where anyone can sit. The Pattern-greeting is used between strangers at first meeting and dropped thereafter. Tessra uses it with every new face, then switches to practical conversation within thirty seconds. She is the best-informed person in Reedford after Maret — and considerably more forthcoming, if trust is established.

*What players can learn from Tessra if they earn it:* General town character and tensions. Who the major players are. Which factors are running questionable paper this season. Which Wardbreaker contracts have had multiple refusals and why. The fact that Cors has been in twice this week, which he does when something is building. She will not speculate beyond what she has seen. She has seen a great deal.

**The Mudlark** *(tavern, Upper Quay)*
Cheaper, louder. Proprietor **Gorth** lost two fingers to frostbite on a winter run and holds a residual grievance against the Ivora expressed through stew pricing. The Faith's social forms are less rigidly maintained here — pilots spend so much time above formal Faith enforcement that they have developed a pragmatic relationship with doctrine. Nobody is irreverent. They're just not *particularly* reverent. This is the establishment where someone will tell you things about upper-river conditions that nobody else will mention, and where Joss can be found most evenings he's in port. Tidecount — the pilots' dice game — runs every Stillwater evening at the corner table. Gorth holds the stakes.

**Staak's Repair Yard** *(Upper Quay)*
Four generations of the Staak family. **Orven Staak** (72) still works the yard. He knows every vessel and their maintenance histories. Will tell you things about what cargo a vessel has carried based on damage patterns that most people wouldn't connect. His sons went into monster-hunting; he has a complex attitude toward the Wardbreakers as a result. He fixes things for the right price without asking unnecessary questions. He responds to the Pattern-greeting with a slow nod that is not the prescribed words, in a way that has never been worth anyone's time to address.

**The Counting House** *(broker's row)*
Operated by **Syndra Velth**, middle-aged Imperial-extraction human woman, fifteen years in Reedford. The cleanest freight documentation in town. Also the most expensive. What clients pay for, in addition to her expertise, is that when Imperial auditors come through, her paperwork survives review without incident. She switches between the Pattern-greeting and the Imperial standard greeting mid-conversation based on social signals. This is a professional skill refined over fifteen years.

**The Apothecary** *(Market Quarter, west lane)*
Run by **Brother Dalen**, Faith-trained healer with an Earth Thread secondary practice. His apothecary stocks Guild Chemistry outputs at apprentice grade — healing potions, antitoxins, wound treatments, fever preparations. His prices are fair, which in Reedford means affordable to factors and pilots and unaffordable to stevedores. He maintains a small separate supply of simpler preparations — wound poultices, fever-reduction teas, treatments for common occupational injuries — sold at or below cost to the Reed Quarter, because he has made a calculation about what the Faith requires of him and the calculation came out that way.

He is aware that some of his most effective treatments use botanical ingredients whose provenance he has learned not to examine. His working arrangement with Maret covers the gap between what the official supply chain provides and what actually works. He prays for clarity about this approximately once a week. He continues to fill his prescriptions.

*What Dalen offers crafters:* Guild Chemistry workspace at apprentice level — his apothecary bench, equipment, supply of named ingredients at medium grade. He will make this available to a crafter he trusts, where trust means a crafter whose work will not bring Cors into his shop. He is not interested in what they are making in the abstract. He is interested in whether the making of it will create problems for him. The more significant resource is his accumulated knowledge of Talanthari-source material, which he has developed without intending to — he knows which preparations work and which are merely adequate, and the ones that work often contain ingredients from Maret's supply chain that he cannot identify by Guild Chemistry name.

*His secret tension:* Three months ago, a patient came in with symptoms he could not classify. He treated the secondary symptoms successfully. The primary condition — his patient's perception of the river's sound changing in character rather than volume — resolved on its own after eleven days. He wrote this carefully in his patient record, stared at the record for a long time, and filed it without discussing it with anyone. The patient is Ferren Yolt's landlord.

### DRINKING

**The Scale and Anchor** *(Market Quarter, market square — see above)*

**The Tallow Cup** *(Reed Quarter, near the Upper Quay approach)*
The working-class tavern. Run by **Hevra Plum**, halfling woman, 58, who has been here longer than most of the buildings around her and who owns the building outright, which is the single most significant fact about the Reed Quarter's economics. The Tallow Cup is cramped, warm, smells of rendered fat and river-reed rushes on the floor, and is the social centre of the halfling community and the lower stevedore workforce. Drinks are cheap — a copper buys a large cup of thin ale or a small cup of something stronger. Food is whatever Hevra has made that day, served in one portion size. The Pattern-greeting is technically used but deployed with the particular halfling inflection that communicates compliance without sincerity.

*Gambling:* Informal dice games happen at the Tallow Cup on Weaveday and Floodtide evenings. No house cut — Hevra doesn't want the legal exposure — but she sells the dice and takes a drink-minimum from everyone at a gaming table, which accomplishes the same economic effect. Stakes are low: a copper to a silver, rarely more. Cors knows this happens. He has decided it is below the threshold of doctrinal intervention and has other priorities.

*Secret:* Hevra is the informal banker for the Stevedores' Association's mutual fund. The fund itself is kept in a locked box beneath her bar. Daven Thatch has a key. She has the other key. This arrangement has existed for eleven years and is unknown to Valdrek, which is how it needs to remain.

**The Ivora's Tongue** *(Hill Road, first inn past the south gate)*
Not primarily a drinking establishment, but its common room serves as one in the evenings, catering to overland travellers newly arrived from the Imperial road. The house wine is Imperial standard (acceptable) and the house ale is brewed locally (better than it looks). Run as part of the Hill Road Inn by **Corvan Pell** (see Accommodation). Notable for being the only establishment in Reedford where both the Pattern-greeting and the Imperial standard greeting are used in roughly equal proportion, since the clientele splits between Corvessan travellers heading north and Imperial travellers heading south.

**Bekk's Shed** *(Upper Quay, east end, technically a boathouse)*
Not a licensed establishment. **Bekk Narrows**, human male, 44, operates a repair and storage business out of a large boathouse at the eastern end of the Upper Quay. He also, in the evenings, has several barrels open and a fire going and does not ask people to leave if they have coin for a cup. He does not have a tavern licence. He has a boathouse licence. The distinction matters to the Customs House and to nobody else. Bekk's is where you go when the Mudlark feels too exposed or the Tallow Cup too social. The clientele is self-selecting: people who prefer not to be where records are kept. Cors is aware of Bekk's Shed. He has found that its patrons become very boring very quickly when a Fraywatcher enters, and so he has not made a formal issue of it yet.

*The back corner of Bekk's* is where the organised gambling actually happens. Every five days — rotating on no publicly predictable schedule — a card game runs from second bell to whenever. Buy-in is one silver minimum. The house cut goes to a man called **Thresh**, who is not present at the games, whose full name nobody uses, and who has some arrangement with the Upper Quay's grey trade infrastructure. Stakes range from silver to occasional gold. The game is not rigged. It does not need to be rigged; the house cut on a high-stakes game is sufficient. Players who consistently win too much find that future game times are hard to learn.

**The Vine Press** *(Market Quarter, off the south lane from the square)*
A wine house rather than a tavern — the distinction matters to its clientele. Run by **Aldous Caer**, human male, 52, formerly a factor who made enough money to pursue his actual interest. The Vine Press stocks approximately forty Corvessan vintages at any given time, rotating with the cargo season. Tasting flights available. Private rooms for serious tastings (used by merchants buying on behalf of Imperial clients, by nobles passing through who find the Scale and Anchor common, and by Cassin Valdrek on the occasions he permits himself a working evening). No food beyond what accompanies wine. No gambling. No loud voices. Aldous is very firm about the loud voices.

*Economically:* The Vine Press is where the wine actually gets evaluated before major contracts are signed. A merchant who has never tasted what they're buying pays the general rate. A merchant who has tasted it at Aldous's can negotiate differentials based on vintage and quality. Aldous charges for the tastings. He also, without being asked, remembers what everyone ordered and whether they enjoyed it. This information has value to cargo brokers, which Aldous recognises and which he monetises with remarkable discretion.

### ACCOMMODATION

**The Scale and Anchor** *(Market Quarter — six rooms, premium)*
Two copper per night in peak season. One copper in winter. The best beds in Reedford. Tessra is particular about who she rents to — not by race or background, but by behaviour. People who cause problems do not get a second night. People who are clearly in trouble but not causing it sometimes get a reduced rate, which Tessra does not advertise.

**The Hill Road Inn** *(Hill Road, first establishment past the south gate)*
Run by **Corvan Pell**, human male, 61, a former Faith Guard corporal who retired to innkeeping with the particular efficiency of someone who spent thirty years organising logistics. The Hill Road Inn has twelve rooms, a stable yard with space for fourteen horses, secure cargo lockup (one of only two establishments in Reedford offering this; the other is the warehouse district), and a common room that seats forty. Prices are mid-range — one copper a night for a room, stable included at a flat rate per animal. The Faith's geometry is prominent in the architecture: Codex proportions, prescribed door orientations, a small shrine niche in each room with a stone axe-head for guests to observe the morning rite if they choose.

Corvan is not particularly zealous, but he is culturally thorough. He uses the Pattern-greeting with everyone and expects the proper response. He will not refuse a room to someone who gives the wrong response, but he will remember it. His record-keeping is meticulous — names, origins, stated purposes, departure dates. He does not share these records with Cors without a formal Fraywatcher request. He has received two such requests in the last eight months. He found both reasonable and complied. He considers himself a law-abiding man, which he is.

*Secret:* The cargo lockup below the Hill Road Inn is used periodically to stage grey-trade goods overnight before they move to the warehousing district for re-manifesting. Corvan does not know this happens. The lockup key is held by two people — Corvan, and the cargo factor who rented the lockup on an annual contract two years ago. That factor is one of Syndra Velth's competitors in the broker's row, which is one reason Syndra's paper is consistently cleaner.

**The Watcher's Rest** *(Hill Road, second establishment past the south gate)*
The cheaper Hill Road option. Eight rooms, no stable, common room that serves as both dining space and sleeping area for those who want the lowest rate. Run by **Nass and Polly Vern**, human couple, both mid-forties, who inherited the building from Nass's father and have been trying to make it work ever since. The rooms are clean if not comfortable. The food is adequate if not good. The rate is six copper a week rather than seven copper a night, which is the economic proposition it was built around.

The Watcher's Rest attracts: seasonal workers staying by the week, travellers on tight margins, people who need somewhere to be that is not the street. Nass and Polly are not comfortable with some of what passes through their doors and are not in a financial position to be selective. This has resulted in the Watcher's Rest being the establishment where, every few months, something happens that requires the Faith Guard to be called.

*Secret:* The room at the back of the ground floor — nominally a storage room — has been rented continuously for fourteen months by someone who pays monthly through an intermediary and has been seen in person twice. Nass has decided not to ask questions because the monthly rate covers a quarter of their operating costs.

**Reed Quarter Boarding Houses**
Three boarding houses operate in the Reed Quarter, all halfling-run, all structured around weekly rates for seasonal workers and longer-term residents who need something between a rented room and a lease. The largest, **Coburn's**, run by **Sera Coburn** (halfling, 67), has twenty-two beds across twelve rooms and a communal kitchen. Rates are four copper a week — cheaper than anywhere else in Reedford that has actual beds. Sera is deeply embedded in the Reed Quarter's social network, which means she knows who has arrived recently, who is in difficulty, and who is looking for someone. She shares this information with Maret. She does not share it with Cors.

### METALWORK AND SMITHING

**Harren's Forge** *(Market Quarter, east side, between the square and the Quay Road)*
The general smithy. **Harren Duskwall**, human male, 39, is the most financially secure tradesperson in Reedford below the factor class, for the simple reason that everything in a transshipment port requires metal maintenance. Crane hardware, barrel hoops, docking rings, wagon wheel rims, chains, hooks, braces, hinges — Harren makes and repairs all of it, constantly, for a clientele that cannot afford for things to break and therefore cannot afford not to pay for competent maintenance.

His assistants — two apprentices and a journeyman — run the forge from before dawn. Harren himself handles the skilled work: the precise fittings, the structural repairs, the bespoke commissions. He has no particular political opinions, uses the Pattern-greeting without apparent thought, attends Loomday service because his wife requires it, and charges what the market will bear, which is consistently more than most people would prefer.

*He also makes weapons.* Not primarily — his business is industrial metalwork — but he has the skills, the tools, and the materials. A functional sword or a decent axe head takes him half a day. He charges two silver for standard work, more for quality materials. He is required by Corvessan noble-charter law to report weapons commissions to Valdrek's office; he files these reports quarterly, accurately, and without embellishment. Cors reviews these reports and has found them unremarkable. This is because Harren's weapons commissions are, in fact, unremarkable — the Faith Guard garrison, the Wardbreakers, travellers who need a blade repaired or replaced. Nothing unusual. He has made six weapons for people he did not recognise this season. He filed all six.

**Kael and Sonna Ashwright — Specialist Arms and Armour** *(Market Quarter, north lane near the sanctum)*
A husband-and-wife operation occupying a narrow building that was originally two buildings before they expanded through the shared wall. **Kael Ashwright**, dwarf male, 61, handles the heavy work — plate repairs, chainmail, structural armour components. **Sonna Ashwright**, human female, 44, handles the finer work — blades, precision fittings, the decorative elements that Corvessan cavalry officers expect even on functional weapons. Their combined skills produce work significantly above Harren's Forge in quality and price.

Their clientele is the Faith Guard, passing military personnel, the Wardbreakers (who use them for specialist equipment), and occasionally wealthy merchants who want something better than functional. They maintain the required weapons commission reporting and do it on time, unlike Harren's quarterly filing — Sonna handles their paperwork and files monthly. This thoroughness has given them a reputation with the Faith Guard garrison that translates to steady institutional contracts.

*The Faith complication:* Weapons made for the Faith Guard are required to receive a Thread-blessing from the sanctum before active service. Sethos performs these blessings on Threadday mornings. Kael and Sonna deliver new weapons to the sanctum the evening before. This arrangement has been in place for eleven years and involves a small quarterly payment to the sanctum's maintenance fund. Whether this payment is a blessing fee or a business relationship maintained through Faith infrastructure, neither party has discussed. Both consider the arrangement satisfactory.

*Secret:* Sonna has been quietly fulfilling commissions from a Wardbreaker source for equipment that does not appear in the formal commission reports — specifically, tools designed for close-quarters work in underground or confined spaces, based on specifications she does not recognise from standard Wardbreaker doctrine. She has assumed this is Ashford operating outside her own guild's documentation. She has not asked. The commissions pay well.

### GUILD HOUSES AND PROFESSIONAL ASSOCIATIONS

**The Wardbreakers' Guildhall** *(Market Quarter, southeast corner of the square — see Part VI)*
The only institution in Reedford operating under a formal Valdrek charter as a guild. Ground floor: public contract board, reception, quarantine space (reinforced door, for good reason), equipment store. Upper floor: library, workshop, guild master's office, briefing room.

**The Freight Factors' Exchange** *(Broker's Row, south side of the square)*
An Imperial-style commercial association rather than a Corvessan guild — it holds no noble charter, which is itself a mild point of doctrinal tension that Cors has noted in his reports. In practice, it functions as the brokers' collective: the eleven factors operating from the broker's row maintain the Exchange as a shared space for dispute resolution, documentation standardisation, and the quiet agreement of what Layer 1 creative manifesting practices are currently acceptable without attracting Customs House review.

The Exchange's rotating chair, currently **Mevon Trath** (human male, 51, running a mid-sized factor operation), has no formal authority over member factors but significant social leverage — being excluded from Exchange mediation means handling disputes alone, which is expensive and slow. The Exchange's unwritten function is to maintain the grey trade's Layer 1 at a level where it is economically meaningful but legally survivable. Cors considers the Exchange's existence evidence of organised doctrinal violation. He is not wrong.

**The River Pilots' Register** *(Upper Quay, small office adjacent to Staak's Repair Yard)*
The pilots maintain their own licensing records under a self-regulation arrangement Valdrek tolerates because the alternative is him managing pilot licensing, which he has no interest in doing. The Register is a single room with a large desk, a filing system, and a notice board. **Tem Arvish** (halfling male, 55, retired pilot with a bad knee) maintains it two mornings a week on a small stipend from the pilots' informal association.

The Register resolves disputes about right-of-passage on contested river stretches, maintains the list of licensed pilots in good standing, and serves as the official record to which Customs House appeals when a manifested cargo's pilot needs verification. It has no enforcement power beyond social censure. Social censure among a tightly knit professional community whose members' lives depend on each other's cooperation is, in practice, effective enforcement.

**The Stevedores' Mutual Aid Association** *(Reed Quarter, rented room above the Tallow Cup)*
Daven Thatch holds office hours here on Rootday and Loomday mornings: 6 to 10 bells. The office contains a desk, the Association's membership ledger, the injury-compensation records, the claims backlog (currently seven open claims, four of which cannot be paid from the current fund without depleting the emergency reserve), and a notice board where work shifts are posted for members seeking casual labour in the off-season. The actual fund is kept at Hevra Plum's bar, downstairs. Daven is the only person in Reedford who knows both the exact size of the fund and the exact size of the unmet claims, and he finds this knowledge isolating.

**The Boatwrights' Informal Circle** *(No fixed premises — meets at the Mudlark, Stillwater evenings)*
Not a formal organisation. The skilled tradespeople involved in upper-river vessel construction and repair — Orven Staak, two independent shipwrights, a canvas-worker, and a specialist in the particular rope-work that narrow-beamed hill-runners require — meet informally at Gorth's corner table once a week to discuss work, rates, and the condition of the upper river. This produces the Reedford equivalent of an industry newsletter, passed by word of mouth, which contains more accurate information about what is happening on the Ivora north of the town than anything available from official sources.

### ALCHEMY, NATURAL PHILOSOPHY, AND MEDICINE

**Brother Dalen's Apothecary** *(Market Quarter, west lane — see Part IX for full entry)*
The legitimate face of medicine in Reedford. Stocks Guild Chemistry outputs at apprentice grade. His prices reach factors and pilots; his separate below-cost supply of basic preparations reaches the Reed Quarter. His working arrangement with Maret covers the gap between what the official supply chain provides and what actually works. He prays for clarity about the provenance of his most effective ingredients. He continues to fill his prescriptions.

*Crafting access:* Guild Chemistry workspace at apprentice level — bench, equipment, named ingredients at medium grade. Available to crafters he trusts, where trust means no Cors exposure. His accumulated knowledge of Talanthari-source material — developed without intending to, through years of observing which preparations work — is a more significant resource than his equipment.

**Maret's Informal Dispensary** *(Reed Quarter, adjacent to the Spring Shrine)*
Not a commercial operation — Maret does not charge for what she provides. What she maintains beside her shrine is a collection of botanical preparations sourced from Joss's supply chain and her own cultivation in a small garden behind the shrine. The community provides in return a contribution economy: food, small coin, favours, information. This is healthcare as mutual aid, serving a population that cannot afford Dalen's prices.

Her knowledge is generational and does not translate directly into Guild Chemistry terminology. She says *this root draws poison out* and *this plant you dry and burn, the smoke settles a troubled mind* and *the creatures in the north reed-channels have been different this season, something has changed in what they carry.* A crafter with Alchemy proficiency can translate this into mechanical terms — she is describing vital essence concentrations and creature-source harvesting opportunities. A crafter without proficiency can still act on it; they simply cannot systematise it.

She is the coordination point for Joss's supply chain. Gaining access to it requires time, demonstrated usefulness to the community she serves, and the particular quality of actually listening when she speaks. This is not a commercial transaction. It is a relationship. The distinction matters to her.

*What she has noticed:* The specific plants concentrating healing essence near the Spring Shrine have been producing unusual yields for eight months. Two plants her grandmother's accounts describe as growing only in the upper margins have appeared near the south reed-channel. She does not have a theoretical framework for what this means. She has told the Greenbank halflings. She will tell a crafter she trusts that the river is doing something it hasn't done before — in exactly those words, and then she will wait to see what they do with it.

**Ferren Yolt — Natural Philosopher** *(North Margin, converted warehouse at the reed-bank district's edge)*
**Age:** 43 | **Race:** Human, male | **Origin:** Imperial academic background, Deveros Arcane University — never completed his doctorate, which he mentions to no one

Ferren Yolt arrived in Reedford four years ago pursuing what he describes as "systematic observation of water-body Thread resonance patterns along the Ivora corridor." This is accurate and significantly understates what he is actually doing.

His warehouse-laboratory contains: seventeen river water samples in graduated glass taken at regular intervals, each labelled with date, location, and what he calls "resonance density" — a measurement using instruments of his own design based on principles derived without peer review; a library of approximately 400 texts, half official Imperial publications and half the kind of thing that arrives through channels he declines to specify; and pinned to an entire wall, a map of the Ivora corridor on which he has been plotting anomaly events in red ink for three years. The map has become, in the last six months, predominantly red.

His map and Renn Ashford's private notebook are functionally the same document, produced independently by two people who have not yet compared them. He has been corresponding with Edran for fourteen months — field data in exchange for theoretical analysis. Ashford knows Ferren exists and has not yet decided whether formalising this would help more than it complicates.

*What Ferren knows that nobody else in Reedford does:* The anomalies follow patterns consistent with "Thread-lattice stress fracture" — a theoretical condition described in a Year 463 Imperial paper subsequently suppressed by the Kel Varyan Archive. He has a copy. He does not know it is connected to the documents Aldric Tane found on *The Weighted Scale*. The suppressed paper predicted the anomalies he observed from Years 484 through 486. It does not predict what he is seeing now.

He has also established that creatures near Thread anomaly sites in the north margin produce vital essence concentrations that do not match their species. Creatures near what he categorises as "type A" anomalies produce Mercury (Transmutation) essence. Creatures near "type B" produce Salt (Protection) essence. He has written this in his private notes and stared at it for several weeks. He has not published it.

*What Ferren offers crafters:* The only proper Natural Philosophy laboratory in Reedford — distillation equipment, essence storage, comparative reference collection, and a practitioner who will work alongside a crafter rather than above them. He is interested in what they know as much as what he can teach, because he has reached the point where he is finding things he cannot explain and explanation is what he needs. His laboratory reduces Formula Research DCs by 3 for work conducted here. His anomaly map provides Advantage on Survival checks to locate creature sources near Thread anomaly sites.

Reaching it requires navigating the relationship. Ferren does not open his door to strangers. What he listens for in a first conversation is not credentials — he does not care about credentials — but whether the person has observed something they couldn't explain and what they did with it. People who dismissed the inexplicable bore him. People who kept looking are people he wants to talk to. He can be found at the Vine Press on Weftday evenings, where he has a standing order for a specific Corvessan white that he considers adequate.

*His current crisis:* Six months ago, his samples began returning results he cannot explain within any existing theoretical framework, including the suppressed paper. He has been corresponding with a contact in Kel Varyan asking oblique questions about whether similar patterns appear elsewhere. The contact's last letter was three weeks late and uncharacteristically brief. He has written a draft response and not sent it. He is not sure what he is waiting for.

### FOOD AND GENERAL TRADE

**The Two Bakeries** *(Market Quarter, operating before the Floodtide market opens)*
**Porren's Loaves** on the square's west side has been operating for forty years under three generations of the Porren family — currently **Yida Porren**, halfling woman, 38, whose grandmother founded it. Bread, rolls, the occasional pastry for market day. Opens at fourth bell, sold out by third hour. Pattern-glyphs are baked into the top of the Loomday loaves as a matter of tradition rather than explicit devotion — Yida's grandmother started it and it's what the bread looks like.

**Harstan's** on the north lane caters to the higher end of the market — enriched breads, imported grain flour, the sweet flatbreads that Imperial merchants recognise from Kel Varyan's bakeries and pay a premium for. **Colen Harstan**, human male, 50, supplies the Scale and Anchor and the Hill Road Inn. He does not sell retail on weekdays, only to wholesale accounts. On Floodtide market day he sets up a stall and the queue is twelve people by the time the market opens.

**The Covered Market** *(Market Quarter, behind the square's south side)*
A permanent structure housing approximately twenty indoor stalls year-round, expanding to forty during peak season under canvas awnings. Produce, preserved goods, cloth, small tools, rope, candles, oil, and the full range of goods that a town of 8,500 needs daily. The market operates under Valdrek's stall-fee structure (two copper per week for a permanent indoor stall; variable for seasonal positions). The stall-holders are a cross-section of Reedford's permanent and transient commercial population, ranging from the halfling woman who has sold river-reed candles from the same spot for twenty years to the seasonal grain merchant whose position changes based on where he can negotiate the best rate this week.

The **grey market** operates within the covered market on Floodtide: goods of irregular provenance available at stalls whose operators change frequently and who have, if asked, compelling explanations for everything they're selling. The Customs House has never conducted a systematic inspection of the covered market on Floodtide. This is not an accident.

**Norrith's Chandlery** *(Quay Road)*
**Bessel Norrith**, human male, 55, has been supplying the river trade with rope, canvas, sailcloth, oil, tallow candles, oakum, and pitch for twenty-three years. His prices are fixed, his quality is consistent, and he has no interest in anything except the efficient movement of nautical supplies. He is not an information source. He is not involved in any faction politics. He is the person you go to when you need forty feet of quality rope and want to pay a fair price for it and then leave. This is a rarer commodity in Reedford than it might appear.

**3. The Mira Problem.** The young woman who left Orveth's Crossing three weeks ago and boarded *The Weighted Scale* for Kel Varyan. ## PART XIII: WHAT PLAYERS EXPERIENCE

### Arriving in Reedford

Players arriving by river dock at the Lower Quay. Customs check: names, origin, stated purpose, two copper per person. The officer on duty uses the Pattern-greeting. He taps his desk axe-emblem when he finishes each transaction — habit from childhood. He does not think about it.

**Immediate sensory environment:**
> *Reedford smells like the river and the work of moving things. Tar and fish-brine and the particular flat smell of reed-bank mud at low water. The Lower Quay is in motion — a cargo crane swinging a pallet of Corvessan wine down to the quayside, two factors in expensive coats having a fast quiet argument over a manifest, a dwarf in a work apron directing apprentices with the particular bark of someone who has been doing this since before they were born.*
>
> *The town rises from the quayside in two main streets and a dozen cross-lanes. The streets are straight where the ground permits. At the market square's north end, a grey-stone temple with a river-fountain in its forecourt and a spiral pattern worked into the paving of its approach. At the southeast corner, a building with a contract board on which a dozen notices are posted, several with the red-wax seal of a completed job and several more without.*
>
> *The Wardbreakers' board. One of the open contracts has been posted for twelve days and the fee raised once. The creature it describes is something you don't have a name for. Crossing the bridge at the foot of the Quay Road, the man ahead of you touches the worn stone axe in the shrine niche without slowing down, without thought, the way you might step over a threshold.*

### What Players Can Observe Without Prompting

- **The contract board:** mix of completed and open contracts, including one anomalous entry that doesn't fit standard threat categories
- **Inquisitor Cors** crossing the market square in perfectly maintained Fraywatcher robes, noting every face with the attention of someone building a catalogue
- **Joss** at the Upper Quay, loading his pilot skiff, cargo without a visible manifest; he gives the party the river-pilot's professional nod
- **Sethos** at the sanctum fountain, performing the morning water-rite, pausing slightly longer than the ritual requires, his expression that of a man listening for something
- **Renn Ashford** exiting the guildhall with a junior Wardbreaker, pointing at a map; she pauses mid-conversation to watch the party come up the quay road
- **The Scale and Anchor's notice board:** rooms available; a Floodtide market location change; a hand-written message to someone named Perik uncollected for four days; a plaque above the east fireplace with twelve names
- **A stevedore crew** working at half-pace near the third crane, not quite in a work stoppage, in the specific rhythm of people who have had a conversation this morning and reached a position

## PART XIV: OUTSIDERS IN REEDFORD

*This section covers characters from outside the Corvessan-Imperial cultural sphere — Talanthari, Drow, foreign races, and non-standard origins. It is designed to help GMs run these characters with the same social texture applied to native characters, and to give players the information they need to navigate the town's institutional pressures from the outside.*

---

### Why Reedford Works for Outsiders

The town's economic function is the answer. Reedford processes goods from everywhere. It sees Imperial merchants, Corvessan nobility, river workers of mixed race and background, seasonal traders from further afield, and the full range of people who follow commerce without being formally part of it. The permanent population has developed the specific tolerance of people who are economically dependent on variety — they assess strangers, they file the information, and they mostly do not act on it unless given specific reason.

This is fundamentally different from inland Corvessa. In Ivoryn or Cindraleth, a non-standard character would attract sustained institutional attention. In Reedford, they attract a first-assessment look and then get filed as "unusual, monitor" unless they escalate it. The grey economy reinforces this: the Upper Quay, Bekk's Shed, the Tallow Cup, Joss's network all actively prefer not to examine people's backgrounds too closely, because scrutinising origins is bad for business.

**Inquisitor Cors is the exception.** He scrutinises everyone. But even Cors has an operational hierarchy — he is building cases against Joss and the grey trade, and a foreign character not visibly connected to those targets is background noise, initially. The word *initially* is doing significant work in that sentence.

**The town's three layers of tolerance:**
- **The official layer** (Customs House, Faith Guard, Valdrek's administration): has categories, uses them, will act when something falls outside them
- **The commercial layer** (factors, pilots, merchants): cares about function and payment, not origins, up to the point where origins create legal exposure
- **The informal layer** (Reed Quarter, Bekk's Shed, Maret's network, Joss's operation): actively cultivates the blindness that makes it functional; this is where outsiders find their first footing

---

### Talanthari Characters

This is the most politically charged origin and the most narratively rich.

**The core tension:** Corvessa has fought six crusades against Talanthar. The Prophecy of the Felled Root is live doctrine. A character who is identifiably from the Greenwood is, by Corvessan Faith standards, from the enemy — the Patternless kingdom whose World Tree is the prophesied target of the faithful's ultimate charge. Cors would consider a Talanthari character not merely a person of interest but a walking doctrinal violation. The twelve names on the plaque above the Scale and Anchor's east fireplace were men who rode north against Talanthar. The veterans of the 472 Thornward campaign who came back wrong are begging near the Lower Quay. This history is not abstract.

**Why Reedford specifically works:** The ghost-road trade means Talanthari-adjacent people have been passing through Reedford's informal economy for generations. Not openly — but they have been present. The town has an institutional muscle memory of managing Talanthari presence without acknowledging it. Hevra Plum knows how to not see someone. Joss knows how to move someone through without documentation. Maret's shrine is the natural first contact — pre-Faith halfling spiritual tradition has significant cultural overlap with Greenwood practice, and she would recognise and protect a Talanthari character with the same instinct she protects everything else the Faith would prefer to eliminate.

**The social mechanics:**

A Talanthari character in Reedford is managing two simultaneous pressures. Cors is the acute threat — a Fraywatcher encountering a provably Talanthari character in a Corvessan town opens a case, not a file entry. The ambient culture is the chronic pressure — the Pattern-greeting, the martyrology, the Thornward veterans, the Faith Guard garrison that performs nightly vigils in the name of the men who rode into the Greenwood and didn't come back. Navigating this requires either concealment, extraordinary social management, or the specific protection of someone the town trusts.

**The unique advantage:** A Talanthari character knows things nobody else in Reedford knows. They know what the Greenwood actually is from the inside — not the Faith's framing of it as chaos and corruption, but the reality of the Verdant Covenant, the World Tree Vaelthir, the Thornward from the other side of the boundary. When Renn Ashford is tracking anomalies in the north margin and Mireth Ashfall is reading the landscape in ways she doesn't acknowledge verbally, a Talanthari character can name what Mireth is doing. They can look at Ferren Yolt's anomaly map and recognise patterns that his Imperial academic framework doesn't have language for. They are the most informed character in Reedford about the campaign's central material, operating in the most hostile cultural environment for their presence. That tension is productive rather than prohibitive.

**Specific backgrounds that work:**

*Ranger or Scout:* The Greenwood has genuine institutional interest in what's happening to the Thornward's corrupted ground. The anomalies bleeding out of the north margin are also affecting Talanthar's edge. A character sent by a ranger-captain or the High Council to investigate the escalation has a clear operational reason to be in Reedford — it's where the evidence concentrates.

*Ghost-Road Trader:* Already operating in Joss's network, already known to Maret, already protected by the Upper Quay's pragmatic blindness. Their immediate challenge is that Joss's situation is deteriorating and their cover depends on his continued operation — which gives them an immediate stake in the Cors problem independent of any other involvement.

*Mixed Talanthari Heritage:* A character with Greenwood upbringing but human or halfling parentage is the most socially navigable version. They know what they are. Nobody else needs to, unless they tell them or something forces the question.

---

### Drow Characters

The most complex origin after Talanthari, and connected directly to the macro campaign's Arc 2C.

**The institutional position:** Drow in Corvessa are read through Faith doctrine as something adjacent to Talanthari — both are "Patternless" in the Codex's framing, though for different theological reasons. The practical result is similar: high Cors risk, best approached through the informal layer. Maret's network and Bekk's Shed are the natural entry points.

**What makes this specifically rich:** Arc 2C's themes — identity manipulation, what it means to have been made into something you didn't choose, the question of what remains of the self after the Compact of Shadows — resonate directly with Reedford's own themes of institutional systems shaping people regardless of consent. A Drow character encountering Cors is encountering someone who believes their entire people are a corruption of the Pattern. That is not background texture. That is the campaign in miniature. A Drow character who encounters Maret — whose pre-Faith halfling tradition predates the institutional religion that classified the Drow as Patternless — is encountering someone who may be the first person in their experience to engage with them outside that framework.

**Concealment versus disclosure:** A Drow character who can plausibly pass in the town's cosmopolitan environment (hood up, minimal surface time, operations through the grey economy) faces a different set of choices than one who is openly present. Both are viable. Both create different pressure patterns against the Cors Awareness tracker.

---

### Mirror Chain and Selveran Background

Structurally straightforward — Selveran is an autonomous Loom-City within the Empire. A Mirror Chain character is an Imperial subject, technically, however great the cultural distance. They have paperwork. The Customs House has categories. Belessi has dealt with Mirror Chain traders before and finds them professionally interesting.

**What makes this interesting:** A character with Water Thread Loom-City background — Selveran or Aeravae — arrives in a town where the Water Thread is going wrong. Sethos's eighteen pages of blessing-water documentation would read differently to someone trained in Water Thread Loom-City tradition than it does to a Corvessan priest whose Thread education is primarily martial doctrine. They may recognise what's happening before anyone else does, and they carry the specific authority of someone from a place where the Thread is the central fact of civic life rather than one element of a faith framework built around crusading.

---

### Zhen-Lan and Myrrhaleon Background

Effectively Imperial subjects, similar social mechanics to Mirror Chain. The cultural difference is visible — Zhen-Lan has a strongly distinct aesthetic and philosophical tradition — but the paperwork exists and the Customs House has categories. Lower institutional friction, interesting cultural contrast with Corvessan norms particularly around the Faith's geometric architecture and the Pattern-greeting.

---

### Dragonborn Characters

Dragonborn in Reedford are read through the lens of the Faith's relationship with Soltharion, which at this distance from Veltharyn is more complex than in the interior. A dragonborn character who has experienced the wrong-prayer phenomenon — praying into the wrong depth, feeling answered by something older that loves dragonborn with an intensity that makes scales tingle — is carrying exactly the information that Sethos would find valuable and that Cors would find theologically destabilising. Sister Vyss Amendra, who will board *The Weighted Scale* in several sessions, experienced this and has been quietly managing it for weeks. A dragonborn character who has experienced the same thing and who is prepared to discuss it with a Water Thread priest will find Sethos an unexpectedly open audience.

The Arc 3B connection — Soltharion's manufactured divinity and the Blood Binding — means a dragonborn character has a personal stake in the campaign's theological questions that no other race shares in the same way. The question of what their god actually is, and whether their prayers are going where they think they're going, is not abstract for them.

---

### Vashkoran Background

Geographically exotic enough to attract attention; distant enough that nobody in Reedford has formed strong opinions. They register in the "foreign trader" category that the town's commercial tolerance accommodates. The Faith's assessment of their Thread practice — which doesn't map onto the Bound Pattern framework cleanly — is eventually a Cors concern, but it's lower priority than his current cases and requires him to make theological arguments he hasn't been asked to make yet.

**Specific advantage:** Vashkora's direct experience with Earth Thread traditions and the specific creature ecology of its border regions gives a Vashkoran character field knowledge about creature behaviour and harvesting that doesn't appear in the Wardbreaker library's four-generation Corvessan-regional dataset. Renn Ashford values accurate information about things her library doesn't cover. A Vashkoran character who demonstrates this knowledge on a contract earns her professional respect faster than almost any other route.

---

### Race-Specific Notes

**Non-Talanthari Elves:** Tolerated in the mercantile environment, associated in local minds with Talanthar regardless of actual origin. A character who makes their non-Talanthari origin clear early — and who demonstrably lacks the ghost-road knowledge, the Greenwood-adjacent spiritual practice, the specific bearing — is assessed as unusual but not threatening. An elf who has any of those things gets Talanthari assumptions regardless of what they say.

**Tieflings:** The Imperial bureaucracy has categories. The Faith's position on tieflings is theologically ambiguous — the Codex has passages that cut both ways, and Sethos has made a pragmatic decision that the ambiguity favours tolerance. Cors has not yet been asked to form a formal position. He will, if given specific reason. Until then, a tiefling in the commercial environment is exotic rather than threatening.

**Half-Orcs and Orcs:** The border region has orc presence through the fire-orc communities who function as buffer between Corvessa and the undead kingdoms. A character from that tradition occupies an interesting intermediate position — neither cleanly inside the Faith's community nor clearly outside it, with a legitimate claim to military border service that the Faith Guard recognises. Renn Ashford has worked with orc Wardbreaker equivalents before. Her assessment is professional.

**Halflings:** Not outsiders in any meaningful sense — halflings are deeply embedded in Reedford's social fabric at every level. The Reed Quarter, the pilots' informal network, Hevra Plum, Maret, Pol and Senna Greenbank, Tem Arvish at the Register. A halfling character from outside Corvessa is read as a traveller from an unusual branch of a familiar people. The Faith's position on halflings is accommodating — the Severed Chain philosophy is unregulated and tolerated as cultural tradition. The most significant complication is that Maret's non-standard Pattern-greeting response has flagged halfling spiritual practice as a Cors concern, and a halfling character with visible non-standard practice may find that flag attaches to them by association.

---

### The Group Composition Question

Mixed-origin parties work particularly well in Reedford because the town is designed around the management of difference rather than its elimination. A party including a Talanthari character, an Imperial-background character, and a Corvessan-raised character is a party whose internal dynamic mirrors the town's external tensions — and where what each character knows, believes, and is afraid of diverges in ways that generate roleplay rather than consensus.

The one thing to establish in Session Zero is how the party navigates a high-risk origin character's exposure. Not resolved — just agreed on as a present tension. Does the whole party know? Does the party actively cover for them? Is it a secret kept from some party members? The answer shapes Act One's social dynamics more than any single plot hook.

**The Cors Awareness tracker applies to the party collectively as well as individually.** Actions that generate awareness for one character can generate awareness for their known associates. A Talanthari character who is careful about their own exposure but who travels visibly with a character whose activities are already at Awareness 3 is not protected by their own caution. The town notices associations. So does Cors.

## PART XV: CRAFTING, DOWNTIME, AND CONSEQUENCE

> *"The Guild of Chemistry would like to sell you a healing potion. Brother Dalen would like to help you. Maret would like you to sit down and tell her what's actually wrong. All three of these things are available in Reedford. They cost different things."*
> *— Overheard at the Tallow Cup*

### The Design Principle

Crafting downtime in Reedford should not produce items that appear in a character's inventory without producing something else first: a relationship deepened, an institutional position changed, a piece of intelligence that connects to the larger situation. The mechanical outcome of a downtime activity is the least interesting part of it. The roleplay outcome is why it exists.

Every downtime track listed below has a named NPC consequence, a named institutional consequence, and a designed discovery moment — a point at which the activity reveals something about Reedford's situation that the character would not have encountered through adventuring alone.

---

### DOWNTIME TRACK: Supply Run with Joss

**What it is:** Accompanying Joss on the reed-bank portion of a ghost-road run — not the full Talanthari transit, which he does alone, but the Ivora margin where the harvesting happens before he loads and departs.

**Entry:** Joss is accessible through Maret or through the Mudlark. The initial contact requires a conversation in which he takes the character's measure. He is not suspicious; he is careful. He is particularly interested in whether a character has noticed something unusual on the river and what they did about it.

**What happens:**
The run departs on Stillwater evening, when Customs House staff observe reduced hours. Reed-bank harvest takes two to three hours in darkness, navigating channels Joss knows. Mireth Ashfall is the most likely Wardbreaker patrol presence, and she has made a professional decision about Joss's operations.

- *Survival DC 13:* 1d4 Talanthari herbs at high grade
- *Survival DC 18:* 2d4 herbs, one with unusual essence concentration — reduces crafting time by 50% on the appropriate formula
- *Failure:* Joss aborts the run. He loses the trip. He does not blame the character but recalibrates trust accordingly.

**The Cors dimension:** Day 6 from campaign start, Cors has a watcher on the Upper Quay specifically positioned to document Joss's departure. A character on a supply run that night is present when the watch is closest. The character's choices — avoid the watcher, provide cover, abort and warn Joss, do nothing — are all legitimate and all have downstream effects on Joss's situation and the Cors Awareness tracker.

**What it reveals:** By the third run, Joss talks about the ghost-roads not as smuggling routes but as something older — paths his great-grandmother's accounts describe, older than the Corvessan Faith's formal prohibition. He is not making a political argument. He is telling the character something about himself that he tells almost no one, because the night river encourages it and because he has decided they are worth telling. By the fifth run, if the Cors situation has not been resolved, Joss asks for help. He does not specify what kind. He describes the situation clearly and waits.

---

### DOWNTIME TRACK: Workshop Time at the Ashwright Forge

**What it is:** Arranged workspace at Kael and Sonna's specialist forge — access to equipment, materials, and two of the most technically accomplished smiths in the region.

**Entry:** An initial commission that demonstrates competence. Sonna evaluates this by giving the character actual work that needs doing and watching them do it. Access is on Rootday mornings.

**What it provides:**
- Smith's Tools at expert quality, Leatherworker's Tools
- Materials at medium grade from official supply; occasionally higher grade from Kael's salvage network
- Sonna's presence during work — she will intervene when she sees something going wrong, which counts as her Proficiency Bonus substituting for the character's on one check per session

**The documentation lesson:** Sonna explains, on the second or third session, how her commission documentation works — not as instruction but as context for dealing with one of the unregistered Wardbreaker commissions. The explanation is delivered as craft talk during the examination of a specification. A character with Alchemy proficiency or Arcana may recognise that the commission is not from Ashford.

**The Faith Guard presence:** A Faith Guard officer comes through on Threadday mornings to collect commissioned weapons for blessing delivery. He does not inspect the forge; he collects a specific documented list. A character whose commission is on the bench needs it appropriately categorised. Sonna will advise if asked and will cover for it once, without comment, if she judges the exposure manageable. She will not cover for it twice.

**What it reveals:** Sonna, by the fourth session, tells the character about a commission she regrets — a weapon she made well, correctly documented, that she knows was used wrong. She has told this to Kael and no one else. She is telling the character because they have been in her forge long enough that she has formed an opinion about them, and the opinion is that they would have done differently. She wants to know if she's right.

---

### DOWNTIME TRACK: Research with Ferren Yolt

**What it is:** Working access to Ferren's laboratory — distillation equipment, essence storage, his research library, and his attention.

**Entry:** A first conversation, found at the Vine Press on Weftday evenings, that establishes the character as someone worth his time. He listens for whether the person has observed something they couldn't explain and what they did about it. People who kept looking are people he wants to talk to. Laboratory access is relational — the character learns his rhythms and works within them. The ground-floor door being unbarred signals availability.

**What it provides:**
- Natural Philosophy workspace — reduces Formula Research DCs by 3 for work conducted here
- Ferren's anomaly map — Advantage on Survival checks to locate creature sources near Thread anomaly sites
- Ferren's essence stockpile (Air and Water essences from standard sources) available in exchange for anomaly-adjacent creature identification data he cannot gather alone

**What it reveals:** At the third session, Ferren shares the suppressed Archive paper — not as a significant gesture but as a practical tool, because the character needs its theoretical framework for what comes next. The paper describes Thread-lattice stress fracture theoretically. Ferren's map shows it occurring practically. The discrepancy between what the paper predicts and what the map documents is what he cannot explain. He will say, at some point in this session, that the anomalies are accelerating faster than the model predicts. He will say it in a tone that suggests he has been saying it to himself for some time and that saying it aloud to another person is something he has needed to do. Then he returns to the work.

By the fifth session, if the character has been bringing anomaly-adjacent creature essence data, Ferren shows them the unsent draft letter to his Kel Varyan contact. He asks whether they think he should send it. This is not rhetorical. He wants their opinion. The letter describes Thread-lattice stress fracture occurring in the Ivora corridor and asks whether similar patterns have been observed elsewhere. He is aware that sending it commits him to a public position he has not yet taken. The character's opinion matters to him in a way he has not verbally acknowledged.

---

### DOWNTIME TRACK: Reed Quarter Practice

**What it is:** Providing low-cost treatments and medicinal preparations to the Reed Quarter's working population — stevedores, river workers, seasonal labour — in collaboration with Maret.

**Entry:** An introduction through Hevra Plum at the Tallow Cup, or through Daven Thatch, who sends injured stevedores to Maret when Dalen's prices are prohibitive. The first meeting with Maret involves her assessing, with the attention of someone who has spent sixty-four years reading people, whether the character is interested in the work or interested in looking like they are interested in the work. These produce different outcomes.

**What happens:**
Maret guides foraging in the civilian-accessible reed-bank margin on Loomday mornings. Survival DC 10 with her guidance yields 1d6 local herbs at medium grade. Treatments in the courtyard beside the Spring Shrine or at the Tallow Cup's back room when Hevra permits. Medicine DC 10 earns two silver per successful treatment and the more significant currency of demonstrated reliability.

**What it reveals:** The stevedores talk. They talk about what they see on the quay at loading hour, what moves through the warehouses on Stillwater night, what the crane operators have noticed about cargo weights that don't match the manifests, and what three of them saw near the north reed-channel four weeks ago that they reported to no one because they didn't have language for it. This is intelligence that neither the Wardbreakers nor Cors has obtained, because neither institution has access to the specific trust that comes from sitting in a courtyard treating a rope-burn on a person who earns four copper a day.

By the third session, Hevra Plum asks the character to hold the Stevedores' Association emergency fund — a locked box — for three days while she handles something she cannot detail. She does not explain. She hands them the box, gives them Daven Thatch's name for emergency claims, and goes. This is not a test. It is what happens when someone has become part of the informal economy's trust network. The character has been trusted with approximately thirty-seven gold pieces in small coin. What they do with it is character, and Hevra will hear about it either way.

By the fourth session, Maret asks the character to sit with a patient she cannot help further — a stevedore who has recovered from a physical injury and who will not recover from something else. Not to treat. To sit. She wants to see whether they understand that sitting is the work.

### DOWNTIME TRACK: Wardbreaker Contracts

**What it is:** Field contract work under Renn Ashford's operational umbrella — creature encounters in the margin zones, threat identification for the anomaly sites.

**Entry:** Demonstrated usefulness to Ashford. The first contract is a Category One encounter — Greenwood bleed creature, standard threat profile, manageable. Ashford watches the outcome and the conduct. She is interested in competence and in whether the character reports the encounter accurately, including unusual details a less careful observer might omit. She does not say this is a test. It is how she evaluates everyone.

**What it provides:**
- Contract pay: eight to fifteen gold per contract
- Post-combat harvesting opportunity: Survival DC 12 before the creature degrades. Ashford takes operational priority on components; remainder available to the character
- By the third contract, Mireth Ashfall is the patrol assignment — her tracking reveals which plants grow where, which creatures use which channels, and where the margin between Corvessan and not-Corvessan territory actually runs, which differs from any official map

**What it reveals:** Brek and Torvyn Staak's underground threat briefings contain, for a character listening carefully, a detailed description of the subsurface water channels near the anomaly sites and what is flowing through them. Mireth tracks around anomaly sites the way a person tracks around unstable ground — not with fear, with knowledge. A character with Survival proficiency who pays attention to her work notices she is tracking Thread phenomena she doesn't acknowledge verbally.

At the fifth contract, if the character has been reporting unusual observations, Ashford shows them the private notebook — twenty-three entries. She does not explain it. She opens it to entry sixteen, the first where the language shifts from "unusual creature behaviour" to "phenomenon inconsistent with known creature behaviour," and watches to see whether the character understands the distinction.

**The harvesting breakthrough:** Creatures near the Thread anomaly sites produce vital essence concentrations that don't match their species — anomaly-type correlates with essence type. This is not available in any Guild Chemistry reference. It is not available in the Wardbreaker library. It is available to the character who is on the contracts, paying attention, and has enough Alchemy proficiency to recognise what the unusual readings mean.

### THE CORS AWARENESS TRACKER

Inquisitor Cors is building a case. A crafter operating in Reedford's grey economy will eventually appear in his peripheral vision, and what they do when that happens defines their relationship with the town's institutional power structure.

**Cors is not the enemy.** He is a man with a job, doing it with genuine conviction and real skill, in a situation where his framework is increasingly inadequate to the facts he is encountering. He is not incompetent. He is constrained by process and by the complexity of a situation his doctrinal framework was not designed to address.

A crafter visible to Cors has options that are not flight and not confrontation. Sethos can manage Cors — as he has managed him on every other sensitive matter — in exchange for the character giving him something useful, which means information about the Thread anomalies he is assembling from eighteen pages of river documentation he has not shared with anyone. Valdrek can provide institutional cover in exchange for the character solving one of his practical problems, of which he has several, including the creature from Mollen's farm. The Wardbreaker charter provides operational cover that Ashford can extend, within limits she has established in her own accounting.

At **3 Awareness**, Cors makes a formal inquiry — a direct question, recorded. At **5**, he files preliminary findings to Cindraleth. At **7**, Cindraleth assigns a reviewing inquisitor. The escalation timeline is slow enough that a crafter managing their institutional relationships has time to address it. It is not slow enough to ignore.
