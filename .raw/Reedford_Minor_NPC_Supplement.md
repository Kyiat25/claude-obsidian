# REEDFORD — MINOR NPCS AND FLAVOUR CHARACTERS
## *The People You Pass Every Day*

> **"A town isn't its important people. It's the woman who sells eel pies at the same corner for thirty years and whose name everyone knows and nobody uses."**
> *— Overheard at the Tallow Cup, attributed to nobody in particular*

---

*These are the people players encounter between the plot-relevant conversations. They are not hooks. They are not information sources with useful secrets. They are the texture of a real place — the characters who make Reedford feel like somewhere people actually live, which is the precondition for players caring what happens to it.*

*Each entry has a name, a brief physical description, a default location and time, a characteristic habit or phrase, and one unexpected detail. Many have a small tension or want. None require the GM to track them across sessions unless they become interesting to the players.*

## THE LOWER QUAY

**Taddo Marsh** *(halfling boy, 12)*
Yenna Marsh's nephew, though he'd say cousin if you asked because the exact relationship is complicated in the way that large halfling families are complicated and he has stopped trying to explain it. He does quay maintenance — sweeping, coiling rope left out, fetching tools — for two copper a week from the quay supervisor, which he considers both inadequate and worth it because the Lower Quay at 4am is the most interesting place in the world and being paid to be there is better than not being paid to be there.

He draws things he sees. On pavements in chalk, on scraps of parchment, on the back of whatever he has to hand. He drew the Crimson Veil vessel's hull marking because he thought it was interesting. He has also drawn three different factors having arguments they thought nobody was watching, a river pilot crying while loading cargo on a Stillwater morning, and a very accurate sketch of the north margin reed-bank at low water that Ferren Yolt would find immediately significant if he ever saw it, which he hasn't.

*Characteristic habit:* Stares at anything visually unusual for slightly too long, then looks away quickly when he realises he's been noticed. He's been noticed every time. He doesn't get better at hiding it.

*Unexpected detail:* He can't read. He has been conducting a lengthy private negotiation with Brek Coburn, who can, about exchanging drawing lessons for reading lessons, without either of them using those words. They've been circling this for six weeks. Brek keeps bringing books he says he wants Taddo to look at the pictures in. Taddo keeps drawing things he says he wants Brek to label.

**Ossa** *(human woman, approximate age 70, Lower Quay entrance)*
One of the sixty permanent beggars in Reedford, specifically the halfling woman's neighbour at the Lower Quay entrance — though Ossa is not halfling, she is tall and thin and somewhere between sixty-five and eighty, and she arrived in Reedford approximately fourteen years ago from a direction she has never specified.

She sells information rather than asking for money. Not intelligence — common knowledge, the kind of thing that anyone paying attention already knows. She charges two copper for "what you need to know before you go in there," where "there" is any establishment in the Lower Quay district. Most people who've been in Reedford more than a week know everything she tells them. New arrivals often find it worth the two copper. She has a perfect record — she has never told anyone anything false, which she considers professional integrity and which most people consider the minimum requirement of the transaction, and which is the reason she has been doing this for fourteen years without being moved on.

*Characteristic habit:* Arranges her possessions with compulsive precision before beginning any conversation — small bundle on the left, walking stick across the front, whatever she's eating set aside. She does this even for two-sentence interactions. She does it in the rain.

*Unexpected detail:* She was, before whatever happened fourteen years ago, a freight factor. A good one. She has not touched a manifest since she arrived in Reedford and she does not intend to. If anyone directly identifies what she used to do, she will not deny it and will not explain it, and the conversation will end with a finality that makes further questions feel rude.

**Kessen and Barra** *(halfling twins, 19, Lower Quay crane operators)*
The only people in Reedford who operate the cranes, which are expensive, irreplaceable, and require a specific certification that the Pilots' Register handles as a sideline because nobody else wanted the administrative responsibility. Kessen operates crane one and two; Barra operates crane three, the one currently at the centre of the mild work action. They inherited this from their father, who inherited it from his father, and the Kessen-Barra family has been operating the Reedford cranes since Year 441 FA, specifically because the family patriarch helped rebuild them after the flood and was given the operating rights as part of the reconstruction arrangement.

They finish each other's sentences, work in synchronised silence across forty metres of quay, and have a running dispute about which crane is better that has been going on since they were eight years old and shows no sign of resolution. Kessen maintains that crane one has better sight-lines. Barra maintains that crane three has better mechanical advantage. They are both right. The dispute continues.

*Characteristic habit:* Nod at each other from across the quay at the start and end of each shift in a specific sequence — one nod each, then a second nod from whoever started the shift last — that is apparently a greeting, a status check, and a shift handover compressed into three gestures. Nobody else knows what it means. They've been doing it since they were fourteen.

*Unexpected detail:* Barra has been writing a history of the Reedford cranes for three years. It is currently forty-eight pages long, covers the reconstruction of Year 441 FA in meticulous detail, and is so far the most comprehensive account of the flood's effect on quay infrastructure available anywhere. She has shown it to no one. She is not sure it's interesting to anyone who isn't her.

**Moren** *(human man, 45, Lower Quay supervisor)*
The man who assigns the stevedore shifts, manages the day's loading schedule, shouts at people who are doing things incorrectly, and knows to the minute how long every cargo movement on the Lower Quay takes. He has been doing this job for fifteen years. He is extremely good at it. He is also, by his own quiet assessment, the most poorly compensated person in the quay's operational structure relative to what the quay would cost if he stopped doing it correctly.

He has made this assessment twice formally — once in writing to Valdrek's counting house, once verbally to Cassin Valdrek in a meeting Valdrek clearly did not enjoy — and received incremental rate adjustments both times that he considers insufficient and that he accepts because he has a daughter at the Hill Road school and a mother at Sera Coburn's boarding house and walking away from the income would require a plan he doesn't currently have.

*Characteristic habit:* Counts under his breath when watching cargo being loaded. Not the cargo — the workers. He is running continuous efficiency calculations that he never writes down and never acts on because acting on them would require resources he doesn't have.

*Unexpected detail:* He is teaching himself to read Talanthari script. Not for any purpose he can name. A manifest came through three years ago with Talanthari markings alongside the Corvessan text, and he found that not being able to read part of a document bothered him in a way that was disproportionate to the practical inconvenience. He has obtained, through means he'd prefer not to specify, a Talanthari-Corvessan commercial glossary that was printed before the Faith formally prohibited Talanthari trade. He practices on Loomday afternoons. He is on his second pass through the glossary. He can now read approximately forty words. He is not sure what he'll do when he can read more.

## THE MARKET SQUARE AND BROKER'S ROW

**Pella Orvish** *(halfling woman, 55, fixed market stall — river-reed candles)*
Has occupied the same stall position in the covered market for twenty years. Makes her candles from a specific reed-grass that grows in the southern margin and that produces, when burned, a smell that everyone in Reedford has been smelling since childhood and associates with indoor warmth. She sells more candles in winter than she could theoretically produce, which she manages by having started the winter's production in late summer and by keeping a reserve she doesn't tell anyone about.

She is extremely opinionated about candle quality and will tell you, unsolicited, what's wrong with any other candle you've recently purchased. This is not sales technique. She genuinely finds substandard candles offensive.

*Characteristic habit:* Calls everyone "friend" — not warmly, just neutrally, as a default pronoun. *"What can I do for you, friend?"* — *"That'll be three copper, friend."* She has been doing this for so long that she no longer notices it. People who are new to Reedford sometimes think she is being friendly. People who've been there a while know she calls Cors "friend" in exactly the same tone she calls everyone else.

*Unexpected detail:* She has been in a long-running dispute with the stall to her left — river-grass rope goods, operated by a human man named Tars — about whether his display extends twelve centimetres into what she considers her stall territory. This dispute has been running for eleven years. It has never escalated beyond passive repositioning of display items and occasional pointed sighs. Neither party has brought it to market administration because both know that market administration would simply split the difference, which neither of them considers acceptable.

---

**Tars** *(human man, 48, fixed market stall — rope goods and small hardware)*
Pella Orvish's nemesis. Not a man with enemies — he is broadly liked, a competent maker of rope goods, a reliable presence at the market, and a patient explainer of knots to people who don't know which one they need. He has exactly one ongoing conflict in his life and it is about twelve centimetres of market stall territory that he would, if he is honest, probably give up without much pain. He continues the dispute because giving it up now, after eleven years, would feel like losing, and he is not a man who loses.

*Characteristic habit:* Ties knots while talking. Not nervously — fluidly, automatically, the way some people fidget with a coin. He'll pick up whatever rope is to hand and produce a perfectly serviceable sheep-shank or bowline mid-sentence and then set it down without acknowledging he's done it.

*Unexpected detail:* His daughter, Maret Tars, 16, named before the current Maret of the Spring Shrine became locally notable, has a complicated relationship with her name. She went through a period of insisting on "Etta." She has returned to Maret. The local Maret knows about this and has a private affection for the girl she has never expressed because she knows it would make the name situation more complicated rather than less.

**Aldric the Assessor** *(human man, 62, Freight Factors' Exchange, occasional market square presence)*
Not technically part of the Exchange — he is a freelance cargo assessor, hired by factors who need an independent valuation before a large deal. He has been doing this for thirty years and has developed, over those thirty years, a reputation for accuracy that is slightly mythological. He is said to be able to tell the grade of Corvessan wine within two years by smell without tasting it. He can, but only for vintages from three specific estates, and he has never corrected the exaggeration because it is good for business.

He eats his midday meal at the same table in the market square every day — rain, cold, peak season crowds — and reads while he eats. He considers lunchtime conversations an interruption unless you have something specific and interesting to say, in which case he will close his book, which is as clear a signal as he gives that you have his attention.

*Characteristic habit:* Adjusts his spectacles before looking at anything he's being paid to assess. He doesn't need to adjust them — they fit fine — but he started doing it at twenty-three as a deliberate signal that he was switching into professional mode, and now does it automatically even when assessing things he's not being paid for, like the quality of market produce or the structural integrity of quayside buildings.

*Unexpected detail:* He is almost completely deaf in his left ear and has been for twenty years, the result of standing too close to a warehouse collapse in Year 467 FA. He compensates instinctively — always positions himself to hear from the right — and most people who've worked with him for years don't know. His assessments are entirely tactile and visual, which means the deafness is irrelevant to his work. He has decided it is also irrelevant to most conversations.

**Wren** *(gender-ambiguous human, approximately 30, runs a cloth stall three days a week)*
Seasonal presence — arrives at Reedford's market for the Floodtide season, stays through Harvest's Pattern, leaves before winter. Has been doing this for six years. Nobody knows where they go in winter or where they come from in spring. They sell cloth of unusual quality at prices that are higher than mass-market and lower than premium, which is a specific niche that serves people who want good cloth without factor prices. They are unfailingly pleasant and completely opaque about every personal detail.

*Characteristic habit:* Knows every returning customer's name, what they bought last time, and what they said they were making with it. Asks after the project at the start of every subsequent interaction. Has never been wrong about a name or a project in six years of returning customers.

*Unexpected detail:* They are the person Corvan Pell's mystery long-term guest is in contact with. Not the guest itself — the intermediary. The monthly payments for the Watcher's Rest mystery room pass through Wren's cloth business as a standing order from a Kel Varyan textile supplier. Wren knows the room is being used for something they haven't been told about, has decided this is not their concern, and has been facilitating the arrangement with the specific neutrality of someone who has made many practical decisions not to know things.

**Brother Hallen** *(human man, 38, junior sanctum cleric)*
Sethos's one full-time assistant at the sanctum. Responsible for the morning water-rite when Sethos is occupied, the maintenance of the sanctum's administrative records, the preparation of Sethos's regrettable Loomday cooking, and a pastoral caseload of approximately forty regular congregation members who come to him rather than Sethos because Sethos is slightly intimidating and Hallen is reassuringly ordinary.

He is, by his own quiet assessment, a mediocre priest. He gives perfectly adequate pastoral care. He performs the rites correctly. He believes what he's supposed to believe. He has never experienced anything he would describe as a direct experience of the Pattern. He has been waiting for this experience since he was seventeen and entered the novitiate. He is beginning, at thirty-eight, to wonder whether waiting is the correct approach.

*Characteristic habit:* Apologises preemptively. *"Sorry, I may have this wrong, but—"* / *"Forgive me if this isn't useful—"* He does it so automatically that people who know him have stopped hearing it. New visitors to the sanctum sometimes find it disconcerting, as though he expects to have already failed at whatever he's about to do.

*Unexpected detail:* He is the only person who has tasted Sethos's Loomday cooking and told him, directly, that it was excellent. He did this once, twelve years ago, as a new acolyte, because he panicked and couldn't think of anything else to say. He has been maintaining this position for twelve years. He considers it the greatest sustained act of will in his life. He is not sure it's the right thing to do. He is very sure it is too late to change course.

## THE UPPER QUAY AND PILOTS

**Daen** *(human woman, 51, licensed upper-river pilot)*
The second-most experienced active pilot in Reedford after Joss, and the one most frequently hired when Joss isn't available. She has been piloting the upper river for twenty-four years and knows the Ivora's upper channels with a specificity that occasionally disagrees with Joss's assessment of conditions, which she considers healthy and he considers mildly irritating.

She has three children, all in their twenties, none of them pilots. She had opinions about this when they were young. She has fewer opinions about it now. She had a significant and public dispute with Joss in Year 483 over right-of-passage on a contested channel that went to the Pilots' Register for arbitration — Tem Arvish's decision, which she considers biased and which Joss considers correct. They have worked in professional cooperation since then with the specific politeness of people who have agreed to set something aside without having resolved it.

*Characteristic habit:* Tests the wind every morning before boarding — holds up a wet finger, tilts her head, sometimes closes her eyes. She does this on the quay, in the middle of conversation, stopping whatever she's saying to do it and resuming without comment. It takes about four seconds. People who work with her regularly have learned to wait.

*Unexpected detail:* She has been documenting changes in the upper river's behaviour for eight years in a logbook that she has not shared with Ashford or Yolt because she does not know what the changes mean and she will not present something she doesn't understand. Her logbook and Ashford's notebook and Yolt's anomaly map cover the same phenomena from three different observational positions and would, if compared, produce a picture significantly more complete than any one of them holds. This has not happened yet.

**Brenn Oaklow** *(human man, 34, independent cargo handler, Upper Quay)*
Not a stevedore — he's an independent, which means he takes freelance loading and transport work that falls between the stevedore union rates and the licensed factor system. He has a flat-bed cart, a very strong back, and a network of irregular cargo customers who need things moved without going through the official weighing stations. He is not precisely grey trade — he doesn't move illegal goods, he checks — but he occupies the informal economy's middle layer.

He is also, to the knowledge of approximately forty people in Reedford, the most reliable person to go to if you need to find out whether a specific cargo arrived in Reedford in the last three months and whether it was logged at the official station or moved another way. This is not a service he advertises. He answers questions honestly when asked by people he trusts, on the grounds that the information costs him nothing and that keeping it when people need it seems worse than sharing it.

*Characteristic habit:* Assesses the weight of anything he picks up before he picks it up — runs his eye over it, sometimes lifts one edge an inch and sets it back down. He is accurate to within about five percent by eye. He does this with furniture, with market produce, with luggage. It is entirely unconscious.

*Unexpected detail:* His grandmother is Old Vass's sister. They share the specific family resemblance of people who are clearly related and never mention it. Old Vass doesn't mention it because Brenn's choices about which cargo he handles are his own business. Brenn doesn't mention it because he has never worked out whether Old Vass approves of him or not and has decided not to find out.

**Feran the Rope-Man** *(halfling man, 66, informal Upper Quay fixture)*
Technically retired. Functionally present at the Upper Quay every morning for approximately four hours, during which time he inspects the mooring lines of any vessel he considers inadequately secured, makes adjustments without asking, and leaves without billing anyone. He has been doing this since he retired from active piloting eleven years ago. The quay supervisor has asked him to stop three times. He has ignored all three requests. The quay supervisor now works around him.

He once saved a vessel from drifting free during a night squall by spending three hours in the rain re-securing lines that the crew had tied incorrectly. He did not wake the crew to tell them. He left before dawn. The crew found their vessel in the morning correctly secured and never found out who did it. He considers this satisfactory.

*Characteristic habit:* Talks to the boats. Not at length — a short word when he approaches a vessel, a quiet acknowledgment when he leaves it, the specific muttering of someone conducting a private professional assessment. He is aware people think this is eccentric. He has been doing it for forty-five years and considers it courtesy.

*Unexpected detail:* He and Tem Arvish have been friends for forty years and have never agreed on a single subject related to the river. Not one. They have a standing breakfast at the Register on the mornings Tem works — Feran brings food from Porren's Loaves, they eat, they disagree about everything, they part on good terms. Neither can explain why this works. It does.

## THE REED QUARTER

**Pip** *(halfling girl, 9)*
Maret Coburn's neighbour and Brek Coburn's occasional companion in whatever he's currently investigating. Pip is nine and has the specific confidence of a youngest child who has grown up surrounded by adults who find her amusing — she will approach strangers without preamble, ask direct questions, and interpret evasive answers accurately.

She is currently investigating whether the story she heard from Brek about the boat at the Lower Quay before dawn is connected to the story she heard from Taddo about the chalk drawing. She is not sure why she thinks they're connected. She is following the feeling.

*Characteristic habit:* Repeats the last word of a sentence she's processing back to herself, quietly, almost under her breath, as though testing the flavour of it. When told something surprising she'll say the surprising part once, flat, to herself, before responding to it.

*Unexpected detail:* She knows Maret — the Spring Shrine Maret — visits the river-marker stone on Stillwater mornings. She has followed her twice without being seen. Or without Maret appearing to notice. She is not entirely sure Maret didn't notice.

**Oskar** *(human man, 44, River Quarter handyman)*
Fixes things. Every building in the Reed Quarter that has something wrong with it has had Oskar through it at some point. He is competent at approximately a third of what he agrees to do, adequate at a third, and cheerfully unreliable at the remaining third, which he makes up for with a genuine willingness to keep working until the problem is resolved even when this takes significantly longer than he estimated.

He is everyone's backup plan. Nobody in the Reed Quarter calls him first. He is who you call when the person you called first can't come for three days.

*Characteristic habit:* Estimates job times by doubling his initial instinct and adding half again. He started doing this eight years ago when he noticed a pattern in his estimates. He is still consistently fifteen to twenty percent low on the adjusted estimate. He has not adjusted again. He considers the current methodology an improvement.

*Unexpected detail:* He is the person responsible for maintaining Maret's clay pipe — the one that feeds the stone basin in her shrine room with river water. He has been maintaining it for twelve years. He treats this work with more care than any of his paid jobs. He considers it the most important pipe in the Reed Quarter. He has never discussed this with Maret. She knows he does it. She has left a small amount of something on the windowsill for him every time, for twelve years. It has varied — sometimes coin, sometimes food, once a specific root that he suspects was medicinal for something he hadn't mentioned and was right about. He has never acknowledged receiving any of it.

**The Brothers Veln** *(halfling men, Davver 38 and Soth — different Soth — 35)*
Not related to the musician Soth. This Soth is a common enough halfling name that the Reed Quarter has two without anyone finding this remarkable, though the two men are aware of the potential for confusion and have developed a slightly elaborate disambiguation system that involves full names in first reference and significant eye contact in subsequent references to confirm everyone is following.

Davver and Soth Veln run a small boat repair operation out of a shed at the northern end of the Reed Quarter. They are not licensed — they don't have the equipment for significant structural repairs — but they handle the smaller work that Orven Staak's yard considers below its rate structure: patching, caulking, minor hardware, the painting and maintenance that keeps working vessels working between major repairs.

Their work is good and their prices are lower than Staak's. This has created a mild professional friction that everyone treats as settled — Orven sends clients to them when the job is too small, they send clients to Orven when the job is too large, and the friction is maintained at exactly the level that constitutes a functional working relationship.

*Characteristic habit:* Finish each other's sentences not because they're completing a thought the other has started, but because they've been having conversations their whole lives and have developed a kind of distributed sentence construction where neither of them is sure who started any given thought.

*Unexpected detail:* Davver has been quietly learning dwarf shipbuilding techniques from a text he obtained from Kael Ashwright's public lending collection — Kael maintains three shelves of technical texts available to craftspeople, a practice he started twelve years ago and that generates him approximately no money and significant goodwill. Davver has not told Soth he's been doing this because he's not sure yet whether it's useful and doesn't want to raise expectations. He has been doing it for eighteen months.

## THE HILL ROAD AND GATE

**Corporal Vesna** *(human woman, 31, Faith Guard gate corporal)*
The senior corporal at Reedford's Faith Guard garrison, which is to say she runs the day-to-day operation of the gate while her nominal superior spends most of his time at the Hill Road Inn's common room and considers this a sensible division of labour.

Vesna is efficient, fair, and constitutionally incapable of corruption. Not from unusual virtue — she simply can't make the mental move that corruption requires, the reframing of rules as guidelines when they're inconvenient. This makes her very good at the gate job and occasionally a problem for people who expected the gate to be a negotiation. She has been asked for a favour approximately forty times in six years at this posting. She has not provided one.

She is also, quietly, becoming worried. Cors's presence and the mild changes it's produced in the town's social atmosphere are registering with her as a professional development she hasn't quite categorised yet. She joined the Faith Guard to do something useful. She is starting to wonder whether what she's doing here is useful or just present.

*Characteristic habit:* Writes the gate log with complete sentences, not the shorthand the job technically requires. Every entry is grammatically correct, properly punctuated, and reads as though someone will eventually read it carefully. Nobody reads the gate log carefully. She writes it this way anyway.

*Unexpected detail:* She has been exchanging letters with her brother in Ivoryn for three years about whether she should request a transfer. He thinks she should. She's not sure. The letters have become, over three years, a detailed ongoing philosophical examination of what it means to do work that matters versus work that simply gets done, which neither of them planned but which both of them have found valuable. Her brother is a baker.

**Old Pemm** *(human man, approximate age 75, Hill Road, morning walker)*
Every morning, without exception, Pemm walks the full length of the Hill Road from the south gate to the Upper Quay and back. He has been doing this for approximately twenty years, ever since his wife told him the walk would be good for his heart and he started doing it and found she was right and continued after she died eleven years ago because stopping felt like losing the last argument. He walks slowly, with a stick, and nods to everyone he passes.

He worked the river for fifty years as a barge pilot on the lower estuary — the long hauls from Velcran toward the Interior — and retired to Reedford because his daughter is here. He finds Reedford beautiful in the specific way of someone from somewhere flat and open who has come to appreciate enclosed, busy, human-scale places in his old age.

*Characteristic habit:* Says *"morning"* to every person he passes on the walk. Every single one. Rain, pre-dawn, busy market day with a hundred people on the Hill Road. He has said "morning" to Inquisitor Cors approximately forty times. Cors says it back every time with the tone of someone who hasn't worked out what to do about this.

*Unexpected detail:* He is the most experienced barge pilot in Reedford by a factor of several decades and he has said nothing about the river's current behaviour to anyone except his daughter, who is Anthe Pell of the Hill Road Inn. He mentioned it to her six weeks ago, very briefly, as a point of curiosity: that the Ivora's tidal behaviour near the lower estuary had changed in the last year in a way that didn't match the seasonal pattern he'd spent fifty years memorising. Anthe wrote it down. She hasn't decided yet who to tell.

**Sienna the Mercer** *(human woman, 43, seasonal cloth trader, Hill Road)*
One of the larger seasonal traders, present from Floodtide through Harvest's Pattern, operating from a rented space in the Hill Road's commercial strip rather than a market stall. Sienna sells cloth wholesale to factors and retail to households, runs a tight operation, and has been coming to Reedford for twelve years with the specific intimacy of a seasonal regular who knows the town better than most permanent residents know it because she pays attention when she arrives rather than taking the place for granted.

She knows things about Reedford's social geography that permanent residents have stopped noticing — which factions have shifted in the last year, what the market prices say about the winter's economic health, which establishments have gotten better or worse, which NPCs are different from how they were last season.

*Characteristic habit:* Introduces people to each other. Not professionally — she just frequently finds herself talking to two people who should know each other and makes the introduction. She does this approximately twice a week across the season. She is correct about who should meet about seventy percent of the time. The other thirty percent produces interesting conversations anyway.

*Unexpected detail:* She is carrying a specific piece of commercial intelligence from Velcran that she obtained from the consortium network Syndra Velth participates in. She doesn't know it's connected to the Crimson Veil situation. She knows it's about insurance premium adjustments on inland river routes, which is commercially significant for her cloth trade, and she intends to discuss it with Syndra when she gets the chance. She has not yet had the chance.

## MISCELLANEOUS AND SPECIFIC MOMENTS

**The Man Who Plays Stones** *(human man, approximately 50, Market Square, Stillwater afternoons)*
He has a name that nobody has asked. He sits at the same spot on the market square's eastern edge every Stillwater afternoon with a flat wooden board and a collection of river stones and plays a solitary game that no one recognises. He has been doing this for three years. Several people have asked him what the game is. He explains it differently each time in ways that are consistent and make sense in the moment but that produce, afterwards, different accounts of the rules. Aldous Caer has collected four separate explanations from four separate people and found none of them contradict each other. He is considering asking again to see if a fifth explanation fills in the gaps.

*What he's actually doing:* Mapping the Thread anomaly sites from sensory memory. Each stone's position corresponds to a location on a mental map of the river and its surrounds. He moves them each week to reflect what he's perceived. He is not from Reedford. He arrived three years ago. He has not explained where from.

---

**The Cart-Mender** *(halfling woman, 58, Market Quarter, weekday mornings)*
She repairs carts. She has a specific pitch at the edge of the covered market where she works — not a stall, just a spot she occupies — and she fixes wheels, axles, and harness fittings for market traders whose equipment fails during the trading day. She charges by the severity of the problem on a three-tier scale she sets herself: small coin, fair coin, or "what can you spare." She applies "what can you spare" to people who clearly can't afford the fair rate and to people who clearly can afford a great deal more and don't seem to know it.

*Characteristic habit:* Hums while working. A specific tune — the same tune, always — that older Reed Quarter residents recognise as an old river-work song that Soth also has in their collection. She has never heard Soth play it. She learned it from her grandmother. She does not know where her grandmother learned it.

*Unexpected detail:* She is completely uninterested in anything outside her immediate professional concerns. Players who try to draw her into conversation will find she redirects to the cart or to the weather or to the quality of the current season's wheel-wood supply. This is not shyness — she simply finds carts more interesting than most conversations, and has reached an age where she doesn't feel obliged to pretend otherwise. A player who brings her a genuinely broken or unusual piece of equipment will find she becomes immediately and fully engaged.

**Renn and the Twins** *(halfling woman Renn, 32, and her twins Milo and Yeva, 4)*
Renn Ashwick — Pell Ashwick's older sister — works the covered market, sells dried herbs and river-grass goods from a seasonal stall, and brings Milo and Yeva every day because she has no one to leave them with during her working hours. The twins have effectively become the market's ambient characters — they are known by name to every permanent stall-holder, they have standing arrangements with Yida Porren for end-of-day bread, and they treat the covered market as their personal territory in the way that four-year-olds treat anywhere they've spent enough time to feel safe.

*What they contribute to the table:* They are the purest indicator of social temperature in the market. When things are normal, Milo and Yeva are everywhere, talking to everyone, getting underfoot. When something is wrong — when the grey trade has gone quiet, when Cors has been through, when the town's mood has shifted — Renn keeps them close and they stay close, and the specific absence of two four-year-olds from the usual corners of the covered market is a signal any player who's been here long enough will notice.

**The Night Watchman** *(human man, 55, Lower Quay, night shift)*
The Lower Quay runs a night watch — one person, Weaveday through Floodtide nights when barge traffic is highest. His name is Andrev. He sits in a booth at the Lower Quay entrance with a lantern and a log, records vessel movements, and wakes the crane operators if anything significant arrives unexpectedly.

He has been doing this for eleven years and has developed, over eleven years of nighttime river-watching, a comprehensive and useless knowledge of what the Lower Quay looks like at 2am and 3am and 4am in every season and weather condition. He knows the sounds of the place when it's empty better than anyone knows the sounds of it full. He noticed the Crimson Veil vessel. He logged it — time, direction, basic description — and went back to his booth. He has not connected the log entry to anything because nobody has asked him about unusual downstream arrivals.

*Characteristic habit:* Offers whoever he's talking to a cup from his thermos. He makes the tea himself, at the start of each shift, and it is extremely good — he has been practicing for eleven years and considers the quality of the night-shift tea a professional matter. He offers it to everyone. Most people don't expect a night watchman to make good tea. He finds their surprise satisfying.

*Unexpected detail:* He keeps a separate notebook — not the official log — in which he has been recording not vessel movements but river sounds. The specific acoustic quality of the Lower Quay at different hours, different weathers, different tidal states. He started this five years ago because he noticed the sounds were different on different nights in ways that didn't correlate with traffic or weather, and he wanted to understand why. He does not understand why. He has eight months of data showing that the sounds have changed significantly in the last eight months. He does not know who to tell this to. He does not know if it means anything.

## A NOTE ON USING THESE CHARACTERS

These characters don't need tracking across sessions unless players make them relevant. The GM's job is simply to have them present in the right places at the right times — Feran on the quay in the morning, Old Pemm walking the Hill Road, the Man Who Plays Stones on Stillwater afternoons, Andrev at the night-watch booth.

A few principles for using minor characters well:

**Give them one very specific detail.** Not three. One — the tally Gorth maintains, the tune the Cart-Mender hums, the way Pemm says "morning" to literally everyone. One specific detail, repeated consistently, makes a character recognisable. Three specific details makes them feel like a GM reading off a list.

**Let players make them significant.** If players become interested in Taddo or Pip or the Man Who Plays Stones, follow that interest without over-preparing. These characters exist to become whatever the players decide they are, not to fulfill a predetermined function.

**Use them to show the town noticing.** When Thread anomalies worsen, when Cors escalates, when the grey trade goes quiet — show it through minor characters first. Pella Orvish is less cheerful. Feran is spending longer at the quay. Renn keeps the twins closer. Old Vass has moved from his bollard to a spot further from the water. The minor characters are the town's emotional barometer. Let them read it.

**Remember that they talk to each other.** Taddo knows Brek Coburn. Brek knows Pip. Pip knows Maret visits the river-marker stone. The minor characters form a web of knowledge that players can tap by investing in the right relationships — but that web also generates its own dynamics that have nothing to do with the players, and occasionally surfaces information that wasn't planted as a clue but that turns out to be one.
